2. Introduction         

This lecture covers one of Plato’s dialogues, called the Gorgias. It’s a dialogue—a fancy term for a philosophical discussion—about rhetoric, or the art of speaking persuasively. You might be wondering what rhetoric has to do with crime and punishment. In fact, we already saw in last week’s lecture that if you can make a persuasive argument in a law court that could be enough to convince the jury to vote in your favour, no matter what the laws said. And it was because Lysias was trained in rhetoric that he was so skilled at creating such arguments. You might also think that rhetoric is an old-timey ancient Greek thing only, not at all applicable to the modern world. But I would counter that we are surrounded, even bombarded, by rhetoric: think about marketing campaigns, overt or implicit. Think about targeted ads on social media. Think about product placement in film and television, not to mention the whole idea of social media influencers. These are all ways in which companies try to persuade you that you need their products. Add to that the idea of “fake news”, where so-called news items are presented with an ideological slant (or are claimed to do so), and you have another example of ways that we are the targets of persuasive speech, or, as I’ve said above, the art of rhetoric. It’s been around since the ancient Greeks, and probably for centuries before.

 In our reading for this week, Plato’s Gorgias, the main character in the dialogue is someone you’ve probably heard of, the philosopher Socrates, so I’m going to start by saying a little about him. [slide #4] We know quite a lot about him. Socrates was an Athenian who lived from 469 BCE to 399 BCE. He fought bravely in the Peloponnesian War (the war between Athens and Sparta, fought from 431-404 BCE). He was married to a woman named Xanthippe and he had two sons. Even though he’s best known as a philosopher, one of the most famous incidents of his life was the trial for the generals of the battle at Arginusae and it illustrates his character nicely. To explain his role, I should say a few words about the Athenian assembly under the democracy. The assembly was an unwieldy group of thousands of men and it needed people to convene it and conduct day-to-day business. By a multi-stage process of sortition (= basically, a random draw) in the Boule, fifty men would be chosen as prytaneis, or presidents. You can think of the prytaneis as like an ‘executive committee’ of the Boule. Each group of fifty men would serve for one tenth of the year; during that term of office, every day they would randomly choose an epistates, or foreman. By pure chance, then, Socrates happened to be epistates on the day when the assembly was to vote on the issue of the generals of Arginusae. The Arginusae were some small islands between the island of Lesbos and the mainland of Asia Minor [slide #5 (map) and #6 (duplicate of #4)]. In 406 BCE (so, during the Peloponnesian War), it was the site of a naval battle between 120 Spartan triremes (warships) and 150 Athenian ones. The Athenians won the battle, but a storm prevented the generals from rescuing the survivors from crippled and sinking ships. When the generals returned to Athens, they were accused before the entire assembly of being derelict in their duty to the survivors. There was a fierce outcry: public opinion was very much against the generals, and a motion was put before the assembly to try them as a group on a capital charge (i.e., with a penalty of death). Socrates objected that this was illegal, as Athenians had a right to individual trials. The other prytaneis overruled him and the assembly voted to execute the generals. Our sources, which are generally pro-Socrates, say that the public opinion shifted later and the people regretted that they had not listened to Socrates and had gone ahead and executed the generals.

         So, this was a moment in Greek history where Socrates had a major role, and where he took a stand on principle. Some might call that just being stubborn, but it’s clear that Socrates showed courage in insisting that the laws be followed. That was not the only time Socrates defied authority in the name of justice. In 404, when the Thirty Tyrants (remember them—the ones the Spartans put in charge of Athens?) ordered him to arrest a citizen without sufficient cause, he openly refused. He was, in general, a man who defied social custom. He was famously eccentric, going barefoot all the time, stopping in the middle of the street to contemplate at random moments, and basically being really annoying in his philosophical discussions (I am reminded of the annoying character in Game of Thrones called the ‘Sparrow’ seen in the inset on the slide – Dr. Nousek). But do keep in mind that in spite of his eccentricities he was a solid Athenian citizen and an able politician. He was wealthy, had a wife and two sons, served his political duties in the assembly, and was a good soldier, if not exactly a war hero. These are qualities we don’t often give him enough credit for. He was famously and was viciously lampooned in political comedy, but he didn’t run a school or charge his students money, things that Athenians looked down upon. He was often in antiquity lumped with the ‘sophists’, who were professional teachers of rhetoric. More on them shortly.

 

3. The Importance of Rhetoric

In a culture like that of Athens, where it was generally the most powerful men who spoke persuasively in the assembly and in the law courts, the skill of rhetoric, or persuasive speaking, was the most crucial one to learn. It represented a key path to power and influence. Teachers of rhetoric, known as sophists, used to boast that they could teach people to persuade others of anything, whether the cause was right or not. They would teach you how to argue one side of a case, and then the other, and get people to believe you both times. While the sophists were very successful and made a lot of money teaching an important skill, they were also a danger to Athenian society. Remember that the Athenian assembly was made up of ordinary citizens from all walks of life. If someone could persuade the assembly of anything whether it was right or not, then how could the assembly make right or just decisions? Socrates, unlike the sophists, argued that it was the truth that made arguments persuasive, but his persuasive style was very similar to the sophistic arguments. He could get people to believe anything, even if he claimed not to be doing that.

            [slide #7] I said just now that Socrates didn’t run an official school or get paid to teach. Still, he had a number of what he called ‘friends,’ but who were, at least unofficially, his ‘students’ or ‘followers’. These ‘friends’ were partly the cause of trouble that led to legal difficulties for Socrates: these legal problems are the other main reason that Socrates is famous in the modern world: he was found guilty and forced to end his life by drinking hemlock. Here’s what happened: in 399 BCE he stood trial for “introducing new gods” and “corrupting young men.” These were religious offenses, and it may be the case that this was a trial brought by his political enemies. There’d been a political amnesty in 403 BCE as part of the restoration of the democracy, and that meant nobody could be tried specifically for their political actions under the Thirty Tyrants. But given what we know about his character, it seems likely that Socrates had made some serious enemies during that time and that these charges for religious offenses was their way of getting revenge.

            A major part of the trial was the proposal of penalties. You may remember from a few weeks ago when I was talking about Athenian trial procedures that sometimes there were set penalties and sometimes there weren’t. Generally, as part of the speeches by the prosecution and the defense, each side would propose a penalty or an amount of damages, and in addition to voting for guilt or innocence, the jury would vote on whether to implement the penalty proposed by the prosecution or the one proposed by the defense. The prosecution at Socrates’ trial proposed execution, as expected, but Socrates had the opportunity to propose a lesser penalty. Well, it seems—in retrospect!—like the best strategy might have been to propose exile, but instead he first proposes that his penalty should be that he gets to eat free meals at state expense as though he were a victor at the games, and then he relents and proposes a fine. The jury votes that he be executed, but the sentence can’t be carried out right away because there was a religious festival at the time.[1]

            Although I didn’t ask you to read the Apology, it’s worth looking at a couple of passages from it quickly. These are from Plato’s version of Socrates’ Apology, which was not a transcript of the speech Socrates gave in his own defense at trial, but is likely to be pretty consistent with what Socrates said. The two passages I’ve chosen are ones that refer to Socrates’ civic engagement and citizen activity; both offer direct evidence for Socrates’ character and ethical stance. The first is about the time when he presided over the assembly after the battle at Arginusae: [slide #8]

 

The only office which I have ever held in our city, gentlemen, was when I served on the Council. It so happened that our tribe Antiochis was presiding when you decided that the ten commanders who had failed to rescue the men who were lost in the naval engagement should be tried en bloc which was illegal, as you all recognized later. On this occasion I was the only member of the executive who opposed your acting in any way unconstitutionally, and voted against the proposal; and although the public speakers were all ready to denounce and arrest me, and you were all urging them on at the top of your voices, I thought that it was my duty to face it out on the side of law and justice rather than support you, through fear of prison or death, in your wrong decision. (Apology 32b-c)

 

This suggests that Socrates was not always the eccentric weirdo that we know him as, but was a solid, capable citizen. Nevertheless, even in fulfilling his civic duty he was inclined to resist the crowd. In another passage, he talks about resisting not the crowd, but the unjust power of the Thirty Tyrants: [slide #9]

 

This happened while we were still under a democracy. When the oligarchy came into power, the Thirty Commissioners in their turn summoned me and four others to the Round Chamber and instructed us to go and fetch Leon of Salamis from his home for execution. This was of course only one of many instances in which they issued such instructions their object being to implicate as many people as possible in their crimes. On this occasion, however, I again made it clear, not by my words but by my actions, that the attention I paid to death was zero (if that is not too unrefined a claim); but that I gave all my attention to avoiding doing anything unjust or unholy. Powerful as it was, that government did not terrify me into doing a wrong action. (Apology 32c-d)

 

This passage is especially interesting because it brings up an important issue. Socrates is concerned with justice, and for him this is not purely a legal matter. What he does is illegal, because after all the Thirty Tyrants are the government and they make the laws. But Socrates argues that it’s wrong to obey an unjust order. We’ll see in the Gorgias that he characterizes this kind of injustice as criminality, taking a moral view rather than a legal view of crime. In this view, even governments and tyrants who make the laws can be criminals. In fact, Socrates will argue that they are worse criminals than people who break the law.

 

4. The Five Things    

Before we get too far into the philosophy, though, I should step back and introduce the Gorgias. Here are the five things. [slide #10 (click a couple times for whimsy)] The author [slide #11] is Plato. Plato was an Athenian who lived from about 429 BCE to 347 BCE. He was one of the followers of Socrates, and although Socrates neither ran a school nor wrote anything down, Plato started to write Socratic dialogues—which is to say scenes in which people hang around with Socrates talking about philosophy—after the death of Socrates, and he eventually founded a school called “The Academy”: there was a gym in Athens sacred to a hero named Academus, and Plato chose that gym as the spot for his school. That’s why we call school “academic.” Anyway, it’s not clear exactly how historical the dialogues are, but they differ significantly from other sources. Many of the events actually took place and the characters were real (e.g., Lysias and his father Cephalus), but the discussions themselves were creations of Plato.

[slide #12: title] We are reading the Gorgias. Note that the definite article is not capitalized or italicized. Platonic dialogues are often named after one of the main characters (apart from Socrates who is a main character in most of them). Gorgias was a sophist from Leontini in Sicily who lived from about 485 to about 380 BCE. He visited Athens as an ambassador in 427, and this was an important event, not least for introducing his Sicilian style to Athenian rhetoric. The occasion for the dialogue is probably not that exact visit, because in the text Socrates refers to the time when he was epistates, which was in 406 BCE. So this must be a visit near the end of the Peloponnesian War.

            [slide #13: date] Plato lived from about 429-347 BCE, and he started writing after 399, so we know that the Gorgias belongs between those dates. Beyond that, it seems to be an early to middle dialogue, but that’s just a guess. The way that scholars typically date Plato’s dialogues is by style. The ones we think are early are the ones where Socrates does most of the talking and he leads his interlocutors around. The ones we think are in the middle are the ones where Socrates is still the main character, but he’s not always right and other people make persuasive and extensive arguments. The ones we think are late are the ones where Socrates appears very little or not at all. So the Gorgias seems to be an early dialogue but with some characteristics of the middle dialogues, so it’s probably closer to 399 than it is to 347.

[slide #14: location] Plato lived in Athens. In fact, he came from a very prominent family. His stepfather was a democrat and friend of Pericles, but two of his uncles were members of the Thirty Tyrants. Like Aeschylus, he spent some time in Sicily, but he lived primarily at Athens and wrote most of his dialogues there. [slide #15: language] As an Athenian, he wrote in Attic Greek. [slide #16: The Five Things together]

 

5. Socrates and Gorgias

With that (rather lengthy, I know!) introduction, I’d like now to follow my usual practice and look at some passages from the text. I’ll identify the passages by their so-called Stephanus pages, which are the pages in an important early printed edition of Plato’s collected works. They’re the numbers in the margins that are followed by lowercase letters. I use them because they’re consistent, so if you happen to have a different edition or translation than I the one ordered for the course (though I don’t recommend that!) you should still be able to find the passages I’m citing. The first passage is from near the beginning and it helps to set the scene. It’s 447d-448a: [slide #17]

 

CHAEREPHON: Yes. All right. I’ll ask him. Can you tell me, please, Gorgias, whether Callicles here is telling the truth when he says that you claim to be able to answer any question that’s put to you?

 

GORGIAS: He is, Chaerephon. That’s exactly what I was doing a short while ago, in fact, and I’ll add that for many years now I’ve never been faced with a question I hadn’t met before. (Gorgias 447d-448a, page 4)

 

So Gorgias is a guest in Athens, and because he’s a sophist he’s famous and supposed to be super smart, so people come up to him and try to stump him with questions. Socrates is a famous questioner, in fact he teaches by asking questions, which we now call “the Socratic method.” So since Gorgias is a rhetorician, which means his specialty is rhetoric, or public speaking, Socrates starts by asking him what rhetoric is. [slide #18] Gorgias responds in 454b:

 

GORGIAS: Well, Socrates, I think—to repeat what I was saying not long ago—that its effect is to persuade people in the kinds of mass meetings which happen in law-courts and so on; and I think its province is right and wrong. (Gorgias 454b, page 15)

 

There are some people who are puzzled when I say that I’ve assigned the Gorgias in this class rather than another dialogue like the Laws or the Crito, or even the Apology, but I hope you can see from this early passage why this is going to be interesting for thinking about crime and punishment in ancient Greece. Gorgias says that rhetoric is the kind of persuasion that happens in mass meetings, including especially the law-courts. So when Socrates and Gorgias, and later Socrates and the students of Gorgias are talking about rhetoric, they’re also talking about law: and of course, lawyers, whose presentation of the case must be persuasive (just like what we saw last week in Lysias and the speech On the Murder of Eratosthenes).

            Socrates makes the point that while Gorgias says the province of rhetoric is right and wrong, there’s a difference between (a) teaching people what’s right and what’s wrong, and (b) persuading people that your opinion of right and wrong is the correct one. [slide #19] This is 455a:

 

SOCRATES: A rhetorician, then, isn’t concerned to educate the people assembled in law-courts and so on about right and wrong; all he wants to do is persuade them. I mean, I shouldn’t think it’s possible for him to get so many people to understand such important matters in such a short time.

 

GORGIAS: No, that’s right. (Gorgias 455a, page 17)

 

There’s a difference between teaching and persuasion, and this plays into the Socratic method of teaching. The courtroom isn’t really a place for teaching people about the law and about justice, because the speakers are interested and biased. A courtroom speaker isn’t out to get justice really, but out to try to win the case. This means that court cases don’t really determine ‘right and wrong’ or ‘just and unjust’ at all. They really determine who was more persuasive. If there is a hardcore reality of who is criminal and who is innocent, that’s not really what court cases figure out, according to Socrates. And this is a really significant opinion for someone in Classical Athens. Here in the 4th century BCE, the Athenian justice system that uses juries isn’t that old, and it’s not universal among Greek poleis. It’s actually pretty subversive to say that it doesn’t determine right and wrong. But ultimately, as we see from other dialogues, Socrates really does think the jury system is adequate, it’s just that he views it as a contract. By living in Athens, he says, people have agreed to a contract that they will abide by the decisions of juries. That doesn’t mean they’ll be right all the time, but if you want to live in Athens that’s the risk you take. It’s why he dies rather than escape prison. He abides by the verdict of the jury even though he thinks it’s unjust, because it would be a greater injustice to fail to recognize the authority of the court. And the Athenians did know that there were deep flaws in the jury system and that it didn’t always produce the right outcome. They just kept using it because it was the best they could think of. They knew that it wasn’t a system that was after the deep truth of justice, and they had other ways of thinking about jurisprudence, like through tragedy (remember the Oresteia) or philosophy.

 

6. Socrates and Polus

So Socrates has just clarified with Gorgias that rhetoric is about persuasion rather than teaching. They continue back and forth in this vein for a little while (Note: the philosophical discussion is interesting in its own right, but we have a lot of crime and justice material to cover so I’ll focus on that –Dr. Nousek). But then Gorgias’ student Polus steps in to take over the conversation. Polus thinks that rhetoric is awesome because if you can persuade people of your opinion, that gives you a lot of power (MWAHAHAH!). [slide #20] Socrates points out a problem with this in 469d:

 

SOCRATES: Well, Polus, here are some thoughts of mine for you to criticize. Imagine I’m in the agora when it’s chock-full, and I’ve got a dagger tucked in my armpit. I tell you, ‘Polus, I’ve recently gained an incredible amount of power, as much as any dictator. Look at all these people. If I decide one of them has to die, he’s dead, just like that…

Can you tell us what’s wrong with that sort of power, to your mind?

POLUS: Yes.

SOCRATES: What is it? Do please tell us.

POLUS: It’s that anyone who does the kinds of things you were describing is bound to be punished. (Gorgias 469d-e, page 41)

 

So now not only is this a dialogue about the law-courts and about right and wrong, but it’s also a dialogue about punishment. Polus thinks that the reason people don’t go around stabbing each other is because they’re afraid that if they get caught they’ll be punished. This is a lot like Rational Choice Theory, and more specifically it’s about deterrence. Socrates, however, thinks differently. He thinks that stabbing people is wrong, and that even if there’s no risk of punishment, it’s still a bad idea to stab somebody. For Polus, the law and the courts are the things that make something right or wrong. For Socrates, there is a morality that exists beyond the law or the possibility of punishment. You shouldn’t not stab someone because you’ll be punished if you get caught, instead you should not stab someone because it’s morally wrong.

            In fact, for Socrates, it’s bad for you to hurt other people: that makes you immoral and you can’t be happy if you are an immoral person. So, as the dialogue proceeds, they start working on an extreme test case. They take the example of Archelaus, the tyrant of Macedon. Since he’s a tyrant,[2] he’s completely in charge, so he makes the laws. What he does can’t be against the law, but Socrates still argues that if his actions are unjust, he can’t be happy. It’s a question of whether something is illegal or whether it is unjust. [slide #21] This is 471a:

 

POLUS: My man Archelaus is unhappy, then, according to you.

SOCRATES: Yes, if he does wrong, Polus.

POLUS: But of course he does. He didn’t have the slightest claim to the throne he currently occupies. His mother was a slave of Perdiccas’ brother Alcetas, so by rights he should have been Alcetas’ slave too. If he’d wanted to behave morally, he’d have been Alcetas’ slave and that would have made him happy, according to you. As it is, though, he’s become incredibly unhappy as a result of the awful crimes he’s committed! [slide #22] In the first place, he sent a message to Alcetas, who was his uncle as well as his master, in which he invited him to stay on the grounds that he would restore the kingdom which Perdiccas had stolen from him. So he welcomed Alcetas and his son Alexander, his own cousin (who was more or less the same age as him), into his house; then he got them drunk, bundled them into a cart, took them away under cover of darkness, murdered them both, and disposed of the bodies. (Gorgias 471a-b, page 43)

 

Okay, let me fill in some of the details: the story is of how Archelaus came to power. Archelaus was born as a slave, but he killed his uncle and his cousin and took over the throne. Polus argues (sarcastically!) that this is what was clearly wrong, because after all he was born a slave so he should remain a slave. Polus of course doesn’t question that the institution of slavery itself might be wrong, but thinks that everyone has a proper place in society and should accept that place, unless, of course, they are smart and powerful enough to get away with improving their circumstances. If a person can get away with a power grab, according to Polus, then why wouldn’t they? He’s not seeing Socrates’ point that it’s assassination that’s the wrong being committed. [slide #23] Socrates boils it down a little in 472d:

 

SOCRATES: Let’s start with the question facing us, which is the crux of our present discussion. You think it’s possible for someone to be happy in spite of the fact that he does wrong and is an immoral person, and you cite the case of Archelaus who is, in your opinion, an immoral, but happy, person. Is that a fair representation of your view?

 

POLUS: Yes.

 

SOCRATES: On the other hand, I claim that this is impossible. So here’s one point on which we disagree. Now then, this happy criminal ... will he be happy if he pays the penalty for his actions and is punished?

 

POLUS: Definitely not. That would make his condition very unhappy. (Gorgias 472d-e, page 46)

 

Polus’ position seems pretty intuitive. If you’re a slave and you kill your master and take over his kingdom, you’re happier than you otherwise would be. And if you get punished, that makes you unhappy. But Socrates doesn’t let up: [slide #24]

 

SOCRATES: So is it your view that a criminal is happy as long as he doesn’t get punished?

POLUS: Yes.

SOCRATES: My view, however, Polus, is that although an unjust person, a criminal, is in a thoroughly wretched state, he’s worse off if he doesn’t pay the penalty and continues to do wrong without getting punished than if he does pay the penalty and has punishment meted out to him by gods and men. (Gorgias 472d-e, page 46)

 

The first thing to note about this is that Socrates isn’t talking only about human punishment, but also about divine punishment. Socrates is taking not just a moral approach to crime here, but also a religious one. He thinks that there are some things that are truly wrong, and that even if you are a tyrant like Archelaus, or even if you are the Thirty Tyrants, you are not really above the law. You may be able to make the laws that say you’re not criminal, but there is a higher law that you can’t just erase. And this, I think, is a significant part of the disagreement between Polus and Socrates. The key is in how you think about crime and punishment, and it’s here we can see Walklate’s approaches in action again. If we take a legal approach to crime and say that a criminal is a person who engages in law-breaking behavior, then it’s very possible for a person to be a criminal and nevertheless live a good life as long as they don’t get caught. Suppose, for example, you were living in a slave society, and you were enslaved. You might be able to be much happier and live a better life by breaking laws than by obeying them. But if we take a moral approach to crime, then a criminal is any person who engages in unjust behavior. Then it’s a lot easier to make the argument that someone who does wrong will be plagued by guilt and will not actually live a better life. And if you add a religious dimension, as Socrates does, then a criminal is someone who engages in irreligious behavior and it’s even more unlikely that such a person can live a good life. So what Socrates and Polus are really disagreeing about here is the whole concept of criminality.

            So much for the first point of disagreement, but there’s more. Socrates moves on in 476a: [slide #25]

SOCRATES: So much for that issue. Next we need to look into the second point of disagreement between us. You claim that nothing could be worse for a criminal than paying the penalty for his crimes, whereas I claim that he’s worse off if he doesn’t pay the penalty. Which of us is right? Here’s a way into the question: would you agree that there’s no difference between a criminal paying the penalty for his crimes and being justly punished for them? (Gorgias 476a, page 52)

 

Here Socrates is making a distinction only to deny that it exists. The distinction is between paying the penalty for crimes, on the one hand, and being justly punished for them, on the other. By denying the distinction, Socrates is suggesting that all punishment and all penalties for crime are just. He’s assuming a universal, moral, and religious definition of crime, which allows him to say that there is no such thing as unjust punishment. Or if there is, then that would be a crime itself. By getting Polus to agree to this, he really puts him in a bind. And he uses his advantage to good effect.

            In the next passage, we start to see more of the logic behind Socrates’ view of crime and punishment. Polus thinks that punishment is a bad thing for criminals, buying into the idea of deterrence. Socrates, on the other hand, thinks that punishment actually helps people. And this isn’t as crazy as Polus initially thinks: after all, we think that prisons are supposed to reform people. They’re called ‘correctional’ institutions after all. Our modern punishments are not only deterrents, but also rehabilitation, and at least in an ideal world, they help criminals become better citizens. Socrates takes up this line of thinking and says that punishing criminals is like providing medicine for sick people. [slide #26] Here’s 478e:

 

SOCRATES: And isn’t this precisely the state of an arch-criminal, with his utter immorality, who successfully avoids being criticized and disciplined and punished—in other words, exactly what, according to you, Archelaus and his fellow dictators, rhetoricians, and political leaders have managed to do?

POLUS: I suppose so.

SOCRATES: Their achievement, then, Polus, is not so very different from that of someone in the grip of an extremely severe illness who successfully avoids having the doctors exact the penalty for his body’s crimes—that is, who avoids medical treatment—because he’s childishly frightened of the pain of cautery and surgery. Don’t you agree?

POLUS: Yes, I do. (Gorgias 478e-479b, page 58)

 

And of course, because this is an early or middle Platonic dialogue, the poor interlocutor (we call the speakers in dialogues “interlocutors” – Dr. Nousek) just agrees with what Socrates says. He continues: [slide #27]

 

SOCRATES: And he’s afraid, I suppose, because he doesn’t understand health and doesn’t know what a good physical state is like. I mean, the position we’ve reached in our discussion makes it seem likely that this is what people who evade punishment are up to as well, Polus, they can see that punishment is painful, but they have a blind spot about how beneficial it is, and they fail to appreciate that life with an unhealthy mind—a mind which is unsound, immoral, and unjust—is infinitely more wretched than life with an unhealthy body. This also explains why they go to such lengths to avoid being punished—that is, to avoid being saved from the worst kind of badness. Instead they equip themselves with money and friends, and make sure that they’re as persuasive as they can be at speaking. (Gorgias 479b-c, page 59)

 

Punishment, says Socrates, is good for the soul. Rhetoricians like Gorgias and Polus, who can persuade juries to acquit them, are only doing harm to themselves by avoiding punishment, like sick people avoiding treatment. If they really understood right and wrong, they would welcome punishment as healing. So while Polus initially saw punishment as a reason not to do crime, or a risk of doing it, Socrates has got him convinced now that the real purpose of punishment is rehabilitation. And this debate is still going on now. How much are prisons supposed to be scary places where people don’t want to go, and how much are they places where criminals are made into productive members of society?

 

7. Socrates and Callicles

At this point Polus is done for, so one of Gorgias’ other students decides to take over. This guy, Callicles, thinks he can do a better job than Polus. He starts off in 482e: [slide #28]

[CALLICLES:] You pretend that truth is your goal, Socrates, but in actual fact you steer discussions towards this kind of ethical idea—ideas which are unsophisticated enough to have popular appeal, and which depend entirely on convention, not on nature. They’re invariably opposed to each other, you know—nature and convention, I mean—and consequently if someone is too embarrassed to go right ahead and voice his convictions, he’s bound to contradict himself. (Gorgias 482e-483a, page 65)

 

We’re used to the idea that Socrates is this iconoclast who teaches radical new ideas, but Callicles says that Socrates is just spouting unsophisticated stuff that common people believe. He says that Socrates just goes along with convention rather than following nature. The Greek words that Callicles uses here are physis for “nature,” that’s where we get the word physics from, and for “convention” he uses nomos. For the sophists, the opposition between nature and culture, or physis and nomos is very important. Remember that nomos means both “custom” and “law.” I’m going to argue that although Callicles says he understands nature, what he’s really going to say is that nomos is a social construction, and that because of that it has no real value. He says that he is brave enough to stand up to the crowd and to say the brutal truth of how the world really works. [slide #29] Here’s 483b:

 

 

 

[CALLICLES:] In my opinion it’s the weaklings who constitute the majority of the human race who make the rules. In making these rules, they look after themselves and their own interest, and that’s also the criterion they use when they dispense praise and criticism. They try to cow the stronger ones—which is to say, the ones who are capable of increasing their share of things—and to stop them getting an increased share. (Gorgias 483b-c, page 65)

 

Callicles says that there are strong people and there are weak people, and that there is stuff in the world that people share. Strong people have the ability to take more of a share than weak people do. But there are more weak people in the world than there are strong people, and so the weak people band together and make up rules that bind the strong people. They say that it’s wrong to take more than your share of stuff. But, Callicles continues, morality is just an invention of weak people who are afraid that strong people will take their stuff. While Socrates takes a moral approach to crime, Callicles says that morality isn’t real or true, but a social construction, or nomos. The only reason it seems valid is because the masses of weak people are in charge, and their social construction keeps the strong few from taking over. [slides #30, 31] He continues in 491e:

 [CALLICLES:] What nature approves and sanctions, on the other hand—I’m going to speak bluntly to you now—is this: the only authentic way of life is to do nothing to hinder or restrain the expansion of one’s desires, until they can grow no larger, at which point one should be capable of putting courage and cleverness at their service and satisfying every passing whim. [slide #31] Now, I don’t think most people can do this, and that’s why they condemn those who can; they’re ashamed, and they try to disguise their failings by claiming that self-indulgence is contemptible, which, as I explained earlier, is an attempt to enslave those who are naturally better than them. And why do they praise self-discipline and justice? Because their own timidity makes them incapable of winning satisfaction for their pleasures. (Gorgias 491e-492b, page 79)

 

Callicles thinks that people praise self-discipline and justice not because those things are good or real, but because they don’t have the courage or the strength to take what they want and to say that nature has no concept of justice. For Callicles, the strong take from the weak and if you don’t like it then you should get stronger.

            Socrates, on the other hand, thinks that justice is real, and that self-discipline is worthwhile. [slides #32, 33] He explains why in 507c:

[SOCRATES:] That’s my position, and I believe it to be true. If it really is true, it looks as though anyone who wants to be happy must seek out and practise self-discipline, and beat as hasty a retreat as possible away from self-indulgence. The best course would be for him to see to it that he never has to be restrained, but if he or anyone close to him (whether that’s an individual person or the whole community) does ever need it, then he must let justice and restraint be imposed, or else forfeit happiness. [slide #33] I’ll tell you what the ideal is that we should set ourselves to live by, in my opinion. We should devote all our own and our community’s energies towards ensuring the presence of justice and self-discipline, and so guarantee happiness. We shouldn’t refuse to restrain our desires, because that condemns us to a life of endlessly trying to satisfy them. And this is the life of a predatory outlaw, in the sense that anyone who lives like that will never be on good terms with anyone else. (Gorgias 507c-e, page 105)

 

There are two important reasons in this excerpt as to why self-discipline is a good idea. The first is that desire can never really be satisfied. It’s always possible to want more. In fact, that’s how desire works. It’s an episodic phenomenon. As soon as you get what you want, you want something else. You always want what you don’t have. Even if you could immediately get whatever it is you want, you would find a way to want something else. It’s just not in human nature to be fully satisfied forever. Sure, there are extremes, and it’s possible to be very unhappy if you have very little in life, but even people who have everything they could want are not perfectly happy. We always want something more. So Socrates says the answer to desire is not always satisfying your desire, but restraining it: we should learn to be happy with what we have wherever that’s possible.

            The other point he makes is that someone who is always satisfying desire and not worrying about just taking what he or she wants is that they live as outlaws and are never on good terms with other people. In fact, friends are important. People are social creatures and we are—generally speaking—happier when we are with other people than when we are alone. This isn’t always true and people do enjoy solitude, but always to be fighting with other people and never trusting others or being trusted is not a good life, says Socrates. By restraining our own desires and taking into account the desires and needs of other people, in other words, by acting justly, we make ourselves happier. So even though Callicles’ point makes sense, that justice is a social construction—even if we buy into that, it still doesn’t mean that it has no value or no ability to make us happy. Social convention or construction is what allows us to get along with other people and to trust them. Even if law is only a kind of contract, it still may be worth keeping it even when we don’t have to.

            But Socrates isn’t done yet. If he’s right, and justice is good, then it’s bad for people to be criminals. As a logical extension of that idea, Socrates expresses a very modern idea in 508d-e: [slide #34]

[SOCRATES:] I deny that being wrongly smashed in the face or stabbed or robbed by a cutpurse is the most contemptible thing that can happen to a person, Callicles; I claim that it’s more contemptible, and worse as well, to hit and cut me and my property without just cause, and I also claim that to steal, enslave, burgle, and in short to do any kind of wrong against me and my property, is not only worse for the wrongdoer than it is for me, the target of his wrongdoing, but is also more contemptible. (Gorgias 508d-e, page 107)

 

According to Socrates, it’s better to be a victim of crime than a perpetrator of crime. Hurting people does more harm than being hurt does. This is why punishment is good for people. It washes way the crime and allows people to live together in harmony again. If you hurt other people, even if you get away with it, then you condemn yourself to the life of a predatory outlaw and you will not be on good terms with people. If you believe, as Callicles does, that anyone at any time would be right to take what they can from other people instead of helping other people, then even if you are extremely powerful, you won’t really have friends. And that’s a bad outcome.

            This means that (according to Socrates in this text) one of the important goals of justice is making a community. We’ve seen before, in the Oresteia, that dealing with crime has a lot to do with the cohesion of a particular community. Violence within a community is criminal, while violence against outsiders is heroic. And note here that Socrates is not condemning all violence. He himself was a soldier. But the criminal alienates themself from their own community, and that’s what makes criminal violence more harmful for the perpetrator than it is for the victim. It’s an interesting view of crime and punishment, and one that resonates with a lot of us in the modern world.

            So having laid out his moral view of crime and punishment, Socrates takes time to return to the religious approach. [slides #35, 36] In 523a-b, he talks about divine punishment again:

[SOCRATES:] Now, during Cronus’ reign human beings were subject to a law which the gods sanction even to this day and which is as follows: any human being who has lived a moral and god-fearing life shall on his death depart for the Isles of the Blessed and shall dwell there, and live a trouble-free life of perfect happiness; however, anyone who has lived an immoral and godless life shall be imprisoned in the place of retribution and justice, which is called Tartarus. [slide #36] In the time of Cronus, and in the relatively recent past during Zeus’ reign as well, living judges dealt with living people and passed judgement upon them on the day of their impending death, which made the administration of justice poor. So Pluto and the supervisors of the Isles of the Blessed came and told Zeus that the wrong kinds of people were getting to both places. (Gorgias 523a-b, page 129)

 

He goes on saying that now the divine judges are better and the right people go to the good place and the right people go to the bad place. What I find most interesting about this passage is that the mechanism of divine justice that governs the afterlife looks a lot like the ancient Greek law-courts. Sure, there aren’t really juries, but early judges were kings or magistrates. Human justice is flawed—after all it’s about rhetoric and persuasion rather than about teaching what’s really right and wrong—but the divine mechanism that works well now is a mirror of that imperfect human system. We might have expected the gods to be more perfect, but it wasn’t like that in Greek theology. In any case, Socrates has just spent a bunch of time teaching Gorgias and Polus and Callicles that justice is good and that crime is bad even for criminals who get away without punishment, but now he says that those guys won’t really get away without punishment anyway. Criminals who aren’t punished in life will be punished in the afterlife. This is a huge difference between Socrates and Callicles: Callicles takes a social constructionist approach and says that justice was made up by weak people. The masses of weak people are in charge and keep the strong people in check by inventing morality. For Socrates, not only are justice and morality logically good for people, but they are also divine. So in a way, Socrates believes Polus that punishment is a deterrent, it’s just that for him people who aren’t afraid of punishment in life should still be afraid of punishment in the afterlife. [slides #37, 38] Here’s 525b-c:

[SOCRATES:] What is appropriate? As long as the person inflicting the punishment is justified in doing so, then every instance of punishment should either help its recipient by making him a better person or should act as an example for others, in the sense that the terrifying sight of the victim’s sufferings helps to improve. Those who are benefited by being punished (whether the agents of punishment are divine or human) are those whose faults are curable; nevertheless, it remains the case both here and in Hades that it takes pain and torment to produce the benefit, since that is the only way in which injustice can be removed. [slide #38] Those who act as examples, on the other hand, are those who have committed such awful crimes that they’ve become incurable. Although this means that they themselves are past help, others can be helped by watching them suffer for ever the worst, most agonizing, and most terrifying torments imaginable as a result of their sins. Their only purpose is to hang there in their prison in Hades as visible deterrents for every new criminal who arrives there. (Gorgias 525b-c, page 132)

 

There are some people, says Socrates, who benefit from punishment. But—and this is different from what he was saying before about criminals being like sick people—there are people who are incorrigible. These people do not benefit from punishment because they can’t be cured. Still, he argues, their punishment is just because it acts as an example or deterrent to keep other people from committing really terrible crimes. This seems like a weak point to me, but Socrates is admitting here that punishment isn’t always directly good for criminals. Instead guys like Archelaus (= the tyrant of Macedon, from Polus’ test case) will be punished in the afterlife as examples. [slides #39, 40] This is 526d-e:

[SOCRATES:] If what Polus says is true, then in my opinion Archelaus will become one of these deterrents, and he’ll be joined by anyone else who’s a dictator like him. In fact, I think most of those who act as examples are drawn from the ranks of dictators, kings, potentates, and politicians, because they’re the ones who can and do commit the most terrible and immoral crimes. Homer testifies to this, since in his poems those who are condemned to be punished for ever in Hades are kings and potentates such as Tantalus, Sisyphus, and Tityus. [slide #40] However, no poet portrays Thersites (or anyone else who may have been bad, but who wasn’t involved in public life) as an incurable criminal in the grip of terrible punishment, and I imagine it’s because he didn’t have the same scope for wrongdoing that he’s better off than those who did. No, Callicles, it’s power that leads men to plumb the depths of depravity. (Gorgias 526d-e, page 132)

 

What’s most interesting about this passage for me is the focus on powerful people. I’ve argued before in this class that one of the important differences between ancient Greek views of crime and modern views of crime is that in the modern world we imagine the criminal to be someone of low socioeconomic status who is marginalized, while in ancient Greece the focus of criminological thinking is powerful men. Thersites, whom Socrates mentions here as someone who is not an incurable criminal, is a character who appears in book 2 of Homer’s Iliad. He’s the ugliest and most cowardly soldier in the Greek army. One day Agamemnon gets the idea that he’ll use some ‘reverse psychology’ and tell the troops it’s time to go home and give up on sacking Troy because it’s too hard. He figures that everyone will object and shout that they want to stay and fight and that will energize the troops and remind them that they want to be there. But it totally backfires and everyone says, “Awesome, let’s go home!” So Odysseus starts going around and trying to convince people to change their minds and keep fighting, and the most vocal of the “let’s go home” crowd is Thersites. He gives a speech talking about how war sucks and all it does is make the rich richer and the common people just die, and Odysseus takes up a big stick and beats him with it really badly. Everyone laughs at Thersites and agrees to stay and fight. So Thersites is the representative of the common soldier against the aristocrats, and Socrates says that whatever his crimes (cowardice, presumably), they’re not nearly as bad as the crimes of kings. Because kings have more power, they have more ability to do bad things and to really hurt people. Power, says Socrates, leads men to the depths of depravity. The idea that the criminals on whom we should focus are the powerful is the inverse of modern criminology’s focus on the poor and the marginalized.

            Having made that point, Socrates comes to the conclusion of his argument to Callicles, and it’s one that isn’t very satisfying in my opinion. I think Socrates does a fine job of making logical arguments for morality and justice, but then in the end he thinks he has to convince Callicles to behave justly by using deterrence. It’s not, in the end, that justice is good that Socrates uses to persuade Callicles, but that if he escapes justice in the real world, he won’t in the afterlife. Socrates has to invent a perfect version of the ancient Greek court system to make justice seem real to Callicles. [slides #41, 42] This is 526e-527a: (last big passage! –Dr. N.)

[SOCRATES:] Well, Callicles, I myself find this account persuasive, and I intend to present the judge with as healthy a soul as possible. So I’ll ignore the public honours which attract most people, follow the path of truth, and try to be as moral a person as I can during my lifetime and after my death as well. I do all I can to recommend this course of action to others too. In particular, in response to your appeal to me, I appeal to you to take up this way of life, to engage in this struggle which, in my opinion, is as worthwhile a struggle as you’ll find in this world. [slide #42] I think it’s a flaw in you that you won’t be able to defend yourself when the time comes for you to undergo the trial and the assessment which I’ve just been talking about. Instead, when you come to be judged by that son of Aegina and he seizes you and takes you away, your head will spin and your mouth will gape there in that world just as much as mine would here, and the chances are that someone will smash you in the face and generally abuse you as if you were a nobody without any status at all. (Gorgias 526e-527a, pages 133-134)

 

Socrates’ threat for Callicles is an afterlife of no power and no status. People who have social status in ancient Greece have rights. If you touch an Athenian citizen without his consent, that’s assault. People with no status, like enslaved people, have no rights. You can beat up your own slave all you want, and even if you beat up someone else’s slave it doesn’t count as harmful to the slave, but rather to the slave’s owner. So Socrates tells Callicles that although he has power and status now in life, if he abuses that power he will be stripped of it in the afterlife, and instead of going to the good place he’ll go to the bad place. Socrates had acknowledged that the human justice system isn’t perfect, but now he says that the divine one is like a perfect version of the human one. And that’s what’s unsatisfying to me as an argument. Rather than sticking to the view that saying just and moral behavior is valuable in its own right, his religious view says that it’s valuable because you can’t escape the punishment of the gods. So for me it’s a disappointing ending to the dialogue, but perhaps it’s expedient and the only way that Socrates thinks he can convince Callicles and Polus to do the right thing. In that way, he’s disingenuously persuading them to be good rather than really teaching them what right and wrong are. He’s using their own skill, rhetoric, against them. And maybe, just maybe, that’s part of Plato’s ultimate goal here.

 

8. Conclusions

Ultimately, this is a dialogue that presents a variety of approaches to crime. Polus uses Rational Choice Theory and deterrence to think about crime, while Socrates takes a moral and religious view. Callicles is more of a social constructionist. In the modern world we also take a variety of approaches to thinking about crime, and there’s not one dominant approach. It’s tempting to think that one of the views that Socrates or Polus or Callicles express is typical of ancient Athenian thought and that the others are radical proposals or revolutionary in some way, but what’s more likely is that all of the views represented in the Gorgias were more or less popular in Athens in the Classical Period. Just like modern criminologists bring together different ideas, the Gorgias is a kind of ancient criminology that works with many ideas and approaches to crime and punishment.
