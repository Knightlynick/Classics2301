2. Introduction

[slide #3] This lecture is an introduction to Greek law, and I want to advise you right from the outset that the lecture is perhaps the most detailed, with a large number of terms and definitions that you will need to learn. As usual, the most important terms are highlighted in blue text on the slides, and you’ll also find a glossary of Greek law terms on OWL (see info on the main page for this week). Now then, while our focus in this course will be on Athenian law in the fourth and fifth centuries BCE, Athenian laws and ideas of crime developed from a long history in mainland Greece and beyond, so we’ll start in an earlier time and work our way forward to the Classical Period. It’s also important to note that Athens was not the only classical Greek polis, and our evidence shows that some aspects of Greek law were common to all the poleis and some were unique. The reason we’re going to focus on Athens this semester is because almost all of our evidence comes from Athens. We have some inscriptions and some writings on law from other poleis, but overwhelmingly our records of court cases and thinking about crime come from Classical Athens. In a longer course it would be interesting to spend time in particular on the contrast between, for example, Athenian and Spartan law, but for us the best thing will be to set Athenian law within the history of the development of Greek law before focusing on the period for which we have the most evidence.

3. Earliest Greek Law          

[slides #4, 5] The oldest Greek literature we have is Homer’s Iliad, written down in the 8th century BCE but developing in the oral tradition even before that. This epic poem purports to look back to the last glory days of Mycenaean civilization, but scholars suspect that many of the historical institutions are more like late Dark Age institutions than Mycenaean ones. There are a number of disputes (the sort of thing that usually prompts acts of crime and punishment!) that happen in the Iliad, and they are often settled by simple violence. It does happen, however, that some people in a dispute take their dispute to the man in charge and ask for a just resolution. We see in the Iliad a mix of self-help, in which someone who believes he was wronged simply takes vengeance, and arbitration, where the disputants agree to have a third party determine the outcome of the case. One of the most important scenes in the poem for us is a description of the shield of Achilles in book 18. Although the Iliad is a poem about a few weeks in the tenth year of the Trojan War, many passages describe daily life outside the war, and the shield is decorated with images of peaceful life. [slide #6] In one scene on the shield, there is a description of an early kind of court case:

The people were assembled in the marketplace, where a quarrel had arisen, and two men were disputing over the blood price for a man who had been killed. One man promised full restitution in a public statement, but the other refused and would accept nothing. Both made for an arbitrator, to have a decision, and the people were speaking up on either side, to help both men. But the heralds kept the people in hand, as meanwhile the elders were in session on benches of polished stone in the sacred circle and held in their voices. The two men rushed before these, and took turns speaking their cases, and between them lay on the ground two talents of gold, to be given to that judge who in this case spoke the straightest opinion. (Iliad 18.497-508, cited on VerSteeg page 5)

 

A passage like this is a good paradigm for how we can use literary texts to gain knowledge about ideas, views, and beliefs relevant to crime and punishment in classical antiquity. This is the earliest description of Greek law we have, and it tells us a lot. First, the crime here was murder. A man was killed, but we don’t know how or whether it was intentional or not. The man who was responsible for the death has claimed that he can pay money as compensation to the family of the dead man, but the dead man’s family is refusing. Presumably the family’s representative is either demanding that the killer be exiled or they are planning to take vengeance. And so, the two sides are at an impasse: thus the arbitration process. It is not the fact of the killing that is in dispute here, but the nature of the punishment. Later on when we look at early Athenian homicide law, we’ll see that what it addresses as the problem is not the initial killing, but the vengeance. It forbids killing in vengeance for murder and instead requires that the victim’s family settle the matter by using the courts. Elsewhere in the Iliad, characters acknowledge that blood money is inadequate and does not bring a dead man back, but that it is necessary to accept it to keep society functioning. The poem doesn’t tell us how this court case turned out, but we can assume that it does not allow the victim’s family to kill the killer. If—and this is possible—the killer is to be killed, he will be executed with the authority of the community rather than killed in vengeance.

            But that’s not all we get here. The scene on Achilles’ shield also tells us about some trial procedure. There is a council of elders, and more than one will apparently attempt to make a judgment in the case. There is a crowd of people around and heralds to keep the crowd under control. The opinion of the crowd will play some role in the judgment of the elders, which lends a certain kind of democratic flavor to the proceedings. There is a reward of gold to be given to the elder whose decision is the ‘most just’, and we don’t know how that will be decided. The elders might decide among themselves who has ruled most justly, the litigants might decide, or the opinion of the crowd might decide. Two talents of gold is a lot of gold, though! So: this is a voluntary arbitration between two parties before multiple elders and a crowd of people who may have some influence over the decision. There are officials, the heralds, whose role is to ensure orderly proceedings. The elders will not refer to a body of written laws or any kind of code, but will decide based on principles of justice and social convention. This is not exactly like later courts, but it does show the origins of Greek law in the late Dark Age. That is, it seems to reflect the practice known to the person or persons who wrote down the Iliad in the 8th c., rather than the procedure of heroic, Bronze Age Greece.

4. Developments in the Archaic Period    

[slides #7, 8] In the seventh century (the Archaic Period, an era of big social change), the Greeks began to turn to written laws. The first written Greek laws, according to legend, were not in Greece at all, but for the colony of Locri in southern Italy (you’ll see the lovely beach of Locri on the left side of the slide). These were written by a lawgiver named Zaleucus. We have inscriptions (=texts etched into stone) of laws from this period including an extensive set of laws that we might even call a law code from Gortyn on Crete (image of Gortyn code on the right of the slide). [slide #9] Written laws are significant because they show a move toward equality and consistency in the law. Sources like the Iliad write of the fair judgments of elders who make decisions based on justice and custom, but other sources, like for example Hesiod’s poem Works and Days, complain that judges and kings are not always fair. We should note, though, that although written laws make a move towards equality under the law, many inequalities are written into early Greek laws. For example, enslaved persons and women were not equal to men, non-citizens were not equal to citizens, and different social and economic classes had different laws and punishments apply to them. [slide #10] Early written laws indicate this feature by using a different word for law. In Greek a custom is a nomos (the plural is nomoi) and this is also a word for law, but early written laws were also called thesmoi (the singular is thesmos). In the Classical Period both words mean law, and we’ll see that there are Athenian officials called the nomothetai and others called the thesmothetai. Nomoi are like ‘common law’ in that they are based on precedent, whereas thesmoi, to the extent that a firm distinction can be made at all, are more like modern civil law in that they are based on written principles rather than the tradition of previous cases. Since the elders decided cases based on nomoi, or custom, early written laws sometimes distinguished themselves from that system by calling themselves thesmoi.

            [slide #11] The archaic period was a time of intense class and political competition between democrats, oligarchs, and tyrants. Sometimes a city would introduce written laws as a way of reducing conflict between these parties within the city since laws, once written down, were definite and less liable to partisan interpretation. In these instances, the city would try to find a non-partisan man to write the laws, and if none was available in the city they would ask an outsider to come in and write laws for them. It seems counterintuitive that an outsider would make laws, but this outsider was a Greek, so he wasn’t really all THAT much of an outsider. This practice of inviting a lawgiver to write laws shows that Greek law didn’t just develop from procedures to settle disputes between individuals, but it developed from class competition as well. We can surmise, then, that laws were aimed at the people who were powerful enough to participate in those conflicts. The laws dealt with relationships between powerful men—heads of households—and to the extent that they involved women, the poor, and enslaved persons, they involved them as they related to landowning citizen men. A crime against an enslaved person or a woman, for example, was not usually a problem except that it was a crime against the kyrios or the head of the household to which the woman or slave belonged.

5A. Draco and Solon: Draco [slide #12]

            Two men stand out as key figures in the development of Athenian law, Draco and Solon. The first lawgiver in Athens was an archon (=magistrate) named Draco [slide #13]. In 621 BCE, Draco wrote the first Athenian law code, and it had the reputation for being very harsh. We get the word “draconian” from this code, and it used to be thought that many crimes, even small ones, were punishable by death. However, it is probably the case that—as we’ll see when we look at Draco’s homicide law—it was not so much that it decreed an automatic death penalty as that it allowed lethal self-help (a kind of DIY revenge). One of the features of written law is that it makes judicial procedures mandatory. That is, unlike the voluntary arbitration we saw in the Iliad, with written laws the decisions of officials must follow the provisions of the law. But since self-help and private vengeance was so prevalent in Greek custom, for Greek laws to work at all with the established culture they had to institutionalize in writing the possibility of private vengeance or self-help. Draco’s law on homicide forbids vengeance killing, but makes numerous exceptions as to when vengeance killing is allowed. So Draco’s laws may not have been so much ‘draconian’, or ultra-harsh, as it were, as they were permissive of private acts of vengeance.

 

5B. Draco and Solon: Solon

Draco’s law code was a good first step, but it did not halt aristocratic competition or social change [slide #14] and in 594 BCE Solon revamped the Athenian government and law code. He abolished all of Draco’s laws except for the law on homicide, and he wrote new laws to replace them. He wrote the laws on wooden tablets posted in public so that all could see them, which is wonderfully egalitarian in theory, but in practice this meant only that they were available for those who could read and had the leisure time to look at them. Still, Solon’s laws were an important development. Even though Athenian law would change much over time, Solon’s laws were never abolished as a whole and all throughout the Classical Period the Athenians regarded their laws as having been written by Solon. [slide #15] As with most Greek laws, Solon’s laws were about men, and wealthy men at that. They were democratic rather than oligarchic, and they emphasized small-scale farmers and landowners rather than aristocratic families, but they were still not fair to those who were not elite men.

            [slide #16] One main goal of Solon’s laws was to regulate government and behaviour. Before Solon, Athens was mostly ruled by the aristocratic council of elders known by the name of their meeting place: the Areopagus, which means “the hill of Ares” (or “Ares’ hunk of rock”). This council was not unlike what we saw in the Iliad, as a council of elders who met to dispense justice. But in the archaic period political organization was happening on a larger scale than in the Dark Age. Each member of the Areopagus was a member of a household, or oikos (the plural is oikoi). In archaic Greek poleis the heads of some powerful oikoi—the head of an oikos is called a kyrios—formed councils to share power in the polis. Over time, these power sharing mechanisms became highly institutionalized with various kinds of offices.

            By the time of Solon, [slide #17] the nine Archons were the most important magistrates and the Areopagus was made up of men who had served as Archons. The process for becoming an archon changed over time. In the 6th century BCE, they were elected. In the 5th century the Athenians would elect a short list and then choose the archons from that list by lot. At some point allotment replaced election even for the preliminary stage. The archons were:

  1. basileus (king, in charge of religious matters)
  2. Polemarchos (warleader)
  3. eponymous archon (the year was named after him)
  4. six thesmothetai (judicial officials).

 

The history of the word basileus is an interesting one, because Homer uses it to name the captains of the armies in the Iliad. Odysseus was a basileus, as were Agamemnon, Menelaus, Achilles, Nestor, and Ajax. They were kings in their cities, and they cooperated with each other. In the Archaic Period, however, the king in a city had been reduced to a largely ceremonial role, and he was in charge of religious matters. If there was a monarch there, he was not a basileus, but a tyrannos, a tyrant. We’ll see in the Oresteia next week that the Greeks considered men ruled by a basileus to be weak. But the office nevertheless remained an important one for religious and judicial matters.

 

6. Legal and Political Structures: the Jury Courts and Magistrates

            [slides #18, 19] Before Solon, magistrates, by which I mean the archons, would decide legal cases themselves. Solon allowed litigants to appeal a magistrate’s decision and he set up a court called the Heliaia, sometimes spelled Eliaia (either way is correct). At first, it seems that the Heliaia was just the Ekklesia called by a different name. This is a fundamental move toward democracy. The Ekklesia was an assembly open to all adult male citizens, and any member of the assembly had the right to stop a magistrate from issuing a summary judgment of him by making a formal appeal to the demos (= the ‘people’). This right to appeal to the people was in some ways the fundamental idea of Athenian courts and trial procedure. But once it was available, soon everyone took up that right at trial and it became too cumbersome for the assembly as a whole to try all of them .

            [slide #20] As a result, the Heliaia evolved into a complex system, and the word could refer to the whole system or to an individual court within it. A magistrate with the appropriate jurisdiction would preside over the court and announce the verdict of the jury. The magistrate did not have the authority to make arguments or give instructions to the jury; basically, he was only there to organize the proceedings and make sure everyone followed the proper procedures. This meant, effectively, that the right to appeal a magistrate’s decision was only rarely necessary. For the most part magistrates didn’t bother with issuing a summary judgment that would be appealed and instead sent the case to trial. But we’ll see in a moment when we look at legal procedures that many vestiges of the old summary procedure and appeal remained in the system.

            [slide #21] In the 5th century, the Athenians selected a volunteer pool of 6000 jurors. Each juror (dikastes) would take the following oath at the beginning of the year:

I will vote in accordance with the laws and decrees of the people of Athens and of the council of five hundred, and on matters where there are no laws, I will vote in accordance with the most just opinion.

 

This large pool of six thousand jurors was the source for the smaller juries for cases and functioned as a stand-in for the Ekklesia in most instances; it was very rare, generally speaking, for the full assembly to hear a case. Only the most important of public cases were tried before the assembly. [slide #22] Jurors were citizen men over 30 years old, and in the 450s BCE, began to be paid for their service. The famous Athenian statesman Pericles instituted a payment of two obols per day (three obols after 425 BCE) for jury service. This was less than what a day of physical labor would earn, and the stereotype was that jurors were men too old for physical labor. We have evidence from Aristophanes’ comedy Wasps, that suggests Athenians didn’t think much of the stereotypical juror. While in some ways the Athenians had great confidence in their court system, we can see from their comedy that they made jokes about how unreliable jurors were. Democracy is not a guarantee of justice, and the Athenians, confident as they were in the democracy, were well aware of its shortcomings and failures.

            [slide #23] Let’s look now at the jury courts more closely. The evidence is not fully clear, but it seems that for most of the Classical Period the standard jury size was 500, but special cases sometimes had different numbers of jurors, and there is a report of one case (a graphe paranomon—we’ll talk about what that is later—against Speusippos by Leogoras in 415 BCE, if you’re curious and want to look it up) that used the full 6000. So when you think of a jury in Athens, don’t think of ‘twelve angry men’. Modern juries are small bodies that can deliberate. Athenian juries were huge by modern standards, and in that way they replicate the experience of a trial before the full assembly. Even so, rich men tried to bribe jurors enough that a complicated lottery system was devised so that nobody could predict what jurors would be hearing what cases.

            [slide #24] As the system of courts developed, the magistrates presided over courts with specialized jurisdictions. The jurisdictions of the Archons were:

  • Basileus: religious matters and homicide (with the ephetai as jury)
  • Polemarchos: cases involving non-Athenians
  • Eponymous archon: family and inheritance cases
  • Thesmothetai: all cases not under the jurisdiction of the others

 

 

[slide #25] In addition, other officials had other jurisdictions:

  • The Eleven: in charge of prisons and executions, but could also try cases of major theft themselves
  • Agoranomoi: cases involving disputes in the Agora
  • Sitophylakes: cases involving grain
  • Epimeletai tou Emporiou: cases involving grain in the port (aka the Peiraeus)
  • Eisagogeis: cases involving loans and banking
  • The Forty: these tribe judges conducted most of the preliminary hearings for private cases

 

Over time [slide #26], the population of Athens grew further and the city also kept closer ties with its allies in the Delian League. The Delian League was a coalition of allied poleis initially formed to fight the Persians, but after the Persian Wars were over Athens took advantage of its leading position to take more and more control over its so-called allies. What had been contributions to a war fund soon became tribute paid to Athens. And because Athens had developed closer ties with the League, the citizens of member cities became more involved in Athenian court cases. As a result, it was necessary to create various boards of minor officials to oversee cases, including the nautodikai and xenodikai. The nautodikai were judges of sailors, and the xenodikai were judges of foreigners. These two boards of officials operated from the mid-5th century to the mid-4th century. After that time the political situation changed and Athens did not have the same need for special judges developed to deal with the Delian League.

[slide #27] So, that’s a survey of officials and their jurisdictions in the Classical Athenian legal system. The Athenians also developed a judicial calendar that determined in what months certain kinds of cases could be heard. There were practical reasons for this. For example, from the mid-4th century “mercantile cases” (dikai emporikai) could be brought from Boedromion to Mounikhion (roughly September to April of the modern calendar, not unlike the academic year). Because most commercial trade involved sea travel, and this took place primarily in the summer months, men who might be involved in commercial litigation were unlikely to be available for court during the summer months. By scheduling dikai emporikai during the winter when sailing was more difficult and less common, the calendar made it easier for merchants to appear in court.

 

7. Political History and the Judicial System [slide #28]

But I’m starting to get ahead of myself talking about mercantile courts and the calendar. Let me back up so I can tell you about political and legislative developments—after all, laws are not only judicial but also relate to politics in the ways that they are enacted. [slide #29] After the lawgivers Draco and Solon, the next key development was that of Kleisthenes, who reformed the Athenian government in 508 BCE. Under this government, a citizen could propose a new law to the Boule, a council of 500 that determined the business to be considered by the Ekklesia, which was the full assembly of Athenian citizen men. If the Boule agreed to it, the Ekklesia would consider and vote on the new law, which was called a psephisma (plural psephismata). By this time, as it happens, the term thesmos had mostly fallen out of use to describe the laws, and Greek laws were called nomoi again. But Kleisthenes made a distinction now between nomoi and psephismata, which we might now translate as laws (nomoi) and decrees (psephismata). The assembly could pass decrees that were considered binding, but these would not change the substance of the laws, though they could affect the way the laws were applied. Though Kleisthenes reformed the government, the Athenians still considered their laws to be the laws of Solon.

[slide #30] About a century later, in 410 BCE the Athenians decided to inscribe the laws of Solon and Draco’s homicide law on stone. As a part of this project, they discovered some contradictions and wanted to resolve them. There are two phases to this. Before 403 BCE the contradictions were resolved by the council (Boule) and assembly (Ekklesia). After the reinscription (or reorganization, if you will) of the laws was completed in 403 BCE, there was a different procedure for creating or changing legislation. The reinscription was an important moment because it showed the Athenians, almost two hundred years after Solon, trying to make the law code make sense as a complete and rational system suitable to the new time in which they lived. They kept the old laws, but needed to make some changes. This need to reorganize their laws was in large part motivated by the changes and chaos of the Peloponnesian War. Remember this was the war fought between Athens and its democratic allies on the one hand and Sparta and its oligarchic allies on the other. [slide #31] In 404 BCE, Athens lost the Peloponnesian War to Sparta and Sparta imposed a governing board of 30 men on Athens. These men became known as the Thirty Tyrants and the democratic resistance led by Thrasybulus overthrew them and restored the democracy in 403 BCE. We’ll learn more about this later. The philosopher Socrates was one of the Athenians who refused to cooperate with the Thirty and nearly paid for it with his life. Of course the (democratic) courts ended up executing him soon enough anyway. We’re also going to read a speech by the lawyer Lysias, who was a prominent member of Thrasybulus’ underground democratic resistance. In any case, in 403, the reinscription was completed with the restoration of the democracy.

[slide #32] After the reinscription of the laws and the reestablishment of the democracy in 403 BCE, the formal laws inscribed on stone were officially called nomoi (singular nomos) to distinguish them from psephismata, which is usually translated as “decrees.” As with most aspects of the reinscription, this was mostly how it had been before, but now it was official. [slide #33] In addition, the Athenians decided that no law was valid unless it had been inscribed on stone during the reinscription of 410-403 BCE and that no prosecution could be brought for actions taken before the restoration of the democracy. This was, in effect, a general amnesty meant to help the city move past the division that had happened when some Athenians were complicit with the Thirty and others fought against them. Of course there were still ways the old political enemies could fight each other in the courts, and it was very common for Athenians to use the courts not so much for justice as much as for a way to cause trouble for political enemies, but the amnesty was supposed to give everyone a fresh start. The fact that there had to be the general amnesty shows how much the courts were a political tool for elite men to use against each other rather than a system for justice.

 From that point on, the Athenians took steps to keep their laws up to date. In order to keep the law code rational and relevant, the restored democracy established a board of legislators known as Nomothetai. [slide #34] The Nomothetai conducted an annual review of all the laws to resolve any contradictions. In addition, at the first meeting of the Ekklesia every year individual citizens could propose new laws and changes to existing laws. If the assembly voted to change or add a law, the Nomothetai would review the change and if they approved it, would inscribe it and announce it. It’s worth pointing out that while it was possible to change laws or add new ones under this system, it was technically not possible to eliminate a law altogether, just to amend it to so as to change its force. The Athenians were loath to remove completely any laws already enacted.

In the late 5th century, (thus around the same time as the reinscription of the laws), Athens also introduced a legal procedure known as graphe paranomon [slide #35].This was a charge against someone proposing a law or decree that was contrary to already established laws. It was a serious charge and in some cases it could carry the death penalty. This helped to keep Athenian laws the same and had a very conservative force. It was dangerous to try to change Athenian laws, and even proposing what could seem like a reasonable decree was sometimes vulnerable to this charge, which was a powerful tool for prosecuting political opponents in the very high-stakes game of Athenian democracy among the elite.

 

8A. Legal Procedures in 5th century Athens: private cases [slide #36]

So far, I’ve given you a brief history of the Athenian government and the structure of the judicial system in the Archaic and Classical Periods. What I’m going to do now is give you a description of legal procedures in the Classical Period, which is to say I’m going to talk about what you would do if you were living in Classical Athens and you had some kind of legal issue and needed to use the courts. [slide #37] The historical root of Greek law—as we saw in the Iliad—was arbitration of a dispute between two people. In Classical Athens, the simplest and fastest way to resolve a dispute legally was through arbitration. Although any citizen could serve as a private arbitrator, the state also had a requirement that male citizens over the age of 59 had to serve as public arbitrators if required.

[slide #38] In the Classical Period, if two Athenians had a private legal dispute, the plaintiff (that is, the person who was initiating legal action) would first approach the four judges of his tribe (Kleisthenes had organized Athenian citizens into units called tribes [phylai]). If the amount in dispute was more than ten drachmas (either for restitution or penalty, the drachma being the standard unit of currency), the judges would refer the case to a public arbitrator. If the two parties were satisfied by the arbitrator’s decision, it was binding, but either party could appeal the decision and demand a jury trial. This was a vestige of the old system whereby a party unsatisfied with a summary judgment could appeal to the assembly, but in the Classical Period it was an appeal to a jury. It also shows the root of the Athenian justice system in disputes between parties being resolved by them asking someone impartial to decide. The public arbitrators, as the requirement that men at least 60 years old serve in this way shows, were not that different from the elders on the shield of Achilles.

[slide #39] This system of arbitration was for private cases. Athens distinguished between private cases, called dikai idiai (singular dike idia) and public cases, called dikai demosiai (singular dike demosia). Because the most common forms of public cases involved filing written charges, dikai demosiai were commonly called graphai (singular graphe) and dikai idiai were commonly called simply dikai. I’ve talked quite a bit now about the modern distinction between civil and criminal law, but it really doesn’t quite match up with the Athenian distinction between public and private cases. Too much of what WE think of as criminal acts (maybe like embezzlement, or assault) were subject to private actions (that is, between private individuals and not involving the state) rather than public ones; and public cases often involved payments to the victim as well as punishments like fines payable to the state. Still, it’s worth noting that the Athenians did make such a distinction.

[slide #40] The major procedural difference between public cases and private cases was that private cases could only be initiated by the aggrieved party, or in the case of homicide by the relatives of the victim (since the truly aggrieved party was in fact dead), while public cases could be initiated and prosecuted by any citizen in good standing. Again, the distinction in ancient Athens was between cases that involved individuals only (=private) and cases that considered the wellbeing of the community as a whole (=public). Solon made this distinction to allow citizens to look out for the public interest even if they were not directly harmed. Since under the democracy the Athenian citizens were all the government, any one could take up and prosecute a case in which the state had been harmed. The real difference between this and modern criminal law was that the Athenians had a slightly different idea of what harmed the state. Simply maintaining order was a matter for private disputes, especially because there was no police force to keep the peace, and crimes had to be more directly against the state to count as public.

 

8B. Legal Procedures in 5th century Athens: public cases

[slides #41, 42] Public cases developed a more complicated set of procedures than private cases did. The most important types of procedures for public cases were (and remember, by “prosecutor” we mean any publicly-minded and motivated citizen, not a district attorney or crown prosecutor etc.):

  • apagoge – The prosecutor arrests the defendant and brings him to the appropriate magistrate.
  • ephegesis – The prosecutor goes to get the appropriate magistrate and brings him to the defendant.
  • endeixis – The prosecutor goes to the magistrate and receives permission to arrest the defendant himself.
  • apographe – The prosecutor writes a list of state property wrongfully held by the defendant.
  • eisangelia – The prosecutor denounces the defendant before the Ekklesia, Boule, or Archon, which can then decide to try the case immediately.
  • Probole – The prosecutor denounces the defendant before the Ekklesia, which decides whether to refer the case to a jury.

 

[slide #43] In addition to the regular public procedures, there were a couple of similar procedures that were not exactly public cases, but were similar:

  • dokimasia – An examination of candidates for office by the Thesmothetai.
  • euthynai – A review of performance in public office by a special board of ten appointed by the Boule.

 

Although the dokimasia and euthynai were not technically trials, they did follow the procedures of trials and they had the form of trials. They could also carry penalties. It’s just that there didn’t need to be specific charges. They were automatically triggered by a candidate declaring for an elected office or an official leaving office at the end of the term.

[slide #44] All of these procedures were for public cases, and they were initiated by volunteer prosecutors. To encourage volunteers, Solon instituted various financial rewards for victorious volunteer prosecutors, usually a percentage of the fine. From the beginning of public cases in Athens, Solon saw that it wasn’t enough to rely on simple patriotism or a sense of duty to get people to put in the effort and to take the risk of prosecuting a case, so they had to be paid. But they weren’t automatically paid. They had to win the case to be paid a percentage of the fine, and this meant that it wasn’t worthwhile to prosecute frivolously cases that were not likely to result in a victory.

[slide #45] Nevertheless, if there wasn’t any real risk except investment of time or making enemies, the temptation of a percentage of the fine went too far in the other direction. One of the problems of offering financial rewards for volunteer prosecutors was so-called ‘sycophancy’. The Greek word doesn’t mean what it means to us today (an obsequious form of flattery), but was rather a very specific legal term for someone who prosecuted a public case in bad faith, not for justice but merely for the monetary reward. To discourage this, any volunteer prosecutor who abandoned a case after it had started or who failed to get at least 20% of the jurors to vote for him was fined 1000 drachmas. In more severe cases, it was possible to prosecute a man for sycophancy, in which case the penalties could be more severe, and could include disenfranchisement or fines of more than 10,000 drachmas, which was a lot of money.

            [slide #46] In addition to volunteer prosecutors, there were also court figures called Synegoroi (singular Synegoros); these were effectively co-operating litigators who worked with one party or the other in a case. There were some special kinds of occasions when Athens used Synegoroi. First, ten were chosen by lot annually to preside over the euthynai, or review of magistrate performance. Second, when the Ekklesia referred a case to a jury trial, it could also appoint a special prosecutor, a Synegoros. Finally, Synegoroi were the officials who defended the present laws against proposed changes before the Nomothetai. These were not exactly trials, but they had the form of trials, and so even though the synegoroi were not really public prosecutors, they are sometimes described as though they were. In this way, it’s not 100% right to say that Athens had no public prosecutors, it’s just that they didn’t prosecute criminal cases.

           

9. How the Courts Worked [slide#47]

But getting back to legal procedures, we’ve now seen a number of ways a case could be brought to trial. It could be a private case referred to trial by a public arbitrator or an appeal of a public arbitrator’s decision, or it could be a public case brought by one of several procedural actions. [slide #48] Once it was determined that a case would go to a jury trial, it was the responsibility of the plaintiff to file the complaint and pay a filing fee. He then informed the defendant of the date of the trial, the presiding magistrate, and the substance of the complaint or charge. If the plaintiff won, the defendant would have to reimburse him for the filing fee. Whether this plaintiff was a volunteer prosecutor in a public case or an aggrieved party in a private case, it was his responsibility to act as both prosecutor and police, performing the summons himself.

            [slide #49] When the plaintiff and defendant appeared in court on the appointed day, this was for a preliminary hearing in which the magistrate asked the defendant whether he admitted or denied the charges. While for the most part a defendant could be expected to deny the charges—because if he didn’t the case would have been settled already before it got to this phase—this was still an important part of the trial. At the very least, it was a vestige of the magistrate’s old authority to issue a summary judgment, and in some cases (for example a case of theft before the Eleven) it was still possible for a magistrate to issue summary judgment at a preliminary hearing if the defendant could not plausibly deny the charges. For example, if someone accused someone else of theft, it wasn’t enough just to say, “I didn’t do it.” Under some kinds of procedures, the defendant had to provide a plausible excuse for being caught in someone else’s house, like “I was just stealing back something he had previously stolen from me.” In most cases, though, this was really a formality that allowed the magistrate to organize the jury trial for a particular date in a particular court.

            [slide #50] During the preliminary hearing, however, the defendant could make a procedural challenge. Sometimes this challenge depended on a fact that could be disputed. For example, if a case involved a non-citizen in the early 5th century, it would normally be heard by the Polemarch. A defendant could call a witness to assert that he was an Athenian citizen and so the Polemarch should not hear the case. Such a witness statement was called diamartyria. This was only for facts that could possibly affect the case, and generally speaking a witness statement was enough. Diamartyria did not allow for more complicated arguments than simple assertions of facts.

            [slide #51] Beginning in the late 5th century, after the reinscription of the laws, it was also possible to initiate a counter-prosecution in which the defendant claimed that the plaintiff was bringing the case to court in some way that was contrary to the law. For example, a defendant could assert that the matter had already been decided by another court. This procedural challenge was called paragraphe. It initiated a separate trial and was effectively a prosecution of the plaintiff by the defendant. This was important because it allowed for procedural challenges that were more complex than the simple assertions of fact allowed by diamartyria. It also investigated the evidence of the facts more thoroughly than witness statements could, by allowing argumentative speeches by the defendant and the plaintiff.

            Okay, now the preliminaries and the procedural challenges were done. [slide #52] When the day for the trial arrived the plaintiff spoke first, followed by the defendant. The length of the speeches was limited by a water clock. The water clock was a jug full of water with a hole in it, and when the jug was empty the speech was over. The time for the speeches varied depending on the type of trial, but trials were finished in a day and often in much less time than that.

            [slide #53] Today people very rarely speak for themselves in court. When we discuss Roman law we’ll see that for them too it was normal for lawyers to speak on behalf of their clients in court. In Classical Athens, however, lawyers did not speak on behalf of litigants. Rather, the plaintiff and defendant spoke for themselves. Often a litigant would hire a speechwriter called a logographer (in Greek a logographos, plural logographoi). The speech prepared by the logographer would be written with the litigant represented in the first person. Later on this semester we’re going to read a speech that’s referred to as being “by Lysias”, but Lysias was a logographer and the “I” in the speech is actually a man named Euphiletos.

            [slide #54] The speeches by the litigants encapsulate all of the evidence, arguments, and witnesses. There was no opening statement or closing statement and no cross-examination when questioning witnesses or presenting evidence but if a litigant wanted to he could call the same witnesses as his opponent. The litigants would be responsible for presenting the relevant laws and producing documentary and physical evidence. Some of our best sources for specific statutes in Athenian law are speeches, but sometimes we just have a note in a speech saying that the law is read out in a particular place or a witness is called in a particular point in the speech.

            [slide #55] In the 5th century witnesses testified orally, but in about 375 BCE a law required witness statements to be submitted in writing, although witnesses were generally present and could be summoned to testify to the truth of their statements. If a witness wasn’t going to be present, it was possible to summon the witness and compel him to be present to swear that his statement was true. Most of the time women, children, and disenfranchised citizens could not testify at all, and evidence given by enslaved persons was only admissible if it was obtained under torture. There were instances, in homicide trials, for example, when the rules for witnesses were a little different and it seems that women at those times could give evidence in court. In general the presumption was that a head of a household or, if necessary, another adult male relative could represent a woman or a child in court, and crimes against women or children were really crimes against the head of their household. Since hearsay was not admissible in an Athenian court, though, in extreme cases like homicide it was possible for women to provide a statement.

All right, in our hypothetical trial both sides have presented their speeches. What’s next? [slide #56] There was no instruction given to the jury by the magistrate and there was no deliberation. Instead, the jury voted immediately. The voting mechanism changed over time. In the 5th century the jurors voted by putting a pebble in either the urn for the plaintiff or the urn for the defendant. In the 4th century, there was just one urn and now the jurors voted by placing in it either a solid bronze disk or a bronze disk with a hole in it. The verdict was decided by a simple majority, and a tie was counted as a victory for the defendant. This means that the magistrate actually had little control over the case, and it put extra responsibility on the litigants to ensure that the jurors were familiar with the relevant laws. The large size of the jury made it more difficult to bribe jurors or for a small group to sway a case, and it more closely approximated the experience of trying a case before the whole assembly. Moreover, the Athenians valued speed in their trials, so immediate voting and a simple majority decision helped to make sure everything was done quickly. Again, trials had to conclude before the end of the day.

So now let’s say we have a verdict. [slide #57] If the jury decided for the plaintiff, then the penalty phase would begin. In some cases the penalty was preset and determined by law, but in many cases the plaintiff and the defendant each made a speech suggesting a penalty, the jury voted for the one they thought was more appropriate. Possible penalties included compensatory damages to be paid to the plaintiff, a fine, confiscation of property, atimia (disenfranchisement), confinement in stocks, exile, and death. There was a prison in Athens, but it was not used for punishment, only for holding people while they awaited execution. Execution, which could be variously by throwing someone into a pit, by exposing them tied to a plank, or by making them drink hemlock, was usually done immediately. It was only when there was some religious reason to wait, like a holiday, that there was any delay.

There you have it: a typical trial in Classical Athens. [slide #58] Although there was no appeal procedure, there were some technicalities that could nullify verdicts. For example, it was possible that a defendant who had failed to appear and was convicted by default could get a retrial if he appeared within two months. It was also possible to sue a witness for giving false testimony, and if this was proven the penalty could be revoked or the false witness could be made to compensate the victim. At a relatively late stage, it became possible to accuse a witness of false testimony and have the issue resolved before a verdict was given, but for the most part false testimony was something that made the false witness liable for damages to the litigant rather than a reason to have a verdict overturned. Evidence suggests that the Athenians were not especially convinced of the reliability of witnesses in general, but they did recognize that this could be a serious problem, especially in cases demanding the death penalty.

 

10. What’s in a Crime? Homicide and Theft [slide #59]

[slide #60] Many acts that we would consider crimes were handled through private cases (dikai) in Athens. Homicide in particular involved special procedures and the Basileus presided over homicide trials because they were religious matters. It’s worth examining these procedures. The Ephetai, a special panel of 51 members of the Areopagus, served as the jury. The procedure involved a pronouncement that the defendant must not enter temples, lawcourts, the Ekklesia, or the Agora. The procedure was designed to cleanse miasma (a contagious pollution caused by wrongdoing). The fact that the Basileus presided and that the defendant was forbidden to enter temples shows that homicide was a religious matter for the Greeks, more than was the case for other kinds of crimes. There is even a kind of homicide trial where an inanimate object, like for example a knife or a roof tile, could be convicted of homicide and ritually ejected from the city. But even though homicide was a religious matter that could infect an entire community, it was still a dike rather than a graphe, meaning that a third party could not prosecute. The prosecutor had to be a member of the family of the victim.

[slide #61] Theft is also an interesting case, as “The Eleven,” who were in charge of prisons and executions, dealt with many thefts summarily. They were authorized to execute summarily certain kinds of thieves if they admitted guilt. It seems impossible that someone knowing he faced summary execution would admit his guilt, but this is one of those times when an accused thief not only had to deny his guilt but also had to convince the Eleven that he had a plausible defense. If he couldn’t come up with a plausible sounding defense on the spot, the Eleven were empowered to kill him. Also, forcible theft was an example of an action that could result in both compensation to the victim and payment to the state. This shows that even a dike, which is a private case because it must be prosecuted by the victim rather than by a volunteer third party, was in some ways like a criminal case. In the modern system a civil case involves compensation to the victim and a criminal case involves punishment by the state, but in Classical Athens a single, private case would include both kinds of penalties.

[slide #62] We will also, later on this semester, look more closely at some charges concerning sexual conduct. Among other laws, it was permissible for a kyrios, the head of a household, to kill a man who seduced a free woman of his household if he caught them in the act. Prostitution was not illegal as a rule, but there were some restrictions. In general it was illegal for citizens to be prostitutes or for anyone to have sex with a married woman. The usual punishment for a citizen who had been found to be a prostitute was disenfranchisement (i.e., loss of citizenship), and it was also illegal to be a pimp for citizen prostitutes. It was not illegal for citizen men to have sex with enslaved persons or prostitutes of either sex, but having even consensual sex with a citizen woman was a crime against her husband, father, or guardian. Rape was illegal, but as with consensual sex it was not really a crime against the person who was raped unless he happened to be an adult male citizen. Otherwise it was a crime against the man in charge, being the owner of a slave, the father of a child, or the husband or father of a woman.

[slide #63] There are a variety of other “crimes” listed in the VerSteeg textbook, including hubris (outrage) and asebeia (impiety). It was also possible to use the procedure called eisangelia to prosecute wrongs that were clearly wrong but did not technically have a law against them. This a holdover from a time when one man had to come up with all the laws at once, and the law codes had some gaps. In the case of an obviously serious crime that wasn’t technically illegal, it was still possible to prosecute by eisangelia, though this kind of trial was rare.

[slide #64] Modern common law calls civil wrongs “torts.” Although Classical Athens did not have this category, many acts which count as modern torts were still subject to legal penalties in Athens. Examples include battery (dike aikeias), false imprisonment (dike heirgmou), defamation (dike kakegorias), and a variety of actions including breach of contract and property damage (dike blabes). The legal distinction between a ‘crime’ and a ‘tort’ shows us how little legal categories matter in the popular imagination of crime. I suspect most of us think of people liable for serious torts as criminals, even if they are not technically guilty of crimes. This shows that the legal categories only loosely map onto the psychological categories of crime and criminality, and that to understand crime we need to move beyond the law and look at cultural representations.
