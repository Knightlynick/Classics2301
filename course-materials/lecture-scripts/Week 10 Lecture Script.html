2. Introduction

            This lecture is an introduction to Roman law. As with Greek law, there’s a lot to cover. We’ll survey the history of law as a concept at Rome, the development of the legislative system (how laws are made), and also look at how laws worked in a practical sense (judicial application of laws at Rome). [slide #2] Just as Athenian law also involved politics, so too is this the case with Roman law. If we think in terms of legacy, however, it’s not Athenian law but Roman law that still serves as the basis of much modern European law. That said, the field of Roman law usually focuses on private law (or civil law) rather than public law (or criminal law). This introduction to Roman law covers both, spending some time on civil law because of its importance in history (and to give some context for criminal law) and some time on criminal law because of its more direct relevance to our class. Also, it’s more fun.

 

3. Early Roman Law

[slide #3] Scholars of Roman law think of its development as happening in various phases; there are two main ways of classifying these phases. In the first scheme, there are three phases: the pre-classical (up to the first century BCE), the classical (through the third century CE), and the post-classical (from the beginning of the fourth century CE). This division into pre-classical, classical, and post-classical is specific to Roman law. Another—more historically focused—periodization divides the phases into four periods: the early republic (ending in the third century BCE), the late republic (ending in 31 BCE), the classical period (lasting until the crisis of the third century CE), and the later empire or postclassical period. These categories are more specific than the ones you learned in the historical survey in Week 2, but are helpful ways of grouping eras and developments of the legal and administrative systems. Moreover, periodization is valuable because it emphasizes that Roman law was not always the same, but it’s also worth remembering that historical change happens gradually and not often all at once.

Because Roman law is different in different periods, [slide #4] it’s useful to spend some time on the development of Roman law from the origins of the city. Rome didn’t just spring up with a fully formed legal system, and as was the case in Greece and Athens, the development of the legal system was largely a political process. [slide #5] The traditional date of the foundation of Rome was 753 BCE, and (as you may remember) at first Rome was ruled by kings who were elected for life. The legendary first king was Romulus, and the second was Numa Pompilius, who was often credited with the creation of many Roman laws. The names of the kings we have in the writings of Roman historians are probably not at all a real list of kings’ names. It seems to have been invented much later after Rome got bigger and Romans decided that they needed a compelling origin story.

Nevertheless, since later Romans attributed to individual kings some early developments that would become influential, we can use the writings of Roman historians to guess about how early Rome developed laws. [slide #6] One of the important early developments was the system of so-called patrons and clients. The Romans attributed this development to Romulus, their founder and first king. As the system was understood, each plebeian was required to find a patrician patron. Though it evolved a great deal over the centuries, this system remained in place throughout Roman history. What it meant was that if you had a conflict with someone else in ancient Rome, your first recourse, besides simple direct action/self-help, was to go to your patron. Your patron would help you either directly by violence or, in the upper levels of society, by finding you a lawyer and helping with your court case. This means that in the lower strata of Roman society, there was a kind of hierarchy that was outside of the formal government structure, and that hierarchy modified the ancient reliance on self-help.

[slide #7] In the regal period, like many Greek poleis of the time (note: this corresponds roughly to the Archaic Period of Greek history), the Romans dispensed justice largely by custom rather than law. Roman legend records that the kings created laws largely on family and religious matters. There was a close connection between religion and law at Rome, and at first the priests had control of the written laws. It’s not often apparent from the very beginning of a community what does and what doesn’t need to be legislated. If you imagine a village like Rome growing slowly into an empire, you can see how in the early days everybody would just know the local customs about what was right and wrong, and officials or patrons could make decisions about individual conflicts or cases based on that. But Roman religion from an early date was conceived of as a kind of contract between the Romans and the gods, and so the beginnings of written law were in religious regulation. Also, as in Athens, private property and inheritance were important at a formative stage, and so family and inheritance law was another area in which the early Romans saw a need for formal legislation.

[slide #8] We don’t know a lot about the Regal Period in Rome, though. Things start to become clearer with the beginning of the Republican Period. According to legend, it was in 509 BCE that the Romans overthrew the last king and instituted as the chief magistrates two annually elected officials. They chose two because that way no one person would be in charge without a check on his power from someone else. We say that Roman magistracies were “collegial,” because each magistrate always had at least one colleague in the same position. [slide #9] These praetors held imperium, or the power to command. Each official also held the power of intercessio, which allowed him to stop an action of the other magistrate. Intercessio is often called veto power, because in Latin veto means “I forbid it.”

[slide #10] In the early 5th century BCE, the plebs (or plebeians) seceded from Rome and formed their own government headed by annually elected tribunes of the plebs. This began the so-called Struggle of the Orders, by which we mean the struggle between the aristocratic patricians and the less well-born, but more numerous, plebeians. Over time, there came to be some very wealthy and powerful plebeians, but traditionally the patricians were the ones who were in charge from the beginning. There was a relatively small number of important families who governed Rome and shared the political offices between them, and this aristocracy was unfair enough that the plebeians decided they could simply do without it. They formed their own government. They had an assembly for voting, and they elected officials called tribunes to be in charge. [slide #11] To get the plebeians back, the Roman patricians made concessions and allowed the plebeian assembly to pass laws that were binding on everyone, including the patricians. They also gave the tribunes the right of intercessio, so they could halt the actions of the patrician magistrates. The whole process took about 150 years to fully resolve. And in another step towards transparency, the patricians made written laws public via the Twelve Tables in 450 BCE. The Twelve Tables weren’t a systematic law code exactly, but their publication did mark an important milestone in legal history. The introduction of a written law code that was posted publicly meant that the priests no longer had secret control over the laws. It meant that everyone who could read (or who could find someone to read for them) could know what the laws said.

 

4. The Political System at Rome

[slide #12] Just as Athenians saw their laws as beginning with Draco’s laws and Solon’s laws, Romans understood their laws to have begun with the Twelve Tables, from about 450 BCE. And the Twelve Tables were incredibly resilient: they were not superseded until Justinian’s codification of 528-34 CE, namely about a thousand years later. [slide #13] Implicit in the Twelve Tables and explicit by the end of the fourth century BCE was the right of provocatio, meaning that a citizen could appeal the summary judgment of a magistrate to an assembly of the Roman people. You may recall that in Athens, one of Solon’s important reforms was allowing people who had been convicted by magistrates the right to appeal to an assembly of the people. Eventually, this turned into jury trials and the complex Heliaia. In Rome, this appeal procedure was called provocatio (meaning a calling forth), and sometimes it gets a fuller name provocatio ad populum, or “calling forth to the people”. It meant that Roman citizens could in effect demand a public trial before the assembly of the people instead of just accepting the decision of a magistrate.

[slide #14] In 367 BCE, the Romans created a third major political office and revamped the titles. The original two chief magistrates had originally been given the name “praetors”, but now (and forever) they became known as “consuls”, and this new magistrate (who was subordinate to the consuls) was called a praetor (if you want a way to remember this, you can think of it like a rebranding: the original offices got a new and improved title (consul) and the old title was given to the new magistrate – Dr. Nousek). The praetor and the consuls now all held imperium, but the consuls held maius imperium, which meant that their imperium was greater and the praetor could not halt a consul’s actions. So at this point there started to be a hierarchy of offices. The higher offices—and the office of consul was the highest—could stop the actions of any other magistrate. Lower down the hierarchy, the magistrates could halt the actions of a magistrate of equal or lesser rank. The exception was the tribunes of the plebs, who could halt the actions of any other magistrate of any rank, including the consuls. This gave the tribunes of the plebs the ability to check excessive power by any magistrate, and so protect the plebeians. [slide #15] While all Roman magistrates with imperium were also military commanders, they increasingly took on administrative duties in Rome. Here, it’s the praetor (that is, that new office created in 367 BCE) who’s most important for us, and for discussing the legal system. The praetor was largely responsible for the justice system, and he appointed judges. [slide #16] By 242 BCE, the Romans were dealing more with cases involving foreigners. They added a second praetorship and from then on one praetor was the praetor urbanus and the other was the praetor peregrinus. The praetor urbanus, or urban praetor, dealt with cases involving Roman citizens, and the praetor peregrinus, or peregrine praetor, dealt with cases involving foreigners or non-Romans.

[slide #17] After the Second Punic War a regular order of offices, called the cursus honorum was established. Magistrates with imperium had virtually unlimited authority when outside the city (militiae). When in Rome (domi), the authority of magistrates was somewhat limited by the rights of provocatio and intercessio. By the late republic (2nd-1st centuries BCE), the cursus honorum looked like this (see image on slide): At the bottom of the hierarchy were twenty quaestors. For plebeians it was possible to skip the quaestorship and start out as tribunes of the plebs. There were ten plebeian tribunes. Above them were four aediles (two patrician and two plebeian). Above the aediles were eight praetors, and above them were two consuls. Only the praetors and consuls held imperium. Over time, since the praetors and consuls were the military commanders (and it was their imperium that made them military commanders), it happened that there were too many campaigns for just the eight praetors and the two consuls to manage. Moreover, the praetors and consuls were needed in the city to govern. So the Romans developed a system of prorogation, which meant that after a praetor or consul served for a year in office, they would be asked to continue as a proconsul or propraetor. Now the praetors and consuls elected for the year could be in the city running things, and the ex-consuls and ex-praetors would be out in the field taking on military commands and provincial governorships. There are also two other offices that weren’t active all the time. In times of emergency, it was possible for the Romans to choose a dictator who would take overall sole control. He had imperium, and he was not subject to intercessio. Despite their general antipathy toward monarchy, the Romans also recognized that sometimes it was important for one person to be in charge. The other occasional office was the censorship. Here the Romans would periodically choose two censors who were in charge of conducting the census. They not only counted the population, but more importantly they determined each citizen’s property class (kind of like a tax bracket, but not for taxes). Things like where to vote and what role you would serve in the army were determined in Rome by how much property you owned, so the censors would make sure to enroll the right citizens in the right property classes. Later on, the censors were given the job of assigning contracts for state business (tax collection, military equipment, etc.). Overall, the cursus honorum meant that Roman politicians worked their way up through the ranks to more and more powerful offices. Only the very best (i.e. most influential or well-connected) politicians made it to the big time, to be censors.

As for the officials lower down the chain, quaestors were financial officials, and aediles were in charge of archives and public works. The praetors are the most important officials for us in this class, because they played a big role in law. They appointed judges in some cases, and served as judges in others, and it was their job to preside over legal matters. [slide #18] Although the praetors were not exactly legislators, in terms of creating official legislation, they each promulgated an edict upon taking office. This edict outlined how they planned to carry out the duties of the office, and where it involved law it almost had the force of legislation. Legal scholars call law based on the praetor’s edict the ius honorarium, to distinguish it from more formal legislation passed by the assemblies (we’ll get to those shortly). [slide #19] Tradition dictated that the praetor’s edicts changed very little over time, and eventually under the empire in the second century CE the edicts of the praetors and aediles became completely static and unchanging.

[slide #20] So much for the magistrates; in addition to those officers of government, there were also many lawyers in Rome. At first, the only lawyers were the pontifices (singular: pontifex), who were priests. By the end of the fourth century BCE, law was no longer exclusively the province of the pontiffs and being a lawyer (along with serving in the army and holding political office) was one of the most honorable jobs in Rome. [slide #21] Besides speaking in court at trials, Roman lawyers had three jobs: ad respondendum (giving legal advice, e.g. to the praetors), ad agendum (preparing cases for court), and ad cavendum (drafting documents). By the middle of the second century CE, those who were jurists (=legal scholars) had much more prestige than those who were advocates (=court lawyers), and beginning with Augustus, some possessed the so-called ius respondendi ex auctoritate principis. The Latin means ‘the right of responding from the authority of the emperor’, and it basically means that the opinions of that lawyer were as binding as if the emperor had said them.

 

5. Jurists and Legal Schools

[slides #22, 23] Now that we’ve come to the point where legal scholarship was a regular practice, we can look briefly at some important individual jurists. The first great jurist of the Classical period of Roman law was Marcus Antistius Labeo. He didn’t just teach law—he also wrote books on law. Little of his work has survived, but he was regarded as the original source of the “Proculian school” named after the first century CE jurist Proculus. [slide #24] Another important jurist of the first century CE was Masurius Sabinus. His most celebrated work was a book on ius civile (civil law ~ private law), treated separately from ius honorarium and public law. He took a practical approach, in contrast to the principled approach of Labeo, and his student, Gaius Cassius Longinus, helped him found the schola Cassiana, later known as the Sabinian school.

[slide #25] In the second century CE, the emperor Hadrian made the opinions of jurists with the ius respondendi binding, and he reorganized the imperial bureaucracy with a well-paid career structure for so-called equestrians (rather than freedmen [note: the ‘equestrians’ were a kind of upper-middle class group, less wealthy and prestigious than senators]). This encouraged wealthy Romans to study law, and increased the demand for schools and teachers. [slide #26] One of the most important Roman law teachers of the second century was a man called Gaius (a proponent of the Sabinian school). Interestingly, in 1861 in Verona, scholars discovered a palimpsest of his Institutes, which were his lecture notes from 160-161 CE. What is a palimpsest, you ask? I’ll tell you! In ancient Rome, some kinds of paper were really valuable, and often instead of getting new paper, Romans would scrape off what was written and write over it. Sometimes the scraping wasn’t very thorough and it’s possible to read under the new writing the traces of the old writing. That’s a palimpsest. So we have the Institutes because someone tried to erase it and write over it, but they didn’t do a very good job (see the image of the palimpsest on slide #26). [slide #27] Other important jurists included Aemilius Papinianus (active from 194 and died in 212), Domitius Ulpianus (active from 202 and died in 223), Iulius Paulus (active throughout the first half of the third century), and Herennius Modestinus (active throughout the first half of the third century).

[slide #28] The emperor Theodosius II reformed Roman law in the 5th century CE. He formalized law school and appointed two official professors of law. He also, along with the Western emperor Valentinian III, in 426 enacted the “Law of Citations,” which made the written opinions of Papinianus, Ulpianus, Paulus, Modestinus, and Gaius binding on magistrates. This was an significant step. Up to that point, jurists had a lot of prestige and those with the ius respondendi could speak with the authority of the emperor, but the writings of dead jurists didn’t count for much except as teaching tools. Theodosius wanted a more complete and formal written law system, so he said that the writings of Papinianus, Ulpianus, Paulus, Modestinus, and Gaius counted as written laws, and magistrates had to decide cases according to those writings. Theodosius also appointed a commission to compile a complete and official law code, and promulgated the Codex Theodosianus in 439 in the East and soon after in the West.

[slide #29] In 528, the emperor Justinian ordered an even more complete job, and commissioned a man called  Tribonianus to oversee what would become the Corpus Iuris Civilis. This consisted of four parts: the Codex Iustiniani, the Novellae (additions and adjustments to the Codex), the Institutiones (effectively a second edition of Gaius’ Institutiones, a beginning textbook), and the Digest (about 2000 fragments or quotations of 39 different jurists from as far back as the first century BCE, with about half from Ulpian and Paul). This is our most complete and our most important source for learning about Roman law. If you study Roman law formally, you will eventually end up reading especially the Digest. The Corpus Iuris Civilis was an effort, and a very good one, to write a complete and stable law code for the empire. It was larger and more complete than any law code that either Greeks or Romans had managed up to that point.

 

6. Roman Legal Principles and the ‘Constitution’

[slides #30, 31] The Corpus Iuris Civilis is our most important source for Roman law, and by this I mean it’s the most important way we learn today about the specifics of Roman law. But in addition to talking about our sources for Roman law, I also want to talk about what the Romans regarded as the sources of Roman law. That is to say, where do the principles of Roman law come from? This requires some understanding of the Roman constitution, by which scholars don’t mean an individual document, but the makeup of the state. [slide #32] The most ubiquitous representation of the state is the abbreviation SPQR. You see it all over Rome today, including on manhole covers. SPQR stands for Senatus Populusque Romanus, or ‘the Senate and the People of Rome’. The senate was an advisory council of elders, made up of former magistrates and reviewed by the censors. After your year in office as a tribune or quaestor or whatever, you would normally be enrolled in the senate for the rest of your life. Periodically, though, the censors would look into the lives of the senators and kick out anyone who had behaved inappropriately. This could be because of corruption or legal trouble or for some kind of moral violation. But that didn’t happen all that often. For the most part senators were senators for life. Although the senate did not technically have any binding authority, it issued statements called senatus consulta (singular senatus consultum) that advised Roman magistrates, and the magistrates nearly always followed the traditional authority of the senate. A senatus consultum didn’t, strictly speaking, have legal force, but because the senate was respected, its advice was generally taken and then a law would be passed based on the principles of the advice given. A lot of Roman government, like Roman law, was simply based on tradition. Nowhere was it written down that a magistrate had to follow a senatus consultum, but it was just what they did. It was a really big deal when a magistrate ignored a decision of the senate, but it did happen sometimes.

So that takes care of the senate part of SPQR. The other part is the People. The Romans had a number of different voting assemblies that made sure that the votes of the (male) citizen population were represented in the government. [slide #33] In addition to dividing the people into patricians and plebeians, the Romans also organized their citizens into three kinds of assembly: the centuriate assembly (comitia centuriata), the tribal assembly (comitia tributa), and the plebeian assembly (concilium plebis).

 

[slide #34] Things to note about the centuriate assembly:

  1. It is based on the assembly for recruiting armies. As in ancient Greece, for most of Roman history military service was based on owning property. A citizen was required to serve in the army, but he had to buy his own equipment. The wealthiest could afford the best armor and weapons and even horses, and they became the so-called Roman knights, or equites. The poorest became the worst armored and worst armed foot soldiers, and battle was a lot more dangerous for them. That meant, though, that the first and most important Roman assembly was the one that divided the people by property classes so they could determine who would serve what role in the army. That assembly was the centuriate assembly, and it became the most important of the voting assemblies for Roman citizens.
  2. It is organized by wealth, and the wealthy ‘centuries’ (the centuries don’t have anything to do with ‘100’ [which is really annoying, I know! – Dr. Nousek] but are better thought of as just units or groupings) are much smaller and vote first. It makes sense that there were fewer rich people than poor people in Rome. The people in the centuriate assembly were divided into centuries, and since there were fewer wealthy people those centuries were a lot smaller.
  3. There were 193 centuries in total, and each one gets one vote in the assembly. If the wealthy centuries were smaller and each century got one vote, that means that in the centuriate assembly, rich people’s votes counted for more than poor people’s votes did. The members of each century would vote, and the majority would determine the vote of that century. Then the decision would be made by a vote of the majority of the centuries. The centuriate assembly favored the wealthy over the poor.
  4. It elects consuls, praetors, and censors, votes on declarations of war, and hears citizens’ appeals in capital cases. The centuriate assembly was the most important one, and it made the most important decisions. So the elections for the highest offices, the consuls, praetors, and censors, were done in the centuriate assembly. It’s worth reiterating that that means that in the most important elections, like for the consulship or for declarations of war, it was institutionalized that rich people’s votes counted for more.
  5. Assemblies are for voting only. Debating and public discussion happens in contiones (singular: contio). Unlike the Athenian assemblies, where the debate would be followed immediately by a vote, in Rome the assemblies voted only. The campaigning and speech making happened elsewhere.

 

[slide #35] Things to note about the tribal assembly:

  1. It is organized by the location of residence. Unlike the centuriate assembly, where wealth mattered, the tribal assembly didn’t care about wealth. While at first it was purely geographical, it was also possible to inherit a tribal affiliation from your parents even if you moved out of the area. So it was mostly geographical, but over time it got kind of mixed up as people kept their old tribes when they moved to new places.
  2. There are 31 rural and 4 urban tribes (as of 241 BCE) and they vote in an order determined by lot (i.e., by random draw order). Unlike the centuries, where the order of voting is determined by wealth, in the tribal assembly the tribes vote in random order.
  3. It elects curule aediles and quaestors, votes on leges (singular: lex), and judges trials. While the most important elections were done by the centuriate assembly, the tribal assembly elected the officials further down the cursus honorum.

 

[slide #36] Things to note about the plebeian assembly:

  1. It is organized by tribe. Like the tribal assembly, the plebeian assembly is more egalitarian than the centuriate assembly. It doesn’t systematically favor the wealthy.
  2. It is made up of plebeians only and presided over by tribunes of the plebs. While both plebeians and patricians voted in the centuriate and tribal assemblies, patricians were excluded from the plebeian assembly.
  3. It elects tribunes of the plebs and plebeian aediles, votes on plebiscites, and judges trials. The plebeian assembly of course elects the tribunes of the plebs and deals with plebeian matters.
  4. Although the plebeian assembly did not vote on leges which we usually translate as “laws”, they did vote on plebiscita (singular: plebiscitum), which after the lex Hortensia of 287 BCE were binding on the whole of Rome.

 

So to review quickly, there were three major voting assemblies. The centuriate assembly, or comitia centuriata, included patricians and plebeians, it favored the wealthy, and it voted on the most important matters. The tribal assembly, or comitia tributa, included both patricians and plebeians, it was more egalitarian, and it voted on less important matters. The plebeian assembly, or concilium plebis, included only plebeians, was organized by tribe, elected plebeian tribunes, and passed plebiscites that were binding on everyone.

 

[slide #37] Not all important Romans were politicians. Senators, in fact, were officially forbidden to engage in business or trade. Wealthy men who chose business over politics were called equites (singular eques) or the “equestrian order.” They are sometimes referred to in English as “knights.” This is because they belonged to the wealthiest property class that wasn’t senatorial, and so back in early Rome when people were equipping themselves (later this was state-funded) they could afford horses for military service. The senators were the army commanders, and the equites were the cavalry or knights. But by the time we have any decent records, the important thing about the equites was not their army service, but rather their wealth and business activities. [slide #38] Equites often bid on contracts with the senate or with magistrates to carry out public business, like the collection of taxes. These equites were called publicani. As tax collection in the provinces became more important and more complex, publicani often exercised great influence. So even though the publicani weren’t senators or politicians, they did have a lot to say about policy in the provinces and in Rome.

[slide #39] Another important group in Roman politics were priests. Priests were members of the senatorial elite elected for life by a special assembly of the tribes. The different types of priesthoods had different responsibilities. We’ve already met one type in this lecture, the  pontifices. They were mostly in charge of sacrifices and festivals, but in the early republic (as we saw above) they had control of the laws as well. Augurs were in charge of the auspices and augury (i.e. bird-watching), and thus had the power to delay or prohibit public business. Before just about any public event, the augurs had to “take the auspices.” They read the omens to determine if the event could happen. You can imagine then, that if an augur didn’t want a particular vote to happen, he could just say that the omens were bad and it wasn’t going to happen. This was also the issue with Publius Claudius and the sacred chickens (from back in the lecture for Week 1). If the augur really wanted the battle to happen, he’d make sure that the chickens were hungry. In this case, the omens were bad and when Claudius went ahead with the battle anyway he was prosecuted and found guilty. So priests could influence Roman politics and legal matters as well.

 

7. Political Organization under the Emperors

So that’s the constitution of the Roman Republic. [slide #40] After 27 BCE, when Augustus made his settlement with the senate, the constitution of Rome was different. Although technically and officially Augustus restored the republican government, he maintained the final authority and over the course of the next few centuries the Roman government became increasingly centralized and hierarchical, developing a bureaucracy. [slide #41] By the early second century CE, the assemblies no longer voted on laws, and the emperor appointed magistrates. The emperor (or his staff) drafted legislation and sent it to the senate for approval. [slide #42] Under the republic, the sources of law were the Twelve Tables, the praetorian edicts, leges, plebiscita, and to some extent senatus consulta. By the fifth century CE, the source of law was the writings of the jurists. (Note from Dr. Nousek: you’ll want to be aware of these distinctions)

 

8. Legal Procedures in Ancient Rome

[slide #43] Now that I’ve talked a bit about the Roman government, I’ll move on to talk a little about legal procedures in Rome. That is, if you had a legal issue to settle in ancient Rome, what would you need to do? Let’s look first at civil law and procedures. [slide #44] The early form of civil procedure in Rome was known as legis actiones (singular: legis actio), or ‘actions of law’. These depended on the use of specific wording. Under this system, a plaintiff brought a defendant before the praetor by a formal summons called in ius vocatio, initiating the in iure phase. The plaintiff specifically quoted a legal procedure and the praetor interviewed the litigants. If the praetor determined that the law applied, he appointed a judge (iudex, plural: iudices) for the apud iudicem phase. The judge heard the arguments and issued a decision, called a sententia. Case closed.

[slide #45] Sometime in the second century BCE, this civil procedure changed. A law called the lex Aebutia[1] legitimized a new kind of procedure called formulary procedure. This had the same structure as the legis actio system, but was more flexible. Now, in that in iure phase, the praetor was not restricted to applying the strict wording of an established procedure, but instead used the interview to write a formula, which defined the case and issued instructions to the iudex. [slide #46] The formula consisted of a iudicis nominatio that named the judge (or 3-5 person panel of recuperatores), an intentio that stated the plaintiff’s claim, and a condemnatio that defined the circumstances under which the defendant should be found liable or not. [slide #47] If a defendant was found liable and failed to pay, a plaintiff could initiate an actio iudicati, in which a magistrate would authorize the plaintiff to use force to exact the payment. There was no appeal procedure beyond the possibility of making an argument at the actio iudicati hearing. [slide #48] Beginning with Augustus, there was also the possibility of a cognitio extraordinaria or cognitio extra ordinem (extraordinary procedure). This became the usual procedure after the second century CE. Here, the emperor, a magistrate, or a delegated official would hear the entire case, combining the in iure and apud iudicem phases. Over time, the possibility of appealing to the next-highest official in the hierarchy became regular as well.

[slide #49] In addition to these three civil procedures, there were criminal procedures in Rome also. These began from the imperium of the magistrates, which included the authority to imprison, castigate, or fine citizens, slaves, women, or foreigners. Roman citizens had recourse to provocatio ad populum. [slide #50] Before the second century BCE, the procedure was rare and straightforward. Anyone could draw the attention of a magistrate with imperium to an action that harmed or might harm Rome. The magistrate would then decide whether to investigate, and possibly punish the offender. The offender could, if he was a citizen, appeal to an assembly of the People. One of the things to remember about this is just how few Roman magistrates held imperium. The consuls and the praetors were the only regular offices with imperium, so there were not a lot of people who had the ability or authority to take on criminal proceedings. This was fine in early Rome, because there really weren’t a lot of criminal cases. Most legal cases were private cases rather than public ones.

[slide #51] Women, children, and slaves could not appeal the actions of a magistrate, which was an effect of the state’s refusal to interfere in family affairs. The paterfamilias (head of a family, cf. Greek kyrios) held the patria potestas (‘fatherly power’), which was theoretically, and occasionally practically, the power of life and death over members of the family and the ultimate authority for all matters. In ancient Rome, as in ancient Greece, the family was the basic unit of society. By this I mean that a household had a head, the paterfamilias or father of the family, and he took care of his family. He was the one who had control of the finances, and if there was a divorce (which was actually very easy to do in Rome), the father always got custody. Although adult citizen sons could establish their own homes and vote in the assemblies, as long as the paterfamilias was alive even adult sons were legally still under the authority of the father and he controlled their money. In practice fathers would allow their children, both men and women, to control their own finances, but legally he actually owned all the money and had to assent to financial transactions. The patria potestas was technically absolute power for the father within the family, and if a father felt his child deserved to die for some reason, at least traditionally he could kill the child. In practice this really was only the case when an infant was born, and the law required the father to inspect the child and to kill the child if it was unfit or deformed in some way. Even this didn’t usually happen in practice, though, and the Romans were pretty tolerant of physical disabilities. A father was also allowed to sell his children into slavery, but there was a law explicitly saying that if a child had been sold into slavery three times (I guess sometimes they got bought back), that child was no longer subject to the patria potestas.

Roman marriage was of two varieties. It was possible for a woman to get married and remain under the patria potestas of her current paterfamilias, but it was also possible for a woman to get married and transfer into the patria potestas of her husband’s paterfamilias. Marriage without the transfer of the patria potestas was by far the more common type. It was also possible for women to be independent and not in the potestas of any paterfamilias. Women could speak in court, but often a woman’s male relatives would represent her in court or in legal matters.

[slide #52] Magistrates were generally only interested in prosecuting crimes that directly involved or threatened the state, and for the most part criminal proceedings involved only senators or other powerful men. As in ancient Greece, there was in ancient Rome a much narrower idea of the interest of the state in criminal matters than there is in the modern world. Without police forces or even an extensive bureaucracy, it just wasn’t possible for a state apparatus to deal with criminality in the way modern states do. Instead, public or criminal cases were for things like treason or electoral bribery. That meant that for the most part criminal cases dealt with senators. That doesn’t mean, though, that what we think of as criminal acts, like murder, were okay. It just means that most things we think of as criminal were matters for civil law in Rome.

So the criminal procedure was basically that a magistrate would investigate and make a decision, and sometimes there would be a trial before the appropriate assembly. [slide #53] At some point by the second century BCE, it became possible for a magistrate to set up a quaestio rather than investigating a criminal matter himself. If he chose to do this he would appoint a consilium of advisors and act as prosecutor and judge, ultimately reaching a verdict and determining a punishment himself. The quaestio, then, was a kind of ad hoc criminal court. There was no jury, but it meant that the magistrate could have a team of people helping him to investigate and to give him legal advice.

[slide #54] In 149 BCE, the lex Calpurnia established the first quaestio perpetua (plural: quaestiones perpetuae), or permanent court. Again, Roman laws were named after the senators who wrote and proposed them, so the lex Calpurnia was proposed by someone named Calpurnius. The particular court that the lex Calpurnia established was the quaestio de repetundiis, to hear cases of repetundae, or financial misconduct (sometimes referred to as “extortion”) in the provinces by a governor. It was normal for a provincial governor to expect that he would make money from the post, but it was also possible to take it too far. A provincial governor who was found to have taken too much and to have treated the people of the province too harshly could be prosecuted in Rome by any Roman citizen who was willing to take up the cause. The quaestio perpetua was presided over by a magistrate, but it used a jury to come to a decision, and in this way it replaced a trial before the assembly. [slide #55] While the ad hoc quaestiones continued, on the model of the quaestio de repetundiis, the second and first centuries BCE also saw the creation of quaestiones to deal with maiestas (more fully maiestas minuta populi Romani, ‘diminishing the majesty of the Roman people’), veneficia et sicarii (‘poisoning and murder/assassination’), ambitus (‘electoral bribery’), and vis (‘political violence’). All of these are really political crimes, or crimes that directly harm the interests of the Roman state. Even the court for poisoning and assassination was not, at least in the beginning, really about murder. It was about political assassination.

[slide #56] In 82 and 81 BCE, the dictator Lucius Cornelius Sulla added more of these permanent criminal courts, and they continued to be added through the reign of Augustus (the last one was Augustus’ court for adultery). The expansion of the quaestiones perpetuae expanded the involvement of the state in criminal law, although throughout the Republican period the state’s interest remained largely restricted to wealthy men and political matters. [slide #57] The lex Cornelia de sicariis et veneficis, which established the relevant quaestio perpetua in 81 BCE, is particularly interesting as it not only established a permanent court for murder, but it also criminalized carrying weapons with the intent to kill or steal as well as making and selling poisons. Originally it seems to have applied to political murder or assassination only, but was soon applied to any kind of murder. So when I said way back at the beginning of the course that murder was not a crime, you could make an argument that in 81 BCE it became one. It really was the case that only a small percentage of the killings or murders that happened in Rome were actually prosecuted under this law, but they could have been. And the prohibition against carrying a weapon in the city with the intent to kill or steal was a step toward the state restricting violence in the city.

[slide #58] What about the prosecution and defense in criminal courts? How did the case procede? Any Roman citizen could initiate and prosecute a trial at one of the quaestiones perpetuae by making a request to the magistrate in charge. The trial began with a nominis delatio (‘denunciation of the name’), attended by both prosecutor and defendant, at which the president of the court would write an inscriptio. Once the prosecutor had signed the inscriptio, he could not withdraw the case without penalty. This rule was like Solon’s penalties for frivolous prosecution in Athens. It prevented Romans from making criminal charges against someone and then offering to drop them in exchange for money or a favor or something. Once you started a case, you had to see it through. [slide #59] After at least ten days, but often longer, there would be a trial (usually outdoors in a public location) at which the prosecution would speak first, followed by the defense, and then there was a formal presentation of evidence and witness statements. Women could be witnesses, but normally not people under the age of twenty in criminal cases. Enslaved people could provide testimony only under torture. [slide #60] Juries were made up originally of senators, but at various times the composition of juries was changed to include a percentage of equestrians—that is, which men were eligible to serve on juries in criminal cases was for some time a controversial issue as one group or the other felt that a jury composed only of its class-peers wouldn’t be fair and unbiased. The number of jurors varied based on the court and on the current political climate. For example, the quaestio de repetundiis usually had 75 jurors, while the quaestio de vi usually had 51, but various laws changed that at various times. A majority of jurors decided the winner, and a tie counted for an acquittal. [slide #61] During the Republic there was no appeal of the verdict of a quaestio perpetua. Beginning with Augustus, however, it became possible to appeal the verdict of a quaestio perpetua to the emperor.

[slide #62] What about penalties for crimes? Sometimes they were explicit in the relevant statutes, and sometimes the penalty was voted on by the jury. Possible criminal penalties included infamia (‘disenfranchisement’), interdictio aqua et igni (‘prohibition of water and fire’, i.e. exile), relegatio (‘exile without loss of citizenship’), multa (‘a fine’), death by burning, crucifixion, being hurled from the Tarpeian Rock, drowning in a sack, damnatio in metalla (‘condemnation to the mines’), damnatio in opus publicum (‘forced labor’), and damnatio in ludos (‘condemnation to the schools’, i.e. the arena). [slide #63] After Augustus, even though there was a new political system in that Rome was ruled by an emperor, the quaestiones perpetuae continued to be used, only becoming completely obsolete in the third century CE. The cognitio extraordinaria (see above, p. 17), however, took over much of their function, serving as both civil and criminal courts. In addition the emperor and the senate heard most major political cases.

[slide #64] What about law enforcement? We saw that in Athens there wasn’t really any form of police force to keep order in the city. At Rome, it was kind of similar: while there was no police or law enforcement in a formal sense, from the early history of the city there had been an office called the praefectus urbi (‘prefect of the city’) who held imperium within the city of Rome temporarily while the king, or later the consuls, were away from the city. Augustus revived this office and gave it to a senior senator, usually an ex-consul. He put the prefect in charge of the cohortes urbanae, or urban cohorts – a military unit stationed in or near the city of Rome. The urban prefect, along with the praefectus praetorio (the ‘praetorian prefect’) and other prefects (notably of the vigiles, i.e. the night watch), now had the duty of keeping order in the city and extended criminal law to the lower classes. [slide #65] The extension of criminal law to the lower classes in the early Empire increased the importance of differential treatment based on class. Honestiores were generally treated better and given less severe punishments than humiliores. The exact makeup of the group of honestiores and the group of humiliores is uncertain and varied over time. [slide #66] Augustus was particularly interested in new moral legislation, and so for example he set up a quaestio perpetua de adulteriis, not only criminalizing adultery but also making divorce after adultery mandatory. Adultery here means having sex with a married woman. Men, generally, could have sex with anyone who was not a free man, woman, or child in the family of a citizen, but wives could initiate divorce at any time.

[slide #67] There’s one final type of legal case to consider. Somewhere between the modern classification of crimes and torts are Roman delicts. Delicts (Latin: delicta, singular: delictum) had some of the characteristics of civil law, in that the procedures were similar and they were prosecuted by the aggrieved parties, and some of the characteristics of criminal law, in that penalties were punitive. [slide #68] Justinian and Gaius recognized four basic kinds of delict: furtum (theft), rapina (robbery, or theft by force), damnum iniuria datum (property damage), and iniuria (personal injury). In addition, they recognized as “quasi-delicts” a small and oddly specific set of actions including causing damage by pouring or dropping something from a building, hanging something from a building in a way that was likely to cause damage, rendering an incorrect decision as a judge, or damage to property kept in a ship, inn, or stable.

9. Conclusion

[end slide] While Roman civil law has been extraordinarily influential and important for the history of law in western Europe, Roman criminal and delictal law has been much less so. Our focus in this course is on the kind of law that has attracted less scholarly interest and that is generally regarded as less impressive and well-developed than Roman civil law. Modern popular culture suggests that criminal law is more interesting, but we don’t know yet if our modern sensibilities and preferences will withstand the test of time like Roman civil law has.
