2. Introduction

            [SLIDE] This week, we focus on the first of several literary sources which deal with the subject of crime, criminality, punishment, and justice, especially as they developed over the course of the 5th century in Athens. Last week I talked about the gradual shift in Athens from the archaic period system of arbitration and the idea of ‘lethal self-help’ as allowed by Draco in his homicide law, to the establishment of a rather complicated system of law and legal procedure in the courts of democratic Athens in the Classical period. Right alongside that development, there also occurred the evolution of Greek Drama, particularly Greek tragedy, as an expression of popular belief and opinion, and as a reflection of ideas that were the focus of public debate. This week’s topic is the intersection of those two developments, the expression of Athenian ideas about crime, punishment, and justice, represented on the Greek stage. [SLIDE] Incidentally, while I was searching the Web for images to evoke the solemnity of Aeschylus’ trilogy, I came across this page for the BBC podcast In Our Time. In 2005 they aired an episode that examines the continued allure that Greek tragedy, and especially the Oresteia has in modern culture. If you’re interested in this topic, I recommend a listen.

           

3. Plot Summary of the Oresteia

This lecture covers the Oresteia. It can be a little difficult to follow the plot, so I’m going to start with a bit of background and plot summary. [SLIDE: Tantalus] The backstory of the Oresteia starts with a guy named Tantalus. Tantalus was really rich, and he was so awesome that the gods used to come over to his place for dinner. [SLIDE: Pelops | Arya] One day he decided to test the gods to see if they could tell what they were eating by cooking a stew made out of his son, Pelops. Demeter, who was really distracted because Hades had just abducted and assaulted her daughter Persephone, accidentally ate a little bit of the shoulder before she realized what it was. The gods put Pelops back together except for that piece of shoulder that they had to make an ivory prosthesis for, and he went on to have a pretty good life. He wanted to marry Hippodamia, who was the princess of a town called Pisa, which was near Olympus. Her father, Oenomaeus, had promised that she would marry whoever managed to get her into a chariot and escape his pursuit, which was probably a good plan because if they could get away he probably couldn’t stop them getting married anyway. So Pelops bribed her dad’s charioteer, Myrtilus, to loosen the lynchpins on the wheels of Oenomaeus’ chariot so that it would crash when he was trying to chase Pelops and Hippodamia. It did, and Oenomaeus was killed in the crash. As he was dying, he cursed Pelops. Pelops himself was fine and had a great life, but his family had a really rough time for a few generations.

[SLIDE: genealogy] Pelops and Hippodamia had six sons, two of whom were Atreus and Thyestes. These two brothers got along pretty well until Thyestes tried to take over Atreus’ place as king. Atreus banished Thyestes for trying to take the throne. Later, when he found out that Thyestes had also slept with Atreus’ wife, Atreus pretended to want to be friends again and invited his brother over for dinner. Secretly, he killed Thyestes’ sons and served them up for dinner, and then after dinner said, “Guess what you just ate?” [SLIDE: Thyestes | Arya]  And then he banished him again. [SLIDE: genealogy] Thyestes had one surviving son, named Aegisthus (that’s an important one to remember), and Atreus had two sons, Agamemnon and Menelaus. So Agamemnon was the king of Argos (in Aeschylus he’s the king of Argos, but in Homer he’s the king of Mycenae), and when Paris ran off to Troy with Menelaus’ wife, Helen, the two brothers sailed to fight at Troy. Agamemnon was married to Clytaemestra (who elsewhere, like in Homer, is called Clytaemnestra), and they had a few children, including a son, Orestes and two daughters, Iphigeneia and Electra. The winds to Troy were bad for sailing, and Calchas the prophet said that the only way to make them favorable was to sacrifice Iphigeneia. Agamemnon said, okay, no problem, and just like that he killed his daughter, the winds shifted, and the army sailed off to Troy. They would be gone for ten years, and eventually they sacked and burned Troy.

So while Agamemnon was at Troy, Clytaemestra was understandably upset that he had killed their daughter, and Aegisthus—Remember him? The son of Thyestes? Aegisthus sensed an opportunity. So he went to Argos, got close to Clytaemestra, moved in with her and started acting like the king, even though Clytaemestra was totally the one in charge. Clytaemestra was generally pretty happy with Aegisthus, but she was a little worried about her son by Agamemnon, Orestes, and so she sent him to be raised by some friends in a town called Phocis. And everything went along okay for a while. But then the Greeks finally sacked Troy, thanks to Odysseus and the whole Trojan horse thing, and Agamemnon was coming home. That moment is the start of the first play in the trilogy, the Agamemnon.

So with all of that as backstory, we start the Agamemnon when the watch-fires from tower to tower say that Troy has fallen and the Greeks are coming home after ten years. Clytaemestra starts to freak out a little bit, and things only get worse when Agamemnon shows up with his Trojan slave princess, Cassandra, not like Clytaemestra has a leg to stand on with the whole cheating thing. This is a recipe for an unhappy household, with Clytaemestra and Aegisthus and Agamemnon and Cassandra, so Clytaemestra and Aegisthus kill Agamemnon and call it justice, and then say everything will be good now. End of the first play in the trilogy.

The next play is The Libation Bearers, and it starts when the now grown-up Orestes and his friend Pylades get back into town. They first meet up with Electra to get the lay of the land, and then Orestes pretends to be a messenger saying that Orestes is dead. On the one hand, this is good news for Clytaemestra, since she doesn’t have to worry about him anymore. On the other hand, she’s kind of sad that her son is dead. But having used the lie to get into the house, Orestes kills Clytaemestra and Aegisthus and he’s happy that he has successfully gotten justice. But at the end of the play the Furies show up and start chasing him. It’s a disturbing scene because only he can see them and he starts freaking out but nobody else can see why. End of the second play.

So the third play starts when Orestes is running from the Furies, who want to drink his blood, and he’s taken refuge in the temple of Apollo at Delphi. Apollo purifies Orestes of the murder and says that’s the end of it, but the Furies refuse to accept the decision. They all go to Athens, where they have a jury trial. The Furies argue for the prosecution and Apollo for the defense, and when the jury votes it’s tied. Athena casts the deciding vote in favor of Orestes, but she placates the Furies by renaming them the Eumenides, that is ‘the kindly ones’, and offers them a home in Athens. There they make sure the Athenians get along with each other and take care of their own, and they reserve their enmity for outsiders. The end.

4. The Five Things

            [SLIDE: five things] Whenever you read any text, but especially an ancient text, you should know five basic things about that text: the title, the author, the publication date, the publication location, and the original language. The first ancient text I asked you to read is the Oresteia. I will ask questions about the five things on the midterm tests and final exam. You need to memorize the five things for all ancient Greek or Roman texts we read.

            [SLIDE: title] First, the title. We know the whole trilogy as the Oresteia. The title of the whole trilogy should be italicized or underlined. I use italics when I type, but when you write out the title by hand, you should underline it as a way of indicating that it would be in italics if typed. Note that I don’t capitalize or italicize “the.” It’s not really a part of the title. By convention, for most ancient titles you capitalize and italicize the title itself, but not the definite article. Moreover, you can say “the Oresteia” or “Aeschylus’ Oresteia,” but not “Aeschylus’ the Oresteia.” I know it seems arbitrary, but following conventions like this is one way of indicating to other people that you know what you’re doing. There is a common exception to this rule, and that’s when the title of a tragedy is taken from the name of the chorus in that tragedy. Greek tragedies had a chorus, and it was a group of characters in the play. In the Agamemnon, the chorus is made up of the elders of the city of Argos, and the play is named after the main character. In The Libation Bearers the chorus is a group of Libation Bearers, and so the “the” is part of the title. The same with The Eumenides, which is another play named after its chorus. And all this counts for the five things. You need to know all four of the titles and know when to capitalize and italicize and when not to. On the tests I may ask you to identify a passage from the Oresteia, and if I do you will need to name the specific play.

[SLIDE: author] Aeschylus is the usual spelling of the author’s name, but you may see other correct spellings occasionally, including “Aiskhylos,” which is a more direct transliteration from the Greek alphabet. [SLIDE: date] Aeschylus first produced the trilogy in 458 BCE, very late in his career, but still remember only a little over 20 years after the end of the Persian Wars. I ask you to memorize the date not so much because the number itself is important as because it’s a useful way to remember the relative chronology of events and to know, for example, what relation the date of the Oresteia has to the dates of other important events. [SLIDE: location] Aeschylus lived in Athens, and although he sometimes traveled to Sicily to present his plays, he first presented the Oresteia in Athens, and it was the last one he would present there. [SLIDE: language] Aeschylus wrote in ancient Greek, and specifically in the dialect of Attic Greek, which was the Greek of classical Athens.

5. Greek Theatre

[SLIDE: theatre image] At its peak, late in Aeschylus’ career, the theater of Dionysus in Athens could hold about 25,000 people. It’s hard to know exactly how many people were living in Athens at the time, but it may have been around 120,000 excluding enslaved people. The theater could hold a significant portion of the population of the city, and at the festival of the City Dionysia it did. The Athenian tyrant Peisistratus probably, but not certainly, inaugurated the City Dionysia in the 6th century. Greek tyrants were often patrons of the arts, and Peisistratus set up this festival of Dionysus where poets, singers, and theater types would compete for prizes. It happened every year in March. There was a great expansion of the festival around the time Kleisthenes established the democracy in Athens in 509 BCE. There is a connection between these plays and the politics of Athens, especially democratic politics. Aeschylus is discussing a serious political and social issue, but through the lens of myth. He’s really talking about contemporary Athenian issues, but he sets the trilogy centuries in the past. We can compare the way in which the poet of the Iliad told the tale of the heroic Bronze Age Trojan War, but nevertheless included elements—such as the arbitration scene on the Shield of Achilles, which we looked at last week—that reflected his own society in the 8th century BCE.

In the Classical Period (which you may recall was from the end of the Persian Wars in 480 BCE to the death of Alexander the Great in 323 BCE), the standard format of the City Dionysia went something like this: three tragedians would be chosen to compete. They would each write a tetralogy consisting of three tragedies and a satyr play. There were also other competitions and performances, but the most prestigious and the most time consuming competition was the one for the three tragic tetralogies. The tragedies were long and serious, and then the satyr play was a short, comic piece involving satyrs (hybrid goat/man creatures who were, quite literally, horny all the time). Most of the time the tetralogies were not thematically related, but sometimes they were. Aeschylus was particularly fond of presenting connected trilogies of tragedies, and the Oresteia is one of these. If you’ve read Sophocles’ so-called Theban trilogy about Oedipus, those three are thematically related but were part of different trilogies at different times in Sophocles’ career. The Oresteia is one connected story presented all at once.

Aeschylus was born in 525/4 BCE. He fought at Marathon in 490 BCE and probably at Salamis too in 480. He was already an established tragedian then, and I say tragedian because no Greek tragedian ever wrote comedies (satyr plays don’t count) and no Greek comedian wrote tragedies. They were two separate forms with two separate origins. He produced his first tragedy in 499. He won the competition at the Dionysia for the first time in 484 and won a total of 13 times. He presented the Oresteia in 458, right before a trip to Sicily, where he died in 455. He was the oldest of the three great Athenian tragedians, the younger two being Sophocles and Euripides. Tragedy developed from choral lyric, and Aeschylus was instrumental in the process. Choral lyric just means that a chorus would sing a song, but at some point somebody, usually Thespis is credited with this, added an individual actor, so that the chorus would have someone to converse and interact with, and thus tragedy as a theatrical form was born. When Aeschylus started writing, the set was just a raised platform in the middle of the stage and there was just a chorus and the one actor. Aeschylus is generally credited with introducing a second actor, though often only one actor and the chorus speak for long stretches. By the time of the Oresteia there was a third actor and the set was a façade representing a building, so the action all takes place in front of the building and people go in and do things and come out and talk about them. In the Agamemnon the building is the royal palace of Argos, and the action all takes place in that one place.

 6. The Agamemnon [SLIDE: Agamemnon staged]

What I’d like to do now, and this will be a very common structure for this course, is to look at some passages from the Oresteia that illustrate major themes, in particular ones that have something to say on the subject of crime and punishment. I’ll draw my arguments on crime and punishment from the analyses I do of passages. [SLIDES: 2016 performance | Ag. 146-159] The first passage I want to look at in the Oresteia is lines 146-159 of the Agamemnon. I’ll put these passages on the slides and read them (I think it helps to bring the literature to life, even if I am not a trained actor), but you’re also welcome to follow along in your books.

Agamemnon 146-159

“Healer Apollo, I pray you

let her not with crosswinds

bind the ships of the Danaans

to long-time anchorage

forcing a second sacrifice unholy, untasted,

working bitterness in the blood and fearing no man.

For the terror returns like sickness to lurk in the house;

the secret anger remembers the child that shall be avenged.”

Such, with great good things beside, rang out the voice of Calchas,

these fatal signs from the birds by the way to the house of the princes,

wherewith in sympathy

   sing sorrow, sorrow: but good win out in the end.

 

This is the chorus speaking, and they are giving us some of the backstory and setup. For me, the key sentence here is, “For the terror returns like sickness to lurk in the house; the secret anger remembers the child that shall be avenged.” This sets up the issue of violence within the household. Agamemnon had killed his daughter, Iphigeneia, and the chorus is pointing out here that he can’t expect to get away without any sort of consequence for this. The child will be avenged. I am going to argue over the course of this lecture that the Oresteia is a story of the establishment of a justice system that halts the cycle of private revenge. All of the acts of violence we see in the trilogy are acts of revenge for previous acts of violence. In the end, Orestes’ trial is a moment when the community of Athens, the democratic state, takes over the responsibility for crime and punishment. Until that moment, the only way to get any sort of justice is to do violence. The Oresteia shows a move from vengeance to punishment, and from responsibility for justice in the private sphere, the oikos (=the household), to the public sphere (=the polis).

            The next passage I want to look at is [SLIDE: Ag. 369-83] Agamemnon lines 369-383:

Agamemnon 369-383

                                                     A man thought

the gods deigned not to punish mortals

who trampled down the delicacy of things

inviolable. That man was wicked.

The curse on great daring

shines clear; it wrings atonement

from those high hearts that drive to evil,

from houses blossoming to pride

and peril. Let there be

wealth without tears; enough for

the wise man who will ask no further.

There is not any armor

in riches against perdition

for him who spurns the high altar

of Justice down to the darkness.

 

This is the chorus speaking about Paris, the prince of Troy, and how he violated hospitality by taking Helen from his host, Menelaus. Hospitality was extremely important in ancient Greek culture, and it can be hard for us to understand the seriousness of violating the peace between host and guest. Ancient epics like the Iliad have stories of soldiers refusing to fight each other in battle because their grandfathers had a tie of hospitality, which means that they had been guests in each other’s houses. So when the chorus says that a man “trampled down the delicacy of things inviolable,” what they mean here is that Paris violated the hospitality of Menelaus. Divine justice, says the chorus, comes for such men.

            But there’s irony in this passage, in that divine justice is coming for Agamemnon too. The curse on his family began when Pelops effectively kidnapped his wife from her father, and the curse of that kidnapping is passed down through the generations. This is divine justice—that actions are repeated and the cycle continues through the generations. Only the coming of political justice and the influence of Apollo and Athena will end the cycle of violence. We know this pattern of household violence today—domestic violence—and under a different name from divine justice. Domestic violence is indeed often passed through generations, and it takes an extraordinary effort or intervention to end the cycle. And indeed it’s not just the anger of the gods that punishes the children for the crimes of the parents, but the curse rather works in the repetition of the crimes. The chorus speaks of Paris trampling the delicacy of things inviolable, but Agamemnon is about to do the same. [SLIDE: Ag. 914-29]

Agamemnon 914-929

Daughter of Leda, you who kept my house for me,

there is one way your welcome matched my absence well.

You strained it to great length. Yet properly to praise

me thus belongs by right to other lips, not yours.

And all this—do not try in woman’s ways to make

me delicate, nor, as if I were some Asiatic

bow down to earth and with wide mouth cry out to me,

nor cross my path with jealousy by strewing the ground

with robes. Such state becomes the gods, and none beside.

I am a mortal, a man; I cannot trample upon

these tinted splendors without fear thrown in my path.

I tell you, as a man, not god, to reverence me.

Discordant is the murmur at such treading down

of lovely things; while God’s most lordly gift to man

is decency of mind. Call that man only blest

who has in sweet tranquility brought his life to close.

 

This is Agamemnon speaking to Clytaemestra now. There’s a lot going on here, and Agamemnon’s words play into gendered expectations. He’s enforcing some cultural stereotypes about women here, accusing Clytaemestra of talking too much. But more importantly, he is contrasting himself with “Asiatics” so we get cultural stereotypes as well. How many of you have heard of “Orientalism”? This is a cultural phenomenon that shows up as early in literature as the Iliad, which narrates a war between the Greeks and the Trojans, who are from what is now the west coast of Turkey, but what was called Asia in ancient Greece. It seems strange to us to look at the landmass that makes up Europe and Asia and view it as two opposing continents, but to the ancient Greeks, Asia (what is now Turkey) was across the Aegean Sea, and was a separate landmass. Even if they went overland, they would still have to cross the Dardanelles (which the Greeks called the Hellespont) or the Bosphorus at the site of Byzantium or Constantinople, where Istanbul is now. So Asia really was a different landmass to the Greeks, and the Iliad tells the story of a war between two continents. The Persian Wars did a lot to strengthen that image, and to some extent it’s never gone away. We still think of Europe and Asia as two continents even though it doesn’t make a lot of geographical sense, and Edward Said’s book Orientalism shows how the idea of a cultural divide or even conflict between the East (which starts in the Middle East where Troy was) and the West (which is the Greek side) has never gone away.

            One of the stereotypes of Easterners or ‘Asiatics’ in ancient Greek culture in general and Aeschylus in particular is that ‘Asiatics’ are effeminate. Women and ‘Asiatics’ don’t understand toughness and responsibility and leadership the way real Western men do. They steal and fight in cowardly ways, and they enjoy luxury too much. So when the chorus and Agamemnon talk about trampling on inviolable things, they’re invoking the stereotype that women and ‘Asiatics’ like luxury and delicacy. When Clytaemestra welcomes Agamemnon home by putting a luxurious robe down for him to walk on, he says that she is trying to weaken him and make him Asiatic Interestingly, Agamemnon points out that the absolute monarchy he associates with Asia, including the prostration of the subjects before the king, is not a marker of the king’s power, but a symptom of the Asiatic king’s arrogant lack of self-control as he unjustly tries to take the place of the gods.

            But Agamemnon gives in and does indeed walk on the robes. He is arrogant and luxuriant and effeminate just like the Asian kings he fights against. And it will be his undoing. Clytaemestra, who throughout the Oresteia is very masculine, will tangle Agamemnon in the robes as he walks on them, and this will immobilize him so that she can kill him. So in the previous passage I discussed (Ag. 369–383) the robes were a metaphor for violating hospitality, and in this passage they stand for orientalist stereotypes, and so it makes sense that they represent Agamemnon’s own arrogant belief that he could get away without vengeance for his crimes. He walks on the robes and becomes feminine and weak, and so he is punished.

            Of course, Agamemnon’s murder will not end the cycle. [SLIDE: Ag. 1275-85] This is Cassandra, the Trojan princess and prophetess foretelling the coming of Orestes:

Agamemnon 1275-1285

And now the seer has done with me, his prophetess,

and led me into such a place as this, to die.

Lost are my father’s altars, but the block is here

to reek with sacrificial blood, my own. We two

must die, yet die not vengeless by the gods. For there

shall come one to avenge us also, born to slay

his mother, and to wreak death for his father’s blood.

Outlaw and wanderer, driven far from his own land,

he will come back to cope these stones of inward hate.

For this is a strong oath and sworn by the high gods,

that he shall cast men headlong for his father felled.

 

She knows she’s going to die. By the gods, says Cassandra, she will be avenged. And the instrument of vengeance will be Orestes. I love this description. Outlaw and wanderer, he will come back to cleanse the house. This passage sets up a new theme. I’ve spoken a little bit about the parallels between the shift in justice on the divine and human levels. This passage rather shows the contrast between divine and human law. The divine law says that Orestes is right to take vengeance, but as long as Clytaemestra and Aegisthus rule Argos, human law will make Orestes an outlaw, even though he has not yet committed the crime. An unjust king, like Aegisthus, can turn a just man into an outlaw.

            But certainly Clytaemestra and Aegisthus don’t see themselves as unjust. Clytaemestra is proud of killing Agamemnon. She sees it even as necessary. [SLIDE: Ag. 1372-87]

Agamemnon 1372-1387

Much have I said before to serve necessity,

but I will feel no shame now to unsay it all.

How else could I, arming hate against hateful men

disguised in seeming tenderness, fence high the nets

of ruin beyond overleaping? Thus to me

the conflict born of ancient bitterness is not

a thing new thought upon, but pondered deep in time.

I stand now where I struck him down. The thing is done.

Thus have I wrought, and I will not deny it now.

That he might not escape nor beat aside his death,

as fishermen cast their huge circling nets, I spread

deadly abundance of rich robes, and caught him fast.

I struck him twice. In two great cries of agony

he buckled at the knees and fell. When he was down

I struck him the third blow, in thanks and reverence

to Zeus beneath the ground, the prayed-for Savior of the dead.

 

She admits to what she has done, and to her premeditation, as she says that her action was “pondered deep in time.” She says that what she said before, welcoming Agamemnon home, was all a lie so that she could catch him in the robes and kill him. She armed “hate against hateful men,” and this is how vengeance works. Violence is terrible, but you must fight fire with fire, hate with hate. Or so says Clytaemestra.

            She continues: [SLIDE: Ag. 1401-1406]

Agamemnon 1401-1406

You try me out as if I were a woman and vain;

but my heart is not fluttered as I speak before you.

You know it. You can praise or blame me as you wish;

it is all one to me. That man is Agamemnon,

my husband; he is dead; the work of this right hand

that struck in strength of righteousness. And that is that.

 

She points out that she was the one who acted in manly fashion. It was Agamemnon in the end who was womanly and vain, walking on the robes. Clytaemestra was righteous. And who would say she wasn’t? Her husband killed their daughter, went to war for ten years, where he slept with a number of women and brought one back to live in their house. And there was no mechanism for correcting this. She could only welcome him back home and be a good wife to him, or kill him. And she chose to kill him. [SLIDE: Ag. 1560-1566]

 

Agamemnon 1560-1566

Here is anger for anger. Between them

who shall judge lightly?

The spoiler is robbed; he killed, he has paid.

The truth stands ever beside god’s throne

eternal: he who has done shall suffer; that is law.

Then who shall tear the curse from their blood?

The house is glued to ruin.

 

This is the law of vengeance. But of course in Aeschylus’ time the courts were well established, and vengeance was forbidden. The state itself reserved the right to take violent vengeance, and the family or the individual was no longer without recourse. So Aeschylus is justifying through a myth of crime the democratic institution of the courts. The state has, in the common phrase, a monopoly on the legitimate use of force, but that doesn’t just restrict what Clytaemestra can do, it also gives her an option she didn’t have in the mythical time of the Oresteia. And, by the way, women in Athens in the 5th century had the rights, but not the responsibilities of citizens, so they could use the courts, though not argue in them themselves. It was difficult, but not impossible, for women to take advantage of the institutions of the polis. Let’s go back to the trilogy. There’s one last passage of note from the first play. [SLIDE: Ag. 1654-1661]

Agamemnon 1654-1661

No, my dearest, dearest of all men, we have done enough. No more

violence. Here is a monstrous harvest and a bitter reaping time.

There is pain enough already. Let us not be bloody now.

Honored gentlemen of Argos, go to your homes now and give way

to the stress of fate and season. We could not do otherwise

than we did. If this is the end of suffering, we can be content

broken as we are by the brute heel of angry destiny.

Thus a woman speaks among you. Shall men deign to understand?

 

This is Clytaemestra to Aegisthus at the end of the Agamemnon. The chorus is angry and Aegisthus is ready to fight them, but Clytaemestra says no. They should end the violence there. She takes the controlling role, and establishes, or so she thinks, the end.

 

7. The Libation Bearers

            So that was the Agamemnon, in which I’ve highlighted some important themes about justice and vengeance, divine and human, as well as concepts of masculine and feminine and Western and Eastern. Now it’s time to move on to the second play in the trilogy, The Libation Bearers. The play starts when Electra, the daughter of Agamemnon and Clytaemestra and the sister of Orestes, leads a chorus of libation bearers to the grave of Agamemnon, where they will pour libations to placate his vengeful spirit. Or that’s what they’re supposed to do. Instead, Electra and the chorus decide to pray for vengeance against Clytaemestra and Aegisthus. [SLIDE: Lib. 117-122]

The Libation Bearers 117-122

Chorus: Remember, too, the murderers, and against them…

Electra: What shall I say? Guide and instruct my ignorance.

Chorus: Invoke the coming of some man, or more than man.

Electra: To come and judge them, or to give them punishment?

Chorus: Say simply: “one to kill them for the life they took.”

Electra: I can ask this and not be wrong in the gods’ eyes?

Chorus: Of course, to hurt your enemy when he struck first.

 

This opening sets up the theme of vengeance again. No less than in the first play, in the second play we will see how the cycle of violence continues through the generations of this family. The chorus assures Electra that it is not wrong in the gods’ eyes to take vengeance on an enemy who has done violence.

            They explain: [SLIDE: Lib. 400-404]

The Libation Bearers 400-404

It is but law that when red drops have been spilled

upon the ground they cry aloud for fresh

blood. For the death act calls out on Fury

to bring out of those who were slain before

new ruin on ruin accomplished.

 

The chorus here is encouraging blood for blood, or an eye for an eye as we often say today. And they elaborate on this by pointing out that this is an internal matter for the family. [SLIDE: Lib. 466-75]

The Libation Bearers 466-475

O pain grown into the race

and blood-dripping stroke

and grinding cry of disaster,

moaning and impossible weight to bear.

Sickness that fights all remedy.

Here in the house resides

the cure for this, not to be brought

from outside, never from others

but in themselves, through the raw brutal bloodshed.

Here is the song sung to the gods beneath us.

 

The mode of vengeance, as opposed to the mode of state justice, involves only people from within the household taking revenge. The cure for the house is within the house. This is an interesting difference between the ancient and modern justice systems. Later on, one of the chapters of Walklate you’re going to read talks about the criminal justice system in the UK. She writes about the responsibilities of state agencies to enforce the law. In ancient Greece and Rome this isn’t the case. Even after the court system is established to make sure that private acts of revenge are superseded by punishment imposed by a jury, in ancient Greece and Rome there were no police. Moreover, there were no state prosecutors, either. If someone committed a crime, it was the responsibility of the victim or the victim’s family to bring the charges and to ensure that the accused showed up at the trial. It was the responsibility of the victim or the victim’s family to argue the case in court as well. So even though Aeschylus is telling the story of a move from private vengeance to state punishment, it’s not what we would think of as complete. Without public prosecutors and state sponsored police, there is still an element of private vengeance involved.

           

8. Intermission: Review of Major Themes

I want to pause here for a moment to review some of the themes and ideas we’ve set up so far. I’ve argued that the Oresteia is a trilogy about the invention of a criminal justice system. In the mythical time Aeschylus depicts, when someone does something wrong, the only options are to let them get away with it or to punish them yourself. We’ve looked at this in particular in the case of Agamemnon. He killed his daughter, left for ten years, brought home his war captive sex partner, and expected his wife to be okay with all of it. She had no options besides killing him or doing what he wanted. The problem with this, as Aeschylus argues in the plays, is that it means that the cycle of violence continues through the generations. But in the Classical Period, when Aeschylus lived in a democratic city of 120,000 people, the institution of the courts prevented this cycle of vengeance from happening. By taking the place of private revenge, the courts in Athens have the ability to end the cycle by punishing criminals. If there is an overall message to take from this story, it’s that violence within the family can’t be dealt with within the family, but it must be a matter for the polis. I’ve also pointed out a difference between the ancient courts and the modern justice system, and that is that in modern democracies we have state prosecutors and police forces. In the Athenian courts, and we’ll see this is true of Rome as well, there were no police or state prosecutors, which means that to our modern eyes there was still some element of private revenge involved in the ancient justice system.

So far, I’ve looked at a number of passages from the Agamemnon and The Libation Bearers to develop some ideas on crime and punishment in Athens. I argued that in the Oresteia Aeschylus is telling a story of the origin of the democratic institution of the jury trial. He shows us a cycle of domestic violence and its end in an Athenian trial. At the same time he shows a divine movement from vengeance to justice, as the divine justice of Athena and Apollo supersedes the vengeance of the gods of the underworld, including the Furies. Now I’m going to continue to highlight passages from the Oresteia that draw out these themes.

 

9. The Libation Bearers

One of the ideas I’ve mentioned is hospitality. In particular, Paris had not only taken Helen from Menelaus, he did so while he was a guest in Menelaus’ house. The chorus said that he had “trampled down the delicacy of inviolable things” and he would be punished for it. I mentioned that hospitality was extremely important in ancient Greek culture. Whether characters in Greek literature are good or evil often shows up in how well they perform hospitality. In The Libation Bearers, Clytaemestra performs hospitality well:

[SLIDE: Lib. 668-672]

The Libation Bearers 668-672

Friends, tell me only what you would have, and it is yours.

We have all comforts that go with a house like ours,

hot baths, and beds to charm away your weariness

with rest, and the regard of temperate eyes. But if

you have some higher business, more a matter of state,

that is the men’s concern, and I will tell them of it.

 

This is appropriate hospitality. She first welcomes the guests and offers hospitality before even asking who they are. She also sticks to appropriate gender roles, saying that if they have important state business she will get the men rather than trying to discuss it herself. It may be the case that she’s simply lying here and she really doesn’t intend to be a good host and a good woman, but I don’t think so, and it does generate some sympathy for her character. I have argued before that Clytaemestra may be a villain, but her murder of Agamemnon wasn’t entirely unjustified and here she seems to be doing the right thing, even though it means (although she doesn’t know it yet) admitting into her house the man who will kill her. And wow is that a violation of hospitality. It’s like the first rule of good behavior between guests and hosts that they’re not supposed to kill each other. So it’s not so clear to me that Clytaemestra is the villain and Orestes is the hero, here.

Once Orestes has revealed the deception and explained who he is to Clytaemestra, they have an argument. Once again, Clytaemestra presents a reasonable case, and Orestes comes off looking like a jerk: [SLIDE: Lib. 913-23]

The Libation Bearers 913-923

Orestes: No. You bore me and threw me away, to a hard life.

Clytaemestra: I sent you to a friend’s house. This was no throwing away.

O: I was born of a free father. You sold me.

C: So? Where then is the price I received for you?

O: I could say. It would be indecent to tell you.

C: Or if you do, tell also of your father’s follies.

O: Blame him not. He suffered while you were sitting here at   home.

C: It hurts women to be kept from their men, my child.

O: The man’s hard work supports the women who sit at home.

C: I think, child, that you mean to kill your mother.

O: No. It will be you who kill yourself. It will not be I.

 

Orestes first accuses Clytaemestra of sending him away, but she replies that she didn’t send him to a hard life, but to a life of privilege in the royal court of Phocis. And even the fact that Orestes is bringing this up suggests that part of the reason he’s going to kill Clytaemestra is not as punishment for murder, but because he’s angry with her for abandoning him. It’s understandable that he would be upset that his mom put him up for adoption, even if she had the very good reason that she feared for his safety in Argos, but it suggests that his motives are not as pure as he thinks they are. He accuses Clytaemestra of selling him, and goes so far as to say that the price she received would be indecent to say. What he means here is that Clytaemestra chose to cheat on Agamemnon with Aegisthus, and this is what put him in danger and made him have to move to Phocis. The thing that would be indecent to say is that she gave up her son so she could have some sex. But Clytaemestra points out that Agamemnon was hardly innocent on that score, either. She is pointing out the double standard of what’s acceptable sexual behaviour for men and for women, namely that it’s worse for women to cheat. Rather than confronting the issue, Orestes changes tack again, this time arguing that Agamemnon was hard at work fighting a war while Clytaemestra sat at home. And this is another double standard at work. The labor that women do in the house is invisible and unvalued. Orestes says, “The man’s hard work supports the women who sit at home,” as though Clytaemestra hasn’t been running the whole city in her husband’s absence.

            Clytaemestra can see what’s happening, and that she’s not going to win this argument. Orestes isn’t going to listen to reason because he’s working himself up to murder. He’s justifying it in any way he can, ultimately saying that it won’t be his fault or even his own action. He says that Clytaemestra will kill herself, even if Orestes does the stabbing. Orestes is saying that Clytaemestra, by her own actions, has given him no choice but to kill her. But I don’t think he’s got a very strong argument here. He is on the side of his father, saying that Clytaemestra was wrong to kill Agamemnon, but in order to make the argument he has to invoke some unreasonable double standards. Some of you may be thinking that Orestes’ argument would sound more persuasive to an ancient Athenian audience than it does to modern, feminist ears, but I don’t think that’s entirely accurate. Clytaemestra is one among many examples of female voices in ancient Greek literature that expose and lament these kinds of double standards. The Greeks were aware of them and the injustice of them, just as we are today. And that awareness, both in ancient Athens and modern society, has done little to cut the pervasiveness of the double standards. So for an ancient Greek audience as well as a modern one, it’s clear that Orestes is stretching to justify to himself and to his victim the murder he plans to commit.  Unable to take responsibility himself, he blames the victim and accuses her of bringing his violence on herself.

            The confrontation continues, and Orestes stops arguing about right and says that she will die for killing Agamemnon, but importantly he acknowledges that his action is wrong: [SLIDE: Lib. 924-30]

The Libation Bearers 924-930

C: Take care. Your mother’s curse, like dogs, will drag you down.

O: How shall I escape my father’s curse, if I fail here?

C: I feel like one who wastes live tears upon a tomb.

O: Yes, this is death, your wages for my father’s fate.

C: You are the snake I gave birth to, and gave the breast.

O: Indeed, the terror of your dreams saw things to come

clearly. You killed, and it was wrong. Now suffer wrong.

Clytaemestra had a dream in which she was nursing a snake, which bit her and drank blood along with milk. She now realizes that the snake was Orestes, her son who will kill her. There is no longer any deception and both know that Orestes is going to kill his mother. But he explains that this is the cycle of vengeance. Wrong for wrong. He feels compelled. But doesn’t everyone who commits a crime have a reason? It is one of the major themes of Aeschylus’ thought in general that the things that seem to be necessary, the things that seem to be forced on us by outside forces, are actually our own deepest impulses. Orestes clearly feels compelled by necessity to kill his mother, but he is driven by the deep psychological wounds of childhood abandonment as much as by justice. And on some level he knows it. He will return wrong for wrong.

            And he does. But he still believes that he has imposed punishment for a crime. He kills Clytaemestra inside the house and then brings outside the evidence that proves her guilt: [SLIDE: Lib. 980-990]

The Libation Bearers 980-990

Behold again, o audience of these evil things,

the engine against my wretched father they devised,

the hands’ entanglement, the hobbles for his feet.

Spread it out. Stand around me in a circle and

display this net that caught a man. So shall, not my

father, but that great father who sees all, the Sun,

look on my mother’s sacrilegious handiwork

and be a witness for me in my day of trial

how it was in all right that I achieved this death,

my mother’s: for of Aegisthus’ death I take no count:

he has his seducer’s punishment, no more than law.

 

Remember that Clytaemestra was able to overpower Agamemnon by tangling him in the robes she got him to walk on. Orestes here brings out those very robes as evidence of Clytaemestra’s crime, proving to the audience as though it were a jury that he acted rightly. He anticipates that he will undergo a trial for his act, so he retroactively gathers evidence that his killing was justified.

            The most important part of this passage, however, is not the evidence that Clytaemestra is guilty, but rather the easy way in which Orestes dismisses his murder of Aegisthus. “He has his seducer’s punishment, no more than law,” says Orestes. The law Orestes is referring to here is Draco’s law on homicide, which we’re going to look at in a lot more detail in a couple of weeks when we read Lysias. According to Athenian sources, he was the first to put laws in writing, and his most famous and long-lasting law was the one declaring homicide to be illegal. You can’t kill someone, even if they killed a relative of yours. There are a few important exceptions that do allow it, and one of them is that it’s not illegal to kill a man if you find him “lying next to” your wife. When we read Lysias 1, we’re going to see that Lysias tries to spin this exception as a law stating that seduction is punishable by death. This is not actually the case. Seducing another man’s wife was actually punishable by a fine. Presumably, the exception to the usual prohibition against killing someone was there because a man can’t be held responsible for his actions in the heat of the moment when he walks in on another man and his wife in flagrante delicto. But Lysias claims that the law demands that seducers be executed. Orestes, at least half a century before Lysias, makes the same argument, and takes no account of his killing of Aegisthus. And indeed, this is the end of that issue. Orestes’ trial is going to be entirely about the murder of Clytaemestra, and no one will care about the murder of Aegisthus. In this way, Aeschylus seems to buy into Lysias’ argument, which is something we’ll come back to when we look at Lysias.

            The last passage I want to look at from The Libation Bearers is Orestes’ final justification before the Furies appear to chase him off. He knows he’s about to lose his mind with guilt, and he makes the last justification, that Apollo told him to kill his mother: [SLIDE: Lib. 1025-1031]

The Libation Bearers 1025-1031

But while I hold some grip still on my wits, I say publicly

to my friends: I killed my mother not without some right.

My father’s murder stained her, and the gods’ disgust.

As for the spells that charmed me to such daring, I

cite above all the seer of Pytho, Loxias. He

declared I could do this and not be charged with wrong.

 

Apollo will live up to this and defend Orestes at his trial, but the gods are not infallible judges. Remember that Artemis instructed Agamemnon to kill Iphigeneia, and yet he was still responsible for her death and punished for it. Orestes is not right just because Apollo says so. He has to have good reason, and Apollo will make an argument to back up his claim. He doesn’t just say, “Because I said so.” He backs his claim in court. And this is the divine movement from vengeance to justice. No longer do the gods simply say how it is. These new gods, Athena and Apollo, work justice through the court of the Areopagus in Athens.

           

10. The Eumenides

In the trial that takes up most of The Eumenides, the Furies argue that Orestes was wrong to kill Clytaemestra because it is worse to kill a blood relative than it is to kill a spouse. Because Clytaemestra only killed her husband who wasn’t actually a blood relative, but Orestes killed his own mother, the Furies say that he is guilty and deserves to be punished. Apollo responds: [SLIDE: Eum. 213-224]

 

The Eumenides 213-224

You have made into a thing of no account, no place,

the sworn faith of Zeus and Hera, lady

of consummations, and Cypris by such argument

is thrown away, outlawed, and yet the sweetest things

in man’s life come from her, for married love between

man and woman is bigger than oaths, guarded by right

of nature. If when such kill each other you relent

so as not to take vengeance nor eye them in wrath,

then I deny your manhunt of Orestes goes

with right. I see that one cause moves you to strong rage

but on the other clearly you are unmoved to act.

Pallas divine shall review the pleadings of this case.

 

Apollo says that murder is murder, and justice must be even. He argues that if the Furies will prosecute one murderer, they need to prosecute them all. Effectively, he’s pointing out that Orestes had no recourse but to accept his mother’s crime or to punish her himself. Just as we saw that Clytaemestra was constrained by the absence of a justice system, so is Orestes. The Furies would never have prosecuted Clytaemestra, and so Apollo says it’s not fair that they should prosecute Orestes. After all, says Apollo, marriage is a deep and natural bond just as blood is.

            But the Furies still disagree. They argue that violence can’t be undone and must be punished. No fine will do here, and they make explicit that they want to suck his blood to weaken him so that they can take him down to Tartarus where he will pay for his crime eternally with the other bad criminals: [SLIDE: Eum. 261-272]

The Eumenides 261-272

It shall not be. His mother’s blood spilled on the ground

can not come back again.

It is all soaked and drained into the ground and gone.

You must give back for her blood from the living man

red blood of your body to suck, and from your own

I could feed, with bitter-swallowed drench,

turn your strength limp while yet you live and drag you down

where you must pay for the pain of the murdered mother,

and watch the rest of the mortals stained with violence

against god or guest

or hurt parents who were close and dear,

each with the pain upon him that his crime deserves.

 

These are the gods who come from below, and they will feed on Orestes’ blood in exchange for the blood he spilled. They will drag him down not just to the underworld, but to the part of the underworld where sinners suffer punishment. In particular, they will place him among those who did violence against a parent, god, or guest. And note here that husband or wife is not included in that list. Doing violence to a guest—violating hospitality—is worse, according to the Furies, than domestic violence between husband and wife. Orestes, although he didn’t exactly kill a guest, did violate hospitality by killing his host, who, to make matters worse, was his mother.

            While the Furies and Apollo argue, Athena decides that both arguments make some sense and she calls for a jury trial: [SLIDE: Eum. 470-484]

The Eumenides 470-484

The matter is too big for any mortal man

who thinks he can judge it. Nor yet do I have the right

to analyse cases of murder where wrath’s edge

is sharp, and all the more since you have come, and clung

a clean and innocent supplicant, against my doors.

You bring no harm to my city. I respect your rights.

Yet these, too, have their work. We cannot brush them aside,

and if this action so runs that they fail to win,

the venom of their resolution will return

to infect the soil, and sicken all my land to death.

Here is dilemma. Whether I shall let them stay or drive

them off, is a hard course and will hurt. Then, since

the burden of the case is here, and it rests on me,

I shall select judges of manslaughter, and swear

them in, establish a court into all time to come.

 

Athena argues that the case is too ambiguous for her to decide alone, and a jury needs to make a collective decision. She acts as an Athenian magistrate, who in Aeschylus’ time had the authority to make a summary judgment, which is to say the authority to punish a criminal right away if the case was clear enough, or to refer the case to a jury if there was justification for it. She declares here that both sides have rights and reasonable arguments, and the jury must decide: [SLIDE: Eum. 485-489]

The Eumenides 485-489

Litigants, call your witnesses, have ready your proofs

as evidence under bond to keep this case secure.

I will pick the finest of my citizens, and come

back. They shall swear to make no judgment that is not

just, and make clear where in this action the truth lies.

 

The trial looks nicely familiar to us, with the jury swearing to make a just judgment and the litigants ready with proofs and witnesses.

            So the trial happens, but it fails. The jury is tied, and they hadn’t decided previously that a tie would count as an acquittal, so Athena decides to cast the deciding vote, and she gives her reasoning: [SLIDE: Eum. 734-43]

The Eumenides 734-743

It is my task to render final judgment here.

This is a ballot for Orestes I shall cast.

There is no mother anywhere who gave me birth,

and, but for marriage, I am always for the male

with all my heart, and strongly on my father’s side.

So, in a case where the wife has killed her husband, lord

of the house, I shall not value her death more highly than his.

And even if the votes are equal, Orestes is the winner.

 

This is not exactly a great legal argument. It’s a little anticlimactic that the first murder trial in Athens, the one that inaugurates the court for the future, is decided by Athena’s decision that she doesn’t have a mother and so she always decides that the man is right. The Furies and Clytaemestra are women, and Orestes and Apollo are men, so she decides on their side. This is not entirely without mythological and cultural sense, since in Greek myth and culture there is a general association between the natural and the female and the rational and the male. We still have some of this association today, thinking of nature as a mother and logic as a masculine pursuit. It doesn’t make logical sense, but it resonates as a cultural idea. And it makes sense in that way here. The move that’s happening is from a natural desire for vengeance to a rational punishment of criminals to protect a community as a whole. Inasmuch as the feminine is natural and the masculine is logical, this is a move from the feminine to the masculine and Athena, a very masculine goddess, is right to support the male. That’s a pretty tenuous argument, though, and I don’t think it holds up all that well. It seems to me to be a poor reason for a legal decision, and it highlights how much the prejudices of the jurors are going to play a part no matter how much they swear to be impartial.

            Nevertheless, it’s the decision Athena gives. Orestes is acquitted and the trial is over. Athena is then faced with the problem of having a crowd of angry Furies in Athens. She resolves the problem by giving them a home, calling them the ‘Kindly Ones’, the Eumenides, and channeling their anger in what she considers a productive way. This is the last major point the Oresteia has to make on the nature of violent crime—that there is a difference between crime and war: [SLIDE: Eum. 858-866]

The Eumenides 858-866

Only in this place that I haunt do not inflict

your bloody stimulus to twist the inward hearts

of young men, raging in a fury not of wine,

nor, as if plucking the heart from fighting cocks,

engraft among my citizens that spirit of war

that turns their battle fury inward on themselves.

No, let our wars range outward—may they range full fierce

and terrible, for those desiring high renown.

No true fighter I call the bird that fights at home.

 

What Athena is saying here is that it is right to direct violence outward, but not inward. Violence in war against external enemies is good, but violence against members of your own community is wrong. It’s criminal. That means that a key idea in the ancient idea of the criminal is the distinction between insiders and outsiders. Who counts as a member of the community? It’s not always as straightforward as it seems. What about immigrants, for example? Can they be victims of crime? What about enslaved people? Is it only Athenian citizens who deserve the protection of the courts? Aeschylus doesn’t tackle any of these questions. What he does is make a necessary distinction between internal violence, which is criminal, and external violence, which is warfare.

            The chorus, no longer Furies, but now Eumenides, express their hopes for the future of Athens: [SLIDE: Eum. 976-986]

The Eumenides 976-986

This is my prayer: civil war

fattening on men’s ruin shall

not thunder in our city. Let

not the dry dust that drinks

the black blood of citizens

through passion for revenge

and bloodshed for bloodshed

be given our state to prey upon.

Let them render grace for grace.

Let love be their common will;

let them hate with single heart.

Much wrong in the world is thereby healed.

It sounds so counterintuitive that hate is a positive value, but Athena has harnessed the hateful power of the Furies for Athens. The Furies are kindly toward Athenians, and they direct Athenian hatred outward against foreign enemies. Remember here that Aeschylus was a war veteran. He presented his first tragedy in 499, fought at Marathon in 490, won his first competition at the City Dionysia in 484, and probably fought at Salamis in 480. The beginning of his writing career was during the Persian Wars, and the victories for the Greeks in general and Athens in particular made Athens very powerful. Violence is a part of his life, and here near the end of his career and close to the end of his life he makes a powerful distinction between war and homicide. Crime is individual and within the community, but warfare is collective and directed outward. Hate can be a positive value, if it’s directed appropriately.

So the Oresteia as a whole does not condemn violence and killing in general, but condemns violence and killing by an individual within the community. This is connected to the relationship between the oikos and the polis. In the Oresteia violence directed inward is within the oikos—within the house of Atreus. The Oresteia says that it’s not just domestic violence that’s criminal, but in the end all violence within the polis is criminal. The courts take what was an internal matter for the family and declare it to be public, in the process inventing the criminal.
