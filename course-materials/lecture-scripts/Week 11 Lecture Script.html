2. Introduction: Who counts as a terrorist?

The concept of terrorism isn’t new, but it seems like the 21st century has seen more than its fair share of ‘wars against terrorism’ or claims that one group or another are ‘terrorists’, depending on one’s political position. This is all the more the case in recent years when rhetoric and social media erodes the concept of objective truth and amplifies societal disunity. One of the arguments made by modern politicians regarding accusations of terrorism is that it’s one of the only crimes where it is too dangerous to wait until there is hard evidence; instead the wellbeing and safety of the community depends on stopping the crime before it happens. As we look back at ancient Rome, in the late republic, there is a similar ideology at work. The events we study this week begin with promises of revolution, with what could be called a terrorist conspiracy to destroy the city of Rome, with undercover operations to catch the would-be terrorists, and vehement arguments about what should be done with those caught up in the plotting. The year is 63 BCE, and on one side there is Lucius Sergius Catilina (whom we call Catiline in English), a politically frustrated, bankrupt aristocrat and the architect of an alleged plot to assassinate Rome’s elected officials and burn the city to the ground. He campaigned on a platform of erasing all debts of rich and poor people alike (not least his own!). On the other side is Marcus Tullius Cicero (just Cicero from now on), the famous orator, lawyer, politician, philosopher and statesman, who just happened to be the chief magistrate (consul) in Rome at the time, and who never (and I mean never!) failed to boast of how he saved the state by uncovering Catiline’s plot and thus saving Rome from utter destruction. (sorry, spoiler alert! – Dr. Nousek)

Significantly, we see in our reading in Sallust some of the arguments, controversies, and wider questions raised by this crisis at this particular time in Roman history. As we’ll see quick (and irreversible) decisions were made by Cicero and the Senate regarding the alleged conspirators—they were executed summarily, I mean—that bring up questions that we grapple with today: is it legitimate to eliminate so-called ‘terrorists’ outside the due process of law? How far should individual civil rights be subordinated to the greater good of ‘homeland security’? Can people be punished for merely plotting to overthrow the state but not acting on it? Recent modern history shows us several models for answering these questions, and I will leave it to you to ponder those. For now we’ll take ourselves back to the first century BCE, to a time when the lines between criminals and enemy combatants were far from clear.  

 

3. The Five Things:

This lecture is about the Roman writer Sallust and his history of Catiline’s War. [slide #2, 3] First, just to make sure we get to them, here are the Five Things. The author is Gaius Sallustius Crispus, or Sallust, who was a Roman politician and supporter of Julius Caesar. He lived (probably) from 86-35 BCE. He was a successful governor and general in the Roman province of Africa but retired from politics amid scandal and began to write history in 44 BCE. Title: We don’t know what Sallust called his history of the Catilinarian conspiracy, but from early on it was known in Latin as the Bellum Catilinae, which Woodman translates as Catiline’s War. Date: Catiline’s War was the first work Sallust wrote in his retirement, and we can narrow down the dates to sometime between 44 and 40 BCE. Location: Sallust lived in Rome, although he had a villa at Tivoli (just outside of the city) where he spent a lot of time in his retirement. Language: Sallust wrote in Latin. So, the five things for this text are as follows:

Author: Sallust

Title: Catiline’s War

Date: 44-40 BCE

Location: Rome

Language: Latin.

 

4. Historical Background to the Conspiracy

[slide #4] Let me first give you some historical and political background. The story of Catiline and his conspiracy begins in the Late Republic, specifically in the 60s BCE. There was political turmoil in Rome over a variety of issues, but there were two main factions in the senate. The so-called optimates, which means “the best men” in Latin, were social and economic conservatives who believed that the best hope for resolving the problems lay in shoring up the power of the wealthy, elite senators. Since senators already wielded the most power in Roman politics, the optimates were the senatorial faction who supported maintaining the status quo as much as possible. The other faction was the populares, who were more interested in gaining power by appealing to the masses. The populares weren’t exactly populist or radical, though. Both factions were made up of senators, or politically active wealthy elite men of Roman society. It was more a difference in attitude and especially in political tactics. There was, for example, a debt crisis at this time, and optimate politicians would campaign on the policy that there should be no debt relief and that people ought to be made to pay their loans no matter what the terms were. Populares, on the other hand, were campaigning on the policy that debt should be restructured and in certain radical instances that debts should simply be cancelled. Some of the important optimates were Marcus Porcius Cato and Marcus Tullius Cicero. Some of the important populares were Gaius Julius Caesar (that’s the Julius Caesar) and Lucius Sergius Catilina, better known to us simply as Catiline.

[slide #5] Catiline was a noble patrician (i.e., an aristocrat with a long family history of political success) and a popularis. He was both an excellent general and a charismatic speaker. He was one of the most radical proponents of debt reform, advocating simply abolishing debts—not coincidentally, perhaps, because he himself was massively in debt. As it happens, the year of the conspiracy Catiline was involved in is the single year in all of antiquity for which we have the most detailed information, from multiple sources. So the chronology of the conspiracy is both well-known and important, and we can follow the events in an extremely detailed way: in some cases we know what happened down to the hour. Here’s what we know.

[slide #6] In 64 BCE Catiline ran unsuccessfully for the consulship of 63. The two consuls elected that year were Cicero and Gaius Antonius Hybrida. Catiline ran again in 63 for the consulship of 62. His platform was designed to appeal to the masses; it called for the cancellation of everyone’s debts and the redistribution of land to allow more people access to generational wealth. [slide #7] Cicero, as consul in 63, was presiding over the elections for the next year. According to Sallust, Catiline had been plotting revolution even before he first ran for the consulship in 64, and before the election in 63 he had a secret meeting with his supporters in which he threatened to assassinate Cicero and take over the city. This may or may not be historically accurate, but it seems to modern historians a lot more likely that Catiline, who was after all a successful and popular politician, had legitimate hopes of winning the consulship and solving his problems until after he lost the election in 63. But Cicero, who was Catiline’s most vehement opponent, accused Catiline of trying to assassinate him before the election of 63. Cicero showed up at the consular election wearing an armored breastplate over his toga. This would be akin to showing up at an election nowadays wearing a Kevlar vest: it was a showy bit of political theatre and no doubt caused the voters to be alarmed. Indeed, it’s possible that at least in part because of Cicero’s theatrics, Catiline lost the election. After two attempts at the consulship, one in which he lost the election to Cicero, and one in which Cicero presided over the elections, Catiline started to get really desperate. One of the reasons for his desperation was that electoral campaigning was expensive, and candidates who were successful could expect to recover their expenses (and more) only by securing a military command or provincial governorship after their year in office (political offices in Rome did not have salaries). Since he had failed two years in a row, Catiline’s debts were piling up. Desperate times called for desperate measures and it was looking likely that the only solution was a violent one. The elections in 63 were in July, so by late summer Catiline was getting very serious in his plans to overthrow the government.

[slide #8] Let’s take a step back. How would someone like Catiline even attempt to overthrow the government? Where would he get men to support him in a violent coup? Well, about a generation before these elections, there were two important military-political figures in Rome: Gaius Marius and Lucius Cornelius Sulla. These guys were huge rivals. Marius was popular with the masses, and Sulla was more popular among the elite. The two of them and their supporters fought a series of civil wars that finally ended in 82 BCE when Sulla became dictator. Their conflict is significant for us in the 60s BCE because Roman generals like Sulla and Marius had armies made up of soldiers who were personally loyal to them, and we call their armies “client armies” because the generals were patrons and the soldiers were clients (recall that you learned a little about the patron-client system in Rome in the lecture about Roman Law – Dr. Nousek). In a civil war like that fought between Marius and Sulla, the soldiers could count on post-war rewards only if their side won and their general secured power in Rome: this made them fiercely loyal to their general. Sulla was victorious in those wars and he rewarded his veterans by giving them land in Italy not far from Rome. This was good farmland, and so it was great for the soldiers but Sulla got it by evicting the farmers who were already there, which was highly problematic. First, the evicted farmers were unhappy and didn’t have anywhere to go or another means of supporting themselves and their families. Second, Sulla’s veterans were often not especially great at farming (plus it was a lot more tedious and boring to be a farmer than a soldier!) and over the next decade or two the whole region became dissatisfied. Fast forward to the late 60s, when Catiline was losing elections and starting to conspire against the government: the situation in Etruria (in central-northern Italy) was bad. Catiline did not have a client army like Sulla or Marius had had in the 80s, but he did have clients placed around Italy campaigning for him, most notably a fellow called Gaius Manlius, who was in Etruria where there were a large number of recently evicted farmers and unhappy war veterans. These guys, especially the Sullan veterans, still had weapons and were motivated to take action towards pretty radical solutions to their problems.

[slide #9] Meanwhile, back in Rome, after the election of July 63 (discussed above), Cicero again accused Catiline of an assassination plot. And here is where we get to the heart of the conspiracy that Catiline is known for. On October 20 some powerful senators gave to Cicero anonymous letters that revealed the conspiracy. On October 21, the senate passed the so-called senatus consultum ultimum (also known as the SCU). In the Roman Law lecture you learned about senatus consulta, or ‘decisions of the senate’. There was a special one of these called the senatus consultum ultimum, or the ‘ultimate decision of the senate’, which advised the consuls to take extraordinary measures to ensure the security of the state. Basically, it was the senate’s grant of emergency powers to the consuls, not unlike a modern declaration of martial law. Keep in mind, though, that the senate is formally only advisory, with no official legal standing, and the consul was responsible for any actions he took, even in this emergency situation. The senate can advise the consuls, for example, to declare a state of emergency and suspend the normal rights of the citizens, but if the consuls do that, they can still later be prosecuted for their actions during the emergency. So when the senate passed the senatus consultum ultimum on October 21, they effectively asked the consuls to take over and deal with Catiline.

[slide #10] On October 27, Manlius raised a revolt in Etruria. The plan was that in Rome Catiline and his co-conspirators would assassinate Cicero and as many other important politicians as possible. They would incite riots and set fires in the city, and with the leading politicians dead, Manlius would march on Rome and Catiline would take over the city. In early November, Catiline was charged with vis, or political violence, but the trial would never take place. On the morning of November 7, there was an attempted assassination of Cicero, but it failed. [slide #11] On the next day, November 8, Cicero convened the senate and delivered a scathing speech. We have a copy of the speech, and it’s really impressive; it’s one of the most famous denunciations in all of history. In fact, the opening lines of the speech have survived into modern political parlance (see the end of this lecture for examples). Catiline, who was a senator, was present at the meeting and reacted haughtily, calling the accusations all lies. This is the famous meeting of the senate from the picture on the cover slides on the slide deck, where Cicero is speaking and Catiline is sitting all by himself without any supporters.[1] The senate sided with Cicero and Catiline, seeing which way the political winds were blowing, left the city to join Manlius in Etruria.

With Catiline out of the city, Cicero intensified the attacks on him in the senate, getting him declared a hostis, or enemy combatant, in mid-November. This was a serious action, for now Catiline was officially an enemy of the state. [slide #12] On December 3 some envoys of the Allobroges (a tribe of Gauls) produced written evidence of the conspiracy and five of the conspirators, including high-ranking politicians by the name of Publius Cornelius Lentulus Sura and Gaius Cornelius Cethegus were arrested. [slide #13] On December 5, in a highly controversial action, the senate voted to execute the captured conspirators immediately. Rmember, though, that the senate could only advise the consuls. There had not been a trial and the conspirators, as Roman citizens, still had the right of provocatio ad populum. Although the senate had passed the senatus consultum ultimum and advised Cicero to take extraordinary measures, and they specifically advised him to order the execution of the conspirators without trial, it was still Cicero who was responsible for giving the order. In fact, a few years after this (in 58 BCE), a tribune of the plebs named Publius Clodius Pulcher proposed a law aimed specifically at those who had executed Roman citizens without trial, and Cicero (now himself understanding the direction of the political winds) fled into exile. He was recalled from exile in 57, but it was still a very risky decision on Cicero’s part to order the executions, even though he argued that he was protected by the senatus consultum ultimum. In any case, after the executions, the senate sent the other consul Gaius Antonius Hybrida to fight Catiline’s army in Etruria, and in January of 62 BCE Catiline died in combat and his army was destroyed.

 

5. Sallust and Catiline’s War

With all of that as background and historical context, we can now look more closely at Sallust’s account of the conspiracy (or war!), especially from the standpoint of what we have learned so far about crime and criminality—both modern and ancient—and the ways in which modern criminologists approach their study of crime and criminal behaviour. One of the main questions we are investigating is already inherent in the way we think of Sallust’s history of the events: is it a conspiracy (and so Catiline would be a criminal) or is it a war (in which case he would be an enemy combatant). At the same time, we are looking for evidence that Romans, or at least Sallust, saw criminal behaviour in a similar way that criminologists do. So let’s do our usual thing and take a look at some parts of the readings that especially bring out these themes. The first passage I want to look at is Sallust’s description of Catiline on page 5 [slide #14]:

 

 

 

L. Catilina, born of a noble line, had great strength of both mind and body, but a wicked and crooked disposition. From adolescence, internal wars, slaughter, seizures and civil disharmony were welcome to him, and there he spent his young manhood. His body was tolerant of hunger, cold and wakefulness beyond the point which anyone finds credible; his mind was daring, cunning and versatile, capable of any simulation and dissimulation; acquisitive of another’s property, prodigal with his own; burning in desires; his eloquence was adequate, scant his wisdom. The enormity of his mind always desired the unrestrained, the incredible, the heights beyond reach. (5)

 

One important thing to note about this passage is that it depicts Catiline as always having been a bad guy. Even from his adolescence, he wanted civil war and slaughter. I don’t think this is probably true, but Sallust here wants to present Catiline as an evil character. It seems much more likely that Catiline was at least in large part driven to war by his desperation and became criminal, rather than always having been a criminal character. But it was the case in ancient Rome (and is still somewhat the case now) that we want to identify the characteristics of people that make them criminal. In other words, criminology often focuses on criminals rather than on crimes. It’s less about the acts that make people criminal than it is about the characteristics of the criminals. This is especially true of positivist criminology, whether biological, psychological, or sociological, and it seems to be true for Sallust as well.

It’s also important to note in this passage, though, that even though Catiline is evil, he’s also kind of awesome. He’s definitely the protagonist of this story. As it happens, Cicero really wanted someone to write a history of the affair that focused on him and how he saved the republic, but instead Sallust wrote a history that focuses on Catiline (take that, Cicero! –Dr. Nousek). Sallust makes Catiline incredibly smart and daring and even though he uses his powers for evil, he still possesses many of the greatest of Roman virtues. So even though he was naturally corrupt even from a young age, he still had good qualities somewhere. To some extent, it was the historical and political circumstances that pushed him into criminality.

The first of those historical circumstances was the precedent of Sulla’s civil wars and dictatorship (discussed briefly above). Sallust says on pages 5-6 [slide #15]:

After the dominion of L. Sulla, he had been assailed by his greatest urge, to capture the commonwealth; and he attached no weight to the methods by which he might achieve it, provided he acquired kingship for himself. His defiant spirit was exercised increasingly each day by his lack of private assets and a consciousness of his crimes, both of which he had augmented by the qualities which I recalled above. He was incited, too, by the community’s corrupt morals, which were afflicted by those worst and mutually different maladies, luxury and avarice. (6)

 

Sallust says that Catiline was influenced by the example of Sulla, that his debt (remember especially the debt from his failed campaigns for the consulship) drove him more and more as time went on, and that the community around him also corrupted him. So on the one hand Sallust is presenting Catiline as natural criminal from a young age (which might be a kind of biological or psychological positivism), but on the other hand he also points out the importance of Catiline’s environment and community (which is more sociologically positivist). There’s one more reason Catiline went bad, and that’s on pages 11-12 [slide #16]:

Finally he was captivated by love for Aurelia Orestilla (in whom no good man ever praised anything but her appearance), but, because she hesitated to marry him through fear of a stepson of adult years, it is believed for certain that he killed his son, thereby ensuring an empty house for the criminal marriage. It is this affair above all which seems to me to have been his reason for speeding up the deed: for his vile spirit - hostile to gods and men - could not be calmed by wakefulness or repose: to such an extent was his conscience preying upon his unquiet mind. Hence his bloodless complexion (11) and ugly eyes, and his walk alternating between fast and slow; in short, there was derangement in his demeanour and face. (12)

 

Isn’t this passage terrific? The image of Catiline wracked with guilt over killing his son, looking pale, with bloodshot eyes, and unable to walk normally, is a wonderfully compelling portrait: too bad it’s not true (there is no historical evidence). However, the passage is a great example of a psychological explanation of crime, as Catiline’s feelings of guilt produce physical symptoms and drive him on toward further crimes.

So what do we make of all this? In his description of Catiline, Sallust is trying to find explanations for why someone like Catiline would turn to the kinds of crime he committed. He says that Catiline was really smart, had great endurance, and was charismatic. One reason is that Catiline was always rotten. Even from a young age he was delighted by evil. Another reason that Sallust proposes is sociological, that is that Catiline’s social environment was corrupt and failed to restrain his criminal impulses. Finally, there’s a psychological element in his guilt over his first major crime, the murder of his son. But in addition to providing these explanations in his description of Catiline, Sallust also provides Catiline’s own explanations for his turn to crime. On page 15 Catiline gives a speech to his supporters [slide #17]:

‘You have already heard before, separately, what I have pondered in my mind. Yet my spirit is kindled more and more each day when I reflect what the conditions of life will be if we do not assert our freedom ourselves. For, ever since the commonwealth passed to the jurisdiction of a powerful few, it has always been to them that the dues of kings and tetrarchs go, that the taxes of peoples and nations are paid; the rest of us- all the committed and good, noble and ignoble - have been simply “the masses”, denied favour, denied influence, beholden to those to whom, if the commonwealth thrived, we would be a source of fear. Hence all favour, power, honour and riches rest with them or are where they want them; to us they have left the dangers, rejections, lawsuits and destitution. (15)

 

So, we have a new dimension in these explanations. Although Sallust’s description of Catiline looked positivist to me, Catiline’s own explanation looks a lot like Relative Deprivation theory. He feels that a powerful few have taken from the masses, including Catiline, what rightfully belonged to them. It’s not just about money, but also about power and honour. Catiline feels unjustly deprived and he will do whatever it takes to correct that.

So much for the initial description of Catiline and the explanation of his turn to crime. Another way this text teaches us about crime and punishment in ancient Rome is by discussing how the Romans dealt with the conspiracy. On page 20 we learn about the senatus consultum ultimum [slide #18]:

He [Cicero] therefore referred the matter, already discussed beforehand in rumours amongst the public, to the senate. And so, as often happens in the case of some frightening business, the senate decreed that the consuls should do their utmost ‘to prevent the commonwealth from suffering any damage’. That is the greatest power allowed to a magistracy by the senate according to Roman custom: to prepare an army, to wage war, to coerce allies and citizens by every means, and to wield the highest command and jurisdiction at home and on campaign. (Otherwise a consul has the right to none of these things without an order from the people.) (20)

 

This is a good description of the powers granted by the SCU. Normally a consul is limited by provocatio, but, when empowered by the SCU, he can coerce citizens by every means, and he can wield the highest command, that is imperium, not only on military campaign but also at home in the city. When in 58 Cicero was trying to avoid exile for executing the conspirators without trial, he argued that the SCU empowered him to do that, but the counterargument was that the SCU is advisory, and the responsibility for the actions still resides in the consul who ordered them. And Cicero did go into exile (though he was later allowed back into Rome). The legal authority of the senate and the SCU was still in flux and not entirely clear even to the Romans of the time.

There was another complication with the implementation of the SCU: the timeline. Even though the senate had passed the SCU in October, when Cicero delivered the so-called ‘First Catilinarian Oration’ before the senate in November, Catiline was still present in the city and at the meeting. And so Catiline at least still thought there was some hope that he would be thought innocent even after the passage of the SCU. On page 22 Sallust shows him offering a defense to the senate [slide #19]:

But when he had sat down, Catiline, prepared as he was to dissemble everything, with face downcast and suppliant voice began to demand of the fathers that they should not believe rashly anything concerning him: he was sprung from such a family, and he had regulated his life from adolescence in such a way, that he had good prospects in every respect; they should not reckon that, as a patrician whose own and whose ancestors’ benefits to the Roman plebs were very numerous, he needed the destruction of the commonwealth--when it was being safeguarded by M. Tullius, an immigrant citizen of the City of Rome. (22)

 

Catiline’s strategy before the senate, in spite of the evidence against him and the consul’s opposition to him, was that he was from a good, Roman, noble family, and Cicero was a novus homo, which is Latin for “new man.” The term means specifically the first in a family to hold the consulship, although sometimes Romans seem to have used it more generally for the first in a family to be a senator. Cicero’s family was not from the city of Rome, but from a town nearby, and even though he was a citizen, Catiline thinks he can present Cicero as an outsider to the group of senators. In fact, Catiline was able to avoid prosecution for a long time precisely because he had support from many consular, that is, insider, families, who were motivated to protect one of their own. If he can present himself as an insider and Cicero as an outsider, then , he must have thought, he stands a chance of turning the tables and discrediting Cicero.

Catiline’s strategy didn’t work, and the senate sided with Cicero, so Catiline fled the city and joined up with Manlius. On page 24, the senate declares him a hostis, meaning an enemy combatant [slide #20]:

Nevertheless he himself delayed for a few days at the house of C. Flaminius in the territory of Arretium, while he furnished arms to a neighbourhood which was already galvanized; then, with the fasces and other insignia of command, he marched to Manlius in his camp. When this was found out at Rome, the senate pronounced Catiline and Manlius enemies and appointed a day before which the rest of their crowd (apart from those condemned on capital charges) could put down their arms with impunity. Apart from that, it decreed that the consuls should hold a levy, that Antonius with an army should speedily pursue Catiline, and that Cicero should act as defender of the City. (24)

 

This is a really interesting passage for the status of Catiline, because it brings up the difference between criminals and enemy combatants. On the one hand, citizens who have committed crimes have rights, like the right of provocatio, which is effectively the right to a trial. Enemy combatants, on the other hand, do not have rights and are subject to violence. They can be killed with impunity, and it’s an act of war, not of punishment. Sometimes, the distinction can be fuzzy, as is the case with the US so-called “war on terror” and many other dubious military actions. By calling it a war and by naming people enemy combatants, the US government feels justified in suspending many of the rights of prisoners. But there was, and still is, a lot of public outcry about that. Here too in Rome in 63 BCE, there’s a certain fuzziness to the distinction between criminals and enemy combatants. To be sure, Catiline and Manlius have taken up arms and are leading an army that’s hostile to Cicero and Antonius and the current senate, but they’re still Roman, and so from a certain point of view they’re criminals rather than enemies.

The fuzziness of their status is reflected in the title of this text. Sometimes it’s called The Conspiracy of Catiline, or The Catilinarian Conspiracy, but in Latin it’s usually called the Bellum Catilinae, which means Catiline’s War. There’s a difference between conspiracy and war. If Catiline is the leader of a conspiracy, he’s a criminal, but if it’s a war, he’s an enemy. The uncertainty of the distinction is enhanced by the support that Catiline had from many Romans in the city. Sallust says on pages 24-25 [slide #21]:

Such was the violence of the disease and the kind of rottenness which had attacked many of the citizens’ spirits. And not only was there the mental derangement of those who were accessories to the conspiracy, but the entire plebs, in its enthusiasm for revolution, approved completely of Catiline’s projects. That, indeed, it seemed to do from its own particular habit. For it is always those in a community who have no resources who resent the good and extol the wicked, who hate what is old and crave what is new, who in their hatred of their own circumstances are enthusiastic for everything to be changed, and who thrive on disruption and sedition with no concern, since destitution is a possession easily held without loss. But, in the case of the City plebs, there were many reasons for its headlong plight. (24-25)

 

Here Sallust points out that the common people of the city, the plebs, fully supported Catiline. They did this because people who have little are always in favor of revolution, he argues. Like Catiline’s earlier speech, this looks a lot like Relative Deprivation Theory, although it’s perhaps a little more absolute than relative, in that the common people support Catiline because they have almost nothing to lose and much to gain. In that way it has a little Rational Choice Theory in it, too, in spite of Sallust’s characterization of the plebs as mentally deranged. But the main point about this passage is that even though Catiline is an enemy according to the senate, it seems that he still had a broad base of support among the Roman People. And so we can ask ourselves, when is an enemy not an enemy, but a hero fighting against the status quo?

Furthermore, there’s a passage that exposes the senate’s decisions as being pretty arbitrary. On pages 31 and 32 Sallust says [slide #22]:

But, when Tarquinius named Crassus, a noble man of the greatest riches and the utmost power, everyone cried that the informant was false and demanded a motion concerning the matter—some deeming the matter incredible, others because, though they reckoned it true, it seemed that at such a moment so strong a man should be mollified rather than provoked, while very many of them were beholden to Crassus through personal business. And so a crowded senate, when consulted by Cicero, decreed that Tarquinius’ information seemed false and he should be kept in chains and given no further opportunity of informing except concerning the person whose scheme it had been that he should lie about so great a matter. (Pages 31-32)

 

So here someone accuses Crassus, the richest man in Rome, and a very powerful senator, of being involved in the conspiracy. But Crassus is untouchable: he’s too powerful to fall. Some of the senators don’t think the accusation is true, but even those who do think it’s true won’t convict Crassus because they’re too beholden to him—he’s too wealthy and powerful. Instead they put the accuser Tarquinius in chains for making a false accusation. Crassus is so important that he’s almost automatically defined as being the good guy, and anyone who accuses him must therefore be a bad guy.

 

6. Criminal or Enemy Combatant?

In any case, after the business with Crassus is done, the senate started debating what to do about the conspirators that had been captured in the city. For the time being, they were being held under house arrest, but a more permanent solution was needed. Although many senators spoke at the meeting, Sallust singles out two speeches as the most persuasive, and says that everyone agreed first with the one speech and then the other. The first speech was delivered by a young, up-and-coming senator named Gaius Julius Caesar, who would later fight the civil war against Pompey and become the dictator. He says, on page 36 [slide #23]:

 

 

 

The Lacedaemonians imposed on the defeated Athenians thirty men to handle their commonwealth. At first they began to execute, without trial, all the worst individuals and those resented by all: the people were delighted and said it was deserved. But after, when their license had gradually increased, they killed good and bad indifferently at whim and terrified the rest with dread. So a community which had been oppressed by slavery paid a heavy penalty for its foolish delight. (36)

 

Caesar uses the example of the Thirty Tyrants (whom you’ll remember from Classical Athens). He says that they executed criminals without trial, and at first it was all fine because those guys were clearly bad, but soon it spiraled out of control and there was killing for no justifiable reason. Caesar isn’t arguing that the conspirators deserve to live, but instead he’s making a so-called slippery slope argument. Once you start executing the worst people, you’re probably going to start executing people who don’t deserve it too.

Caesar also points to the history of Roman law, and argues that although Romans of earlier generations, following the older Greek practice, used to have the death penalty, it was now standard practice not to execute citizens. This is on page 37 [slide #24]:

Yet at that very same time, in imitation of Greek custom, they chastised citizens with lashes and exacted the ultimate reprisal from the condemned. But, after the commonwealth had matured and the number of citizens led to thriving factions and the innocent began to be entrapped and other things of this type to take place, then the Porcian Law and other laws were provided, laws by which exile was permitted to the condemned. This I think is, an especially good reason, conscript fathers, for our not adopting a new counsel. Naturally those who created so great an empire from small resources had better prowess and wisdom than there is in us, who scarcely retain what has been so well acquired. (37)

 

The earlier Romans, says Caesar, saw that Roman politicians were using the law courts to settle personal feuds and have their personal and political opponents killed, and so they stopped executing citizens and instead permitted them exile. Rome had eliminated the death penalty for citizens. And those Romans, the ones who made that decision, were great because they built up the Roman empire. So surely they must have been wise.

So Caesar has said that the conspirators should not be executed, and then he needs to propose what should be done instead. On page 37 he says [slide #25]:

‘Should they therefore be discharged and Catiline’s army be increased? Not at all. But I do propose as follows: that their money should be confiscated and they themselves held in chains in the municipalities which have the most effective resources; that no one afterwards should bring a motion before the senate concerning them nor discuss them in front of the people; and that the senate thinks that whoever acts otherwise shall be acting against the commonwealth and the welfare of all.’ (37)

 

Caesar’s plan is that the conspirators should be moved out of the city and should be held prisoner in houses in the municipalities outside of the city. This is really a novel idea. I’ve said before that there was no long-term imprisonment used as a punishment in antiquity, and that this is one of the major differences between ancient and modern punishment. That’s true, but Caesar seems to be suggesting it here. It would have been a really radical suggestion at the time, but even so the senate seems to have found the speech persuasive. Sallust says that everyone was in agreement with Caesar for a while. But then another speaker persuaded them all otherwise.

The speaker who turned everyone against Caesar was another up-and-coming senator named Marcus Porcius Cato. We know him as Cato, or sometimes Cato the Younger. He starts on page 37 [slide #26]:

‘Far different is my inclination, conscript fathers [that means “senators”], when I contemplate the circumstances of our dangers and when I think over to myself the opinions of several of the members. They seem to me to have spoken about the punishment of men who prepared war against their own fatherland, parents, altars and hearths; but circumstances suggest that we should be wary of those men rather than that we should be deliberating what decision to take against them. Other misdeeds you pursue only when they have already been done but, unless you see to it that this one does not befall us, you will invoke the courts in vain when it has happened: when a city has been captured, the defeated have nothing left. (37)

 

Cato is arguing that Caesar is treating the conspirators as regular criminals, but that the kind of crime they engage in must be prevented rather than punished. He says that the conspirators are bringing war against Rome, and unless they are dealt with immediately they threaten to overthrow the state. If they succeed in taking over, then they’ll just define themselves as non-criminals and they’ll never be punished. So even though the conspiracy hasn’t actually been carried out and there hasn’t actually been any damage, Cato says that the conspirators must be punished anyway. This, he argues, will send a message to the army that’s marching on the city. This is on page 39 [slide #27]:

For that reason, when you decide about P. Lentulus and the others, be assured that at the same time you are issuing a decree about Catiline’s army and about all the conspirators. The more attentively you conduct these matters, the weaker the spirit in those quarters will be; but, if they see you wilt only a very little, all of them will soon make their defiant presence felt. (39)

 

Here Cato makes a kind of deterrence argument. He says that if you deal harshly with the captured conspirators, you will weaken the spirit of their allies. This is the kind of argument people often make in favor of the death penalty: If you kill some very bad criminals, then others will be less likely to commit those crimes.

But that’s not all that Cato argues. He continues his speech, and now he invokes gender stereotypes. He encourages the Romans to be manly and take action and not to be weak and womanly. He’s drumming up support for execution, and that kind of violence can be a tough sell, especially when there’s a shaky premise for executing high-ranking Roman citizens without trial. War, says Cato, is manly business and requires a strong constitution: pages 39-40 [slides #28, 29]:

‘Citizens of the greatest nobility have conspired to burn their fatherland; they summon the Gauls, a people most hostile to the name of Rome, to war; the enemy leader and his army are poised over our heads: do you, then, still hesitate and doubt what to do with the enemies caught inside the walls? I propose that you take pity on them - mere young men who did wrong through ambition and discharge them still armed! Do not let your mercy and pity make you pitiable yourselves, should they take to arms! “Certainly the issue itself is harsh”, you say, but you “do not fear it.” [slide #29] Yet you do, and very greatly; but through idleness and soft-heartedness you hesitate, one waiting for another, evidently trusting in the immortal gods, who have often saved this commonwealth in its greatest dangers. Yet it is not by prayers and womanly supplications that the help of the gods is acquired; it is by watchfulness, action and good deliberation that everything ends successfully. When you submit to lethargy and apathy, you invoke the gods in vain: they are angry and hostile. (39-40)

 

So Cato presents the conspirators as enemies, even though they have not been declared enemy combatants. He says that in war you have to take manly action, and that demands that you kill them. He passes over the fact that there has been no trial, because if he can convince the senators that this is war, then no trial is necessary. And it works. The senate overwhelmingly votes with Cato and they advise Cicero to execute the conspirators. They send the other consul Antonius with an army to go fight Catiline’s army. Cato succeeds in getting the senators and the consuls to treat this as a war rather than as a criminal conspiracy. And so Catiline isn’t a criminal, but an enemy combatant.

After the senate votes and Cicero orders the conspirators executed, all that remains is for the armies to meet. As at the beginning of the text, we get an image of Catiline at the end that is contradictory. Yes, he’s evil and he’s an enemy, but he also has the virtues of a good Roman hero. On page 46 he’s a good soldier and general [slide #30]:

Meanwhile Catiline was active in the front line with his unencumbered troops: he helped the flagging, summoned the fit to take over from the injured, made every provision, fought hard himself, and often struck the enemy: he performed simultaneously the duties of committed soldier and good commander. (46)

 

Catiline is acting like a hero. He’s strong, he fights hard, he helps his soldiers, and he acts as a good general should. Sallust continues [slide #31]:

When Petreius, contrary to what he had expected, saw the great strength of Catiline’s exertions, he led his praetorian cohort into the enemy’s centre and, having caused great confusion there, killed them as well as others who resisted in various other places; then, from the flanks on both sides, he attacked the rest. Manlius and the Faesulan fell fighting amongst the foremost; as for Catiline, after he saw his forces routed and himself with only a few left, mindful of his lineage and his own old-time status, he rushed into the thickest of the enemy and there, fighting, was stabbed. (46)

 

At the end of his life Catiline remembers his ancestry and he dies bravely as befits a good Roman. But who is this Petreius who is commanding the Roman forces? Didn’t the senate send Antonius? Well it turns out Antonius had some bad gout that day and he couldn’t fight. So he stayed in his tent while his second-in-command Petreius led the army. In contrast to Catiline, who endures hunger and cold and pain and fights bravely alongside his soldiers, the Roman consul has to lie down in his tent instead of fighting because his feet hurt. This emphasizes Catiline’s heroic qualities by contrast, even in a narrative of his evil. The worst criminals are those who could have been the best people.

The contradictions and ambiguities of Catiline don’t end there. Catiline’s character is both criminal and heroic, and the war with Catiline is a war against both insiders and outsiders. Sallust says on page 47 [slide #32]:

Yet neither had the army of the Roman people achieved a delightful or bloodless victory: all the most committed had either fallen in the battle or retired seriously wounded. As for the many who had emerged from the camp for the purposes of viewing or plundering and were turning over the enemy corpses, some discovered a friend, others a guest or relative; likewise there were those who recognized their own personal antagonists. Thus, throughout the entire army, delight, sorrow, grief and joy were variously experienced. (47)

 

The feelings that Catiline and his war evokes are both joy at the victory and the defeat of enemies, but also sorrow and grief at the death of friends or guests (and remember the importance of hospitality!) or relatives. This is a case that tests the limits of what it means to be a criminal or an enemy. Sallust thinks it set a precedent for corruption and civil war that led to the downfall of the republic. It shows that even though there are great differences between ancient and modern approaches to crime and punishment, the ancient Romans also struggled with issues like when to treat people as criminals and when to treat people as enemy combatants.

 

7. Conclusions and the legacy of Catiline

Before closing out the lecture for this week, I thought it might be of interest to see how Cicero’s famous First Catilinarian has entered modern political discourse at various times in recent history. Slide #33 shows some examples of how Cicero’s Latin slogan represents dissatisfaction with various powers. The Roman consul launched his tirade against Catiline with the rhetorical question quo usque tandem abutere, Catilina, patientia nostra? (“For how long, Catiline, will you go on abusing our patience?), meaning, essentially, ‘how long do you expect us to put up with your crap?’. The phrase has been redeployed in the modern era to characterize perceived villains in political conflicts around the world. On the slide you’ll see screenshots from issues as diverse as the Brexit decision, or Italian local politics, protests against Donald Trump’s presidency, or even inaction in the fight against the spread of COVID back in the pandemic. It has become a rallying cry of those who are angry or displeased with the powers that be—an interesting twist on its origin, where Cicero represented the establishment and was deliberately provoking a perceived enemy into taking deliberate action against the state. The legacy of the phrase, as well as the interpretation of whether Catiline was himself a criminal, an enemy combatant, or a hero of the people, depends a lot on one’s perspective. Rhetoric, as seen in the lecture on Plato’s Gorgias and soon again in our last lecture of the course, is alive and well even well into the 21st century. Thanks for listening and/or reading.
