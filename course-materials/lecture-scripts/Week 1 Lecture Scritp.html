3. What is ‘The Justice System’?

The first thing I want to do to introduce you to the course is to look at some instances of how the thing we call “The Justice System” appears in various types of media. (slide #5) For me, the first thing that comes to mind is Ned Stark in S1 of Game of Thrones, shown here: as Warden of the North and leader of his people, he carries out the sentence he has himself just imposed on a deserter. This is an important lesson to his children (and a powerful check on the abuse of power: it’s a lot easier to impose a sentence of death, e.g., if you don’t have to do the deed. Another example from real history is that of the Salem Witch Trials (slide #6). Here you have court trials before judicial officials, and punishment for the crime of witchcraft. To modern rational ways of thinking, such trials were nothing but a sham, since it was almost 100% a given that the accused would be found guilty and punished; it’s hard to prove a negative, and proving that you were NOT a witch was extremely difficult. Next we have some contemporary instances of the criminal justice system at work in Canada. (slide #7). Back in 2002 Omar Khadr, a Canadian citizen teenager residing in Afghanistan with his father, was captured by American troops after allegedly killing a US serviceman. He was imprisoned in the detention camp in Guantanamo Bay for ten years, convicted of murder in a US military court, and later appealed his conviction in order to return to Canada. His case made headlines in summer 2017 when the Canadian government settled a civil lawsuit Khadr had brought, arguing that his rights as a Canadian citizen had been infringed when the federal government did not protect him. We see in this example both criminal justice (the murder conviction) and civil justice (the lawsuit) in action.

When we think of ‘Crime and Punishment’ it’s important to remember that (Canadian) courts don’t just deal with violent crimes. (slide #8) Another case that made headlines was the prosecution of several Canadian senators for alleged financial misdeeds (known as the “Canadian Senate Expenses Scandal”). Political affairs in Canada are rarely as newsworthy as in the US, but this scandal made news for several years. Senators such as Mike Duffy were accused of claiming ineligible expenses and lining their pockets. Duffy stood trial in criminal court in 2014, was acquitted on all charges, and the scandal could have ended there. In August 2017, however, Duffy filed a civil lawsuit against the Senate and the RCMP seeking damages for negligent investigation and what might be called a malicious prosecution. In the winter of 2018/19 the lawsuit was dismissed by the Ontario Superior Court; Duffy continued to appeal but the matter was finally put to rest in 2021 when the Supreme Court of Canada dismissed the case (i.e. Duffy was unsuccessful in seeking damages from the Senate).

These two cases are complicated and controversial, but several elements of them are applicable to the topics we will cover in this course as we examine ideas of crime and punishment in antiquity: (1) the citizenship status, or the socio-economic status of the accused; (2) a military court as a special venue for certain crimes; (3) the idea of long-term detention of criminals (or those accused of crimes); (4) the appeals process; (5) the distinction between criminal cases and civil cases; and (6) the use of the lawcourts for political maneuvering and financial gain. These are features of modern Canadian crime and punishment, and it’s worth thinking about how much our knowledge of contemporary society influences the way we think about crime and punishment in antiquity.

 

4. Ancient Case Study: P. Claudius Pulcher and the Sacred Chickens

Let’s look now at some examples from the Classical world. (slide #9) I’ll start with the story of a crime and its punishment, in this case one that happened in ancient Rome.  In 249 BCE Rome and the North African city of Carthage were fighting the First Punic War.  The Roman consul Publius Claudius Pulcher was in command of 123 ships and was about to attack the Carthaginian fleet at Drepana.  Now, the Romans believed that the will of the gods could be revealed via bird omens. In order to determine if the gods favored the attack, he ordered the traditional test involving the sacred chickens.  The test is pretty simple.  You take out the sacred chickens and give them some food.  If the chickens eat the food in front of them, this shows the gods’ favor.  When Claudius did the test, the chickens refused to eat, and so Claudius said, “Since they won’t eat, let them drink!” and he ordered them thrown into the sea.  He attacked the Carthaginian fleet anyway and suffered a major defeat, losing 93 of his 123 ships.  When he returned to Rome, his political enemies charged him with capital treason and he was tried before the assembly in the Roman Forum.  By law the trial had to be completed in a single day, but a storm interrupted the trial and the assembly was forced to abandon proceedings.  The tribunes ruled that since the trial had not been completed in a day, Claudius could not be convicted and could not be charged again.  He could, however, be charged for the same crime, but this time not capitally.  He was duly convicted and fined 12,000 denarii. [Note: 1 denarius = average daily wage for an unskilled laborer]

At this point, you might pause to think about what this story tells us about Crime and Punishment in ancient Rome.  Could something like this happen today in Canada?  What elements of this story are totally foreign to our ideas of crime, and what parts are the similar to stories of modern crime and punishment?  As a story of crime and punishment, this one has some familiar elements to it.  First, there is the issue of so-called double jeopardy.  Once Claudius had been tried, he could not be tried again on the same charge for the same crime, even if he was acquitted only on a technicality.  Second, the punishment in this case was initially execution, but was ultimately a fine, both of which are possible punishments in modern courts, although Canada doesn’t execute criminals.  Finally, the charge was treason, also a modern crime.  On the other hand, there are also some unfamiliar things about this case.  First, the charge involves a religious matter.  It wasn’t the fact that Claudius lost the battle that was important, but rather that he engaged in the battle at all when the auspices—the signs from the gods—told him not to.  Today, we don’t charge people with treason for killing sacred chickens.  We don’t even have sacred chickens. Second, this case was really about a war crime, but was tried in the same courts as any other crime.  It was regular in ancient Greece and Rome to charge generals with crimes when they returned from a military campaign for their actions on that campaign, particularly if they had suffered defeat.  Today, a special military court would try a general accused of some kind of misconduct in war.  Finally, this was a trial held outdoors in the Forum before a formal assembly of the Roman people rather than indoors in a courtroom before a jury.

 

5. What this Course is About

This course is about investigating similarities and differences like these, between ancient ideas of crime and punishment and modern ones.  (slide #10) We will look at some ancient cases and some modern theories to try to see how well they match up.  In addition, we will look at some other ancient representations of crime and punishment. If we were looking at examples from a later historical period we might have court records and documents from archives, as well as theoretical texts or judicial manuals for procedure. Some texts like that have come down to us from ancient Greece and Rome, but not enough to give us a clear picture of what those societies thought as a whole. Instead, much like in the modern era, the art and literature that a society produces reflect the values and issues of that society. Thus, we’ll read three plays, some history, some courtroom speeches, and some philosophy to see if we can’t find out what the ancient Greeks and Romans thought about crime and how they portrayed it in their popular culture.  For most of us today, our ideas about crime and punishment are shaped more by television and news reports, televised trials, and of course online discourse and social media than by direct experience of crimes or courts. In our investigation of ancient crime and punishment, we’ll look not just at courtroom speeches, but also at the ancient equivalents of television, news reports, and scholarship.  In this way we’ll learn not so much what was legal and what was not, but instead how the ancient Greeks and Romans conceived of the category of the criminal.

Because this is an introductory course designed for non-specialists and because it’s just a half course, our reading is going to be very selective.  We won’t look at all of ancient Greece and Rome, but we will focus our attention on democratic Athens at its height and on the late republican period in the city of Rome.  Our investigation will be limited by other factors as well.  In the modern world there are records of law codes and many cases that demonstrate the application of the law.  The ancient record is a lot patchier.  We have to piece together what we know from scattered and sometimes fragmentary sources, and so there’s a lot we don’t know.  For example, in a few weeks we’ll read an Athenian speech on the murder of a man called Eratosthenes, but we don’t know how the case ultimately turned out or even really if the speech was actually delivered in court.  Moreover, the records we have mostly deal with cases involving wealthy, adult men.  Not only are these the people most likely to have their affairs preserved in written records, but they were also the ones who had access to the courts and who were most involved with the law.  In many ways, ancient Greek and Roman laws were designed for the elite; the poorer classes, women, children, and slaves were almost completely excluded from the law.  That means that they largely fall out of the historical record. We’ll see that self-help, by which I mean defending yourself and taking your own revenge for harm done to you, was an important factor in ancient crime and punishment, and that the scope of criminal law in the ancient world was far more limited than it is today.

6. Case Study: O. J. Simpson

We will even have trouble defining what exactly constitutes a crime in the ancient world.  Modern law makes a clear distinction between civil and criminal law.  Plus, we have police and public prosecutors who are employed by the state to enforce criminal law, which involves acts that harm the state in some way.  Civil law, on the other hand deals with private relations or disputes between individuals.  There are a number of famous cases in the modern world where distinctions can be made between criminal law and civil law. (slide #11) In 1994 ex-football star and TV personality O.J. Simpson was charged with the murder of his wife Nicole Brown (from whom he was separated) and her friend Ronald Goldman.  The police arrested O.J. and the public prosecutor charged him with murder, but the jury acquitted him, so there was no penalty.  In addition to this criminal case, Ron Goldman’s father brought a civil lawsuit for battery and wrongful death, and the jury found Simpson liable and ordered him to pay $33,500,000 in damages.  The criminal case was for the crime of murder and could have carried a punishment of a fine to be paid to the state, imprisonment, or execution.  All of these punishments are aimed at helping the state in some way as well as punishing the criminal.  The fine gives money to the state, imprisonment protects the people from future crimes and attempts to rehabilitate the criminal to make him into a productive member of society, and execution rids the state forever of an incorrigible criminal.  On the other hand, the civil case does not directly involve the state, and although the state does provide a judge, jury, and courtroom, it is the plaintiff and the defendant who fund the case and the damages are paid not to the state but to the plaintiff as compensation for the damage caused. 

7. Ancient Concepts of Civil and Criminal Law

Ancient Athens and Rome also made this distinction between civil law and criminal law, which scholars who study the ancient world more commonly call a distinction between private cases and public cases.  On the other hand ancient Athens and Rome did not have state funded police forces or prosecutors or prisons, and the category of public, a.k.a. criminal cases was far more limited.  The Athenians, for example, dealt with murder not in a public case but rather in a private case.  Had O.J. Simpson lived in classical Athens, he would have had only the civil trial and not the criminal one.  In this way, it’s accurate to say that murder in classical Athens was not a crime.  (slide #12) Incidentally, Murder Was Not a Crime is also the title of a book on ancient Roman homicide law, as seen here.  It does seem to be the case, however, that even if in a technical sense murder was not a crime in ancient Athens and Rome, the Athenians and Romans still conceived of murderers as a category of people at least analogous to what we think of as criminals.  That means that the modern category of the criminal existed in ancient Athens and Rome, but it seems to have had a different relationship to the law.

In modern Canada, the state takes a direct interest in the maintenance of order – we have, for instance, the city police, the OPP, the RCMP.  Although a crime may be committed against a victim, it is the state that organizes the prosecution, manages the punishment, and receives the damages.  In the case of murder, for example, even if the family of the victim is willing to forgive and forget, the state will prosecute a suspect anyway because the state has an interest in maintaining order by punishing murderers.  On the other hand, debt collection is in the modern world a private matter, and if someone fails to pay a debt, the state does not prosecute this as a criminal act.  If, for whatever reason, a lender decides to forgive a debt, this is possible in a way that a victim forgiving, say, an armed robber is not.  We will see in this course that the ancient Greeks and Romans conceived of what we think of as crime much more as a confrontation between private parties, like modern civil law, and it was only when a crime directly involved the state, as, for example, in the case of electoral bribery, or treason, that the matter became a public case.  In most instances, it was the responsibility of the victim or the victim’s family to bring the charges, arrange for the trial, and to ensure the attendance of the accused.  If the family of a murder victim did not choose to bring charges, nothing would happen to the murderer at all.  This means that what we think of as criminal cases as opposed to civil cases were rare in ancient Greece and Rome.  On the other hand, many of the actions that were tried in civil cases, or private cases, in ancient Greece and Rome were actions that we think of as criminal.  But just because actions like murder were not, in the strictest legal sense, criminal, does not mean that the ancients didn’t think of the people who committed murder to be bad people.  Thus we’ll need to step outside of a purely legal definition of crime for the category to make any sense in this course.  The relationship between public and private cases will occupy a lot of our time.

 

8. Case Study: Foucault’s Case for Temporal Distortion

(slide #13) There are differing opinions on how closely modern crime and ancient crime are related.  In his book Discipline and Punish: The Birth of the Prison, the French historian Michel Foucault argues that “We are much less Greeks than we believe” (p. 217). That is, just because democracy, philosophy and other ideals from antiquity exist in later societies, does not mean that the heritage and parallels are exact. Foucault argues that the industrial revolution and the bourgeois revolutions of the late 18th century changed the world much more than we usually allow for.  Even though the American and French revolutionaries represented themselves as restoring the democratic and enlightened principles of ancient Athens and Rome, they were instead creating something new, Foucault argues, and modern societies function completely differently from ancient societies.  For example, modern Canada is governed by a democracy, which is a word taken from ancient Greek.  This makes us think that there is some kind of continuity between Greek government and Canadian government, and that modern democracies inherit an ancient Greek tradition by way of ancient Rome.  Foucault, though, says that ancient democracy and modern democracy are two very different things, and we just call them the same thing to give democracy an air of antiquity and tradition.  Athenian democracy, after all, was small scale and allowed votes only to wealthy men of citizen status, and not to women, immigrants, or slaves.  It was not rooted in a capitalist economic system, as is modern democracy, and so Athenian democracy is a fundamentally different political and economic organization.  What we think of as democracy is a capitalist institution founded on liberal ideas of individual freedom and it simply did not exist in ancient Greece or Rome. And not just the concept of democracy. In Discipline and Punish, Foucault is focused on the idea of crime and how we deal with crime.  He says that just as what we think of as democracy did not exist in democratic Athens, what we think of as crime and criminality did not exist in ancient Greece and Rome either.  One of his examples is the concept of “prison”.  In the ancient world, prisons were only temporary holding places for defendants awaiting trial or criminals awaiting execution.  We might think of the philosopher Socrates in prison.  He was in prison because a religious holiday prevented him from being executed right away, but as soon as the holiday was over he was killed.  Today, prisons are both places of punishment and so-called correctional facilities.  We think of criminals as people who have something wrong with them and that in most cases they can be fixed.  While the idea that punishment should correct criminals was present in antiquity, it was a minority view.  Punishment in antiquity was about making amends to the victim and protecting the community from future harm.  Today we still punish to make amends to victims and to protect the community from future harm, but we also punish to rehabilitate criminals for their own benefit.  Long-term incarceration is our primary means of punishing significant crimes, and it did not exist in the ancient world.  Conversely, we do not punish crimes with exile today, and this was a very common practice in antiquity.  The difference between incarceration and exile suggests a major difference in the ideas and effects of punishment.

 

9. Ancient and Modern Ideas of Criminality

I started this lecture with an example of an ancient crime, namely Publius Claudius Pulcher and the sacred chickens.  That crime and the subsequent punishment shows that Foucault is not entirely right or wrong.  Crime and punishment in classical antiquity, by which I mean ancient Greece and Rome, are in some ways similar to and in other ways different from modern crime and punishment.  The example of Claudius Pulcher and the chickens was focused on the criminal act and the trial.  (slide #14) Another example from ancient Rome will help to illustrate some of the more remarkable differences in punishments. One of the most famous punishments from ancient Rome is the so-called culleus [kū-lay-us] or “sack.”  This is the one where a parricide—that is to say, a murderer of a parent or grandparent—is sewn into a leather sack with a dog, a rooster, a snake, and an ape and then hurled into running water (Cic. Rosc. Am. 70; Dig. 48.9.9).  The animals, according to our sources, were not essential and could be substituted.  The sack was a very old punishment, and when Cicero (a Roman lawyer and statesman) speaks of it he says that it was something his ancestors did.  It has an almost legendary status.  We have an anonymous source from around the time of Cicero (viz. the 1st century BCE), the Rhetorica ad Herennium, that tells us that a man named Malleolus was convicted of matricide and executed in 103 BCE by means of the sack, half a century before Cicero’s time.  The description in this text is a little different from what we see in other sources.  It says: (slide #15)

The man who is found guilty of killing a parent is veiled, tied up in a sack and thrown into running water…Malleolus was condemned for killing his mother; on his condemnation, his head was immediately wrapped in the skin of a wolf, and wooden soles were bound to his feet, then he was taken to the prison.  There those who were defending him brought in tablets and wrote his will for him in his presence, and there were witnesses.  Then the penalty was imposed. (Rhet. Ad Her. 1.13.23; cited in Robinson 2007, 45, trans. Robinson)

 

There was a law on parricide (the lex Pompeia de parricidiis), enacted probably in 55 BCE, that seems to have limited the use of the sack, and by the classical period of Roman law in the early empire it was very rarely if ever used.  Instead, in that era parricides were more commonly executed in the arena, often burned alive or given to beasts.  The sack was apparently reintroduced in the late empire by the Christian emperors as a special penalty for particularly heinous crimes.

            This is a place you might pause to think about what the penalty of the sack tells us about ancient Roman attitudes to crime.  The evolution of the penalty tells us a little bit about the changing ideas of crime in the ancient world.  First of all, it tells us that even in a single place like Rome, attitudes toward crime and punishment changed significantly over time, and the laws changed along with them.  We should always be careful when talking about the laws of the Greeks and Romans to specify what historical period we’re talking about.  Second, the details of the culleus as punishment suggest some clues about the Roman idea of crime in different periods.  In early Rome up until the middle of the republican period—and I’ll talk more about the history of Rome in the next lecture but for now just think about this as early Rome—the punishment of parricides seems to have been a religious matter.  While there is some possibility that putting wooden soles on the feet of the condemned parricide was to prevent him from running away, it is far more likely that this was to prevent the impure criminal from having any contact with the soil of Rome.  Similarly, the sack itself and the drowning in running water suggest a religious ritual significance.  Running water purifies, and the sack prevents the condemned from having physical contact with the city and its people, keeping the ritual pollution contained.  This idea of ritual pollution and the need to protect the community is one that appears throughout in the ancient world, and will turn up time and again in our readings. By the late republic, when the lex Pompeia limited the use of the sack, the religious significance of the punishment had faded and it seemed merely an especially cruel method of execution, distasteful to contemporary ideas.  In the late empire when Christianity had deprived it of all religious significance, it was brought back as a powerful deterrent precisely because of the spectacular cruelty .

            The early version of the culleus, full of Roman religious significance in preventing and purifying ritual pollution, demonstrates a significant difference between ancient and modern ideas of crime and punishment.  The punishment of the sack seems very strange to us not only because its ritual significance is lost to us, but also because crime and punishment are for us largely rational and secular matters.  We are not, for the most part, concerned that a murderer is unholy and pollutes the community religiously.  The loss of the ritual significance of the sack and its consequent lack of use in the late republic suggests that late republican attitudes toward crime and punishment were becoming more secular and rational, and so more like our own ideas.  I think we’ll see this in our investigation of late republican sources later on in the semester.  The re-adoption of the sack in the later empire with its emphasis on the spectacle of execution as a deterrent shows how much the power of the Christian emperors depended on spectacular displays centered on the person of the emperor.  It’s debatable whether this is still the case.  On the one hand, we hide away prisoners and do not perform spectacular public executions, but on the other hand we still depend on highly visual rituals of power, such as formal dress and speech in courtrooms.

10. Summing Up and Looking Forward

(end slide) Over the course of this semester, we’ll think together about what we mean when we say words like “crime,” “punishment,” and “criminal” and we’ll try to see if the Greeks and Romans thought of the same kinds of things in those categories.  We’ll first take a quick trip through ancient history, then make a real start with an overview of Athenian law, followed by a look at some examples of Athenians thinking and writing about crime.  We’ll also read an introduction to modern criminology.  The field of criminology is related to law, but it also goes beyond just the legal way of thinking about crime as well.  We’ll move on from there to an introduction to Roman law, and then we’ll look at some instances of Romans writing about crime.  We’ll look at some speeches, like Lysias’ defense of Euphiletos and Cicero’s defense of Marcus Caelius.  We’ll look at some history, namely Sallust’s history of the conspiracy of Catiline, some philosophy, namely Plato’s Gorgias, and some tragedy, namely Aeschylus’ Oresteia trilogy.  By looking at philosophy and popular culture as well as history and legal rhetoric, we’ll be able to put together a picture of crime and punishment that goes beyond the law itself and sees how Greeks and Romans thought of crime and punishment in a broader context.
