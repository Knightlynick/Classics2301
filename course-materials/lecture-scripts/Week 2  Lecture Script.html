1. Preliminaries

Welcome back to “Crime and Punishment in Ancient Greece and Rome”.  I’m still Dr. Debra Nousek and this is the second of our recorded lectures for the course, one for each week in which there is lecture material on the syllabus. Last week’s lecture was an introduction to ideas of crime and punishment from both an ancient and a modern perspective using examples drawn from both time periods.

 

2. Dating Systems

This second lecture provides a brief overview of the history of ancient Greece and Rome.  Before we get to the history of ancient Greece and Rome, I have a few notes about dating (no, not that kind of dating: this is about chronology). On slide #2 you’ll see a timeline for Greek history and one for Roman history. These are meant to illustrate graphically the periodization used to divide up the eras of ancient history; I’ve put them at the bottom of the slides as we proceed through the lecture so that you can easily orient yourself as you proceed. Now, for some of you this historical background may be a review, especially if you’ve already taken CS 1000 or another 2000-level Classics course. But if not, I think it’s essential to have a strong sense of the historical context in which the ideas and legal systems we will discuss took place. So let’s get to it. Slide #3 shows you some common systems for organizing chronology. BC stands for “Before Christ.” AD stands for Anno Domini, which is Latin for “In the Year of the Lord.”  You will also sometimes see the abbreviations BCE, which stands for “Before the Common Era” or sometimes “Before the Christian Era,” and CE, which stands for the “Common Era” or sometimes the “Christian Era.”  I usually use BCE and CE because, while these terms acknowledge the historical importance of the rise of Christianity and preserve the calendar the West has been using for a long time, they do so from the outside.  In other words, not everybody thinks that Christ is “the Lord,” as in the Latin phrase, but everybody can acknowledge that the date system most commonly used in the West is rooted in the rise of Christianity, whatever your opinion of that rise may be.  Years BCE count down, so 323 BCE is the year that precedes 322 BCE.  There is no year zero, so 1 CE comes right after 1 BCE.  We often count ‘centuries BCE’ and CE, so 23 BCE is in the first century BCE, 123 BCE is in the second century BCE, 223 BCE is in the third century BCE, and so on.  We’ll focus a lot of attention in this class on the fifth, fourth and first centuries BCE.  And one last note, traditionally BCE, CE and BC all go after the year, but AD goes before it, so you’ll see, for example, using a modern date, you’d write or say ‘2026 CE’ or ‘AD 2026’.

3. Periodization

Now that you’ve got the dating system, I want to talk about periodization, which means the division of history into periods, or segments (somewhat like the way we sometimes refer to certain decades as eras (e.g. “the Trudeau Era”), but on a larger scale).  [slide #4] While the specific years for these periods can be a little flexible, the periodization we’ll use for this class goes like this: For Greece, 6500-3000 BCE is the Neolithic Period, 3000-ca. 1100 BCE is the Bronze Age, 1100-750 BCE is the Dark Age, 750-480 BCE is the Archaic Period, 480-323 BCE is the Classical Period, and 323-30 BCE is the Hellenistic Period.  You’ll notice that as we get to those 5th and 4th centuries the dates are more specific. This is because we have better sources to draw upon. For Rome, 753-509 BCE is the Regal Period, 509-27 BCE is the Republican Period, 27 BCE-284 CE is the Imperial Period, and 284-476 CE is the Later Roman Empire.  Many of these periods have subdivisions that are important for other ways of looking at the ancient world, but I want to keep to the larger picture for now. In this course our focus will be on the Classical Period in Greece and on the Late Republican Period in Rome, but it’s worth knowing about the others for some context.

Before I go further, let me try to anticipate some questions that usually crop up about dates and what you need to know for the tests: for the most part, you should learn the sequence of events or people, know the periods of history I’ve just covered, and be able to tie events and people to a historical period. Some dates will be significant and the general rule of thumb is that if you see a date mentioned more than once, or emphasized with respect to a major figure in the lectures, that’s something you should be sure to learn. Some students have found it helpful, when creating their study notes, to dedicate a page to everything that you learn for each historical period, to help you associate events with their historical context.

 

4. Neolithic and Bronze Age Greek History

So going way back to the first inhabitants of the geographical area that is now Greece, [slide #5] the Franchthi Cave[*] in the Peloponnesus, which is in mainland Greece, was continuously inhabited from about 20,000 BCE to about 3,000 BCE.  The first evidence we have of agriculture and the domestication of animals at the cave dates to about 6,000 BCE.  Think about those ranges for a bit and realize that cities like Athens and Rome have been inhabited for a few thousand years.  There were people living at this site (the cave) for almost 15,000 years before they even developed agriculture.  Archaeological evidence suggests that agriculture and domestication arrived as a so-called package from the Middle East rather than being independently developed in Greece.  In fact, as far as we can tell, agriculture has only developed independently three times in human history.  Once in about 8,000 BCE in southeast Anatolia (what is now Turkey), once in about 6,800 BCE in China, and once in about 4,000 BCE in Mesoamerica.  Once agriculture came to Greece, the pace of change started to increase.  Just about 3,000 years after agriculture arrived in Greece the Early Bronze Age began.

The first half of the Bronze Age in Greece was dominated by a civilization we call Minoan.  [slide #6] We call it ‘Minoan’ because it was centered on Crete and Greek myth tells us that the legendary king of Crete was Minos, whose wife gave birth to the Minotaur.  In actuality, the language of the Minoans was not Greek and we don’t know what they called themselves.  Their civilization was remarkably advanced and in about 2,000 BCE they built amazing palaces and cities, some of which you can still see on the Greek islands.  They also developed a writing system we call “Linear A” around 1,900 BCE.  Because we don't know the Minoan language, we have no idea what the writing says.

[slide #7] Around 2,000 BCE, while Minoan culture was flourishing on Crete, there is evidence of widespread destruction in mainland Greece followed by the emergence of a new culture.  Archaeologists theorize an incursion of Indo-European peoples.  “Indo-European” refers to a family of languages.  It turns out that ancient European and ancient Indian languages have a lot of similarity, and from this historical linguists have deduced common descent.  So the Minoan civilization was not Indo-European, but the new civilization that started to emerge in mainland Greece in about 2,000 BCE was.  The widespread destruction and rise of a new civilization was a turning point in the Bronze Age, but it was not the end of Minoan dominance in the Aegean.

[slide #8] During this time (Middle Bronze Age, 2000-1600 BCE), Minoan civilization was still dominant, and it greatly influenced the emerging Mycenaean civilization of the mainland.  We call this civilization ‘Mycenaean’ because the most impressive city from this period in Greece was Mycenae.  In Homer’s Iliad, by the way, Agamemnon is the king of Mycenae.  The Mycenaeans spoke a language that was an early form of Greek, and their culture formed the basis of Greek culture.  The gods of the Mycenaeans were early forms of the Greek gods, and it was during this time that Greek myth as we know it began to develop.

[slide #9] Beginning in the 15th century BCE, almost all of the Minoan centers were destroyed by fire, and when the Palace at Knossos (the major Minoan center on Crete) was rebuilt, the written language changed from Linear A, which we don’t know, to a language we call Linear B, which is a kind of early Greek.  We call the writing systems Linear A and Linear B because they use the same characters, but they use them to represent different languages (Not unlike the way we use the same characters to write English as German or French). The languages are an example of how the Mycenaean civilization of the mainland absorbed the older Minoan civilization of the Aegean islands.

[slide #10] The domination of Mycenaean civilization in the Aegean lasted from about 1400 BCE to about 1100 BCE.  It was during this time that the historical Trojan War happened.  [map slide #11] In the 19th and early 20th centuries of the modern era, [repeat of slide #10] the famous archaeologists Frank Calvert, Heinrich Schliemann and Wilhelm Dörpfeld, and then Carl Blegen excavated at Hissarlik in northwestern Turkey and they proved that it was the site of Troy.  There are nine levels of the city.  [slide #12] Troy VI was particularly impressive but was destroyed in an earthquake around 1250 BCE.  Troy VIIa was destroyed by an invasion around 1150 BCE, and Blegen argued that this one, Troy VIIa, was the Troy of Homer’s Trojan War.

[slide #13] Around the time of the Trojan War, Mycenaean civilization collapsed for mysterious reasons.  Starting in about 1200 BCE, the great cities were destroyed by fire one by one over the course of about two generations.  Similar destruction occurred all over the eastern Mediterranean from the Hittite Empire in the east to Sicily in the west.  Egyptian inscriptions describe an invasion of “northern peoples,” or “sea peoples.”  If we think about the archaeological evidence for the Trojan War, then, we can see that the destruction of Troy was part of a larger pattern of violent destruction all over the eastern Mediterranean.  It is the story that remains of what was really a major collapse of civilization in the region.

 

5. The Dark Age(s) (1100-750 BCE)

[slide #14] The period that follows is known as the Dark Age(s), and covers about 3.5 centuries (from 1100-750 BCE). Communities were small and simply organized, but around 900 BCE they started to get larger and more complex.  It was during the next 150 or so years that the legends surrounding the Trojan War started to take shape.  Although they had some root in the historical reality of the destruction of Troy, the legends as we have them only started to form centuries after the historical events.  Moreover, they developed in a time without writing.  We call the Dark Age “dark” not so much because it was somehow bad or primitive, since archaeological evidence suggests that there was flourishing long distance trade and excellent art and culture, but because we have so little evidence for the period.  This is changing as archaeologists continue work on the Dark Age, but certainly there is no evidence of written records during this time.  Instead, there was a lively oral tradition, and the legends of the Trojan War that developed in the oral tradition were a new kind of myth.  No longer was myth based entirely on religious ritual, but now it was also based on literary narratives with a discernable relationship to history.  The Greeks of the Dark Age were remembering the last of the glory days of Mycenaean civilization.

[slide #15] The 8th century BCE, that is the 700s BCE, was a time of great change in Greece.  We think of it as the end of the Dark Age and the beginning of the Archaic Period.  The traditional date for the first Olympic games was 776 BCE, and this is probably not far from the truth.  Writing reemerged, and the alphabet, now totally different from Linear B, was an early form of the Greek alphabet still in use.  The Homeric epics were written down during the 8th century BCE.  It was also in this century that the formation of the polis occurred.  The polis was a kind of city-state that formed the basis of Greek political life throughout antiquity.  Examples include Athens, Sparta, Corinth, and Thebes.  In fact, we get the word “political” from the word “polis.”  Although different Greek poleis (the plural of polis is poleis) were organized in different ways, they shared more similarities than differences.  They were usually run by a system that organized the male heads of aristocratic families into some kind of cooperative and competitive system, creating an aristocratic class that both competed with each other and cooperated to dominate the other classes.

[slide #16] Another new development of the 8th century BCE was colonization.  The Greeks sent colonists both east and west, all around the Black Sea and the Mediterranean Sea.  Some of the most important colonial sites were on Sicily and in southern Italy, but there were many that were even further away.  The reasons for colonization were complex, but are tied to the organization of the poleis.  In particular, sending out a colony was a good way of controlling both population growth and political competition.  Colonization meant that the Greeks were becoming a major cultural power around the Mediterranean and Black Seas, but there were also Phoenician colonies being founded around the same time.  The Phoenicians were what the Greeks called the people who lived in the area where Israel and Syria are now.  One of the most important Phoenician colonies was Carthage, in North Africa.  They’ll be important later when I talk about Roman history.

6. The Archaic Period (750-480 BCE)

[slide #17] The next century—the 7th century BCE—saw the introduction of the hoplite phalanx, institutionalizing warfare between the Greek poleis.  Warfare was a way in which the Greek city-states competed with each other, but the institutionalization of a shared style of war was paradoxically something that united the poleis as well.  Moreover, the hoplite phalanx helped to create a spirit of equality in the army.  There’s not a lot of room for individual heroism in hoplite warfare, because its effectiveness relies on strict coordination and formation.  [slide #18 GoT ‘hoplites’] The institution of the hoplite phalanx helped to create a class of people who were wealthy enough to afford the armor hoplites wore (they had to buy it themselves or be condemned to be the light-armed troops who were more likely to be injured) but not really elite.  [slide #19; repeat of slide #17] This was a kind of upper middle class that would eventually form the governing class of democratic cities.  The 7th century was an era of intense competition not only between city-states, but within the poleis between classes as well.  Tyrants often overthrew the traditional aristocrats who had organized the polis system in the 8th century.  Tyrants were monarchs, but they achieved their power by appealing to the poorer classes and rousing sentiment against the aristocrats.  This means that some of the greatest artistic and cultural achievements of archaic Greece were sponsored and encouraged by tyrants as a way of shoring up popular support against the oligarchic aristocrats.  There was also an emerging middle class in the poleis, made up of men who were neither the landless poor nor the wealthy aristocrats, and these men were the bulk of hoplites.

[slide #20] In 594 BCE Solon the Athenian circumvented the competition between tyranny and oligarchy by reforming the Athenian government.  He eliminated debt-slavery and made political representation based on wealth rather than birth.  This was a way for the emerging middle class to take power in Athens, establishing a democracy opposed to both tyranny and oligarchy.  In 508 BCE, Kleisthenes further reformed the government of Athens in the direction of democracy. Since Solon started the democratic reforms in the beginning of the sixth century and Kleisthenes further reformed the government at the end of the sixth century, we often think of that century in political terms as the time of the invention of democracy.  The sixth century was not only a time of political change and the development of democracy.  It also saw the beginnings of Greek philosophy and natural science, thanks to Thales, Anaximander, and Pythagoras.

7. The Classical Period (480-323 BCE)

[slide #21] Moving on to the fifth century, in 499 BCE, the Greek cities in Ionia, which is the west coast of Anatolia, what is now Turkey, rebelled against Persian rule.  This is known as the Ionian Revolt, and Athens sent aid to the rebels, who were nevertheless defeated in 494 after a five-year war.  Shortly afterwards, the Persian king Darius I invaded Greece, but he was defeated not far from Athens at Marathon in 490. A subsequent invasion by Xerxes was turned back after battles at Thermopylae, Artemisium, and Salamis in 480, and finally at Plataea in 479. The end of the Persian Wars marks the end of the Archaic Period and the beginning of the Classical Period in Greece.

[slide #22] The fifth century, following the victory over the Persians, was the height of Greek civilization.  This was the time of the three great tragedians Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides as well as the comedian Aristophanes, the philosopher Socrates, the politician Pericles, the historians Herodotus and Thucydides, and the sculptor Phidias, who made such statues as the Athena Parthenos (seen in this slide, as a reconstruction in Nashville) in the Parthenon and the Zeus of Olympia as well as the frieze and the pediments of the Parthenon. When anyone thinks anything to do about ancient Greece, usually it’s something from the fifth century.

[slide #23] But it was not all peace, love, and high cultural achievement; the fifth century was also a time of brutal warfare as a democratic coalition led by the Athenians and an oligarchic coalition led by the Spartans fought the Peloponnesian War from 431 to 404 BCE.  In the 28-year war, both sides routinely executed the adult male citizens of defeated cities and enslaved the rest of the inhabitants.  The war captives were such a rich source of slaves that the scale of slavery increased dramatically, and the great advancements and cultural achievements of cities such as Athens were enabled by the labor of slaves.  Think about the lasting fame of the Parthenon, high atop the Acropolis, and how amazing it is, but when you do think about the fact that it was made possible by the widespread use of slave labor.

[slide #24] After the defeat of Athens in 404 BCE, the classical period continued into the fourth century.  Although Sparta won the Peloponnesian War, Athens continued to be a cultural leader among the Greek poleis.  This was the time of the philosophers Plato (whom we’ll read) and Aristotle and the great orator Demosthenes.  Demosthenes was famous in particular for speaking out against the Macedonian king Philip II, who was taking control of more and more cities in Greece.  When Philip was murdered in 336, his son, the twenty-year-old Alexander became king and launched his campaign that conquered the Middle East all the way from Egypt to India.  In 323 BCE Alexander died and his empire fragmented.

 

8. The Hellenistic Period (323-30 BCE)

[slide #25] The death of Alexander marks the end of the Classical Period and the beginning of the Hellenistic Period.  “Hellenic” is an adjective that means “Greek,” since “Hellas” is the Greek word for “Greece.”  The Hellenistic Period is called “Hellenistic” because the nineteenth century historians who named the period saw that Alexander had spread Greek civilization and the Greek language far beyond Greece.  The majority of Greek speakers after Alexander were not actually Greek, but rather were Graecizing (or “Greek-like”, or maybe “Greek-ish”) people—not Hellenic but rather Hellenistic.  The Hellenistic period in the third century BCE was a time of sophisticated learning.  The Stoic and Epicurean schools of philosophy flourished, and the Library of Alexandria was founded. Euhemerus, who theorized that the gods were really just exaggerations of important historical figures, and Apollonius of Rhodes, who wrote an epic poem, Argonautica about Jason and the Argonauts, wrote during the third century BCE.  Incidentally, Hellenistic Egypt is where the Rosetta Stone comes from (seen here on the right).  It’s an early second century BCE inscription of a royal decree in Greek as well as Egyptian hieroglyphs and demotic.  It shows Hellenistic Greek culture interacting with the local Egyptian culture conquered by Alexander and ruled by his successors the Ptolemies, and because the decree is written in Greek and demotic as well as hieroglyphs, it was instrumental in the process of modern scholars learning to decipher hieroglyphs.

[slide #26] The third century BCE saw Rome’s first interventions in Greek affairs, and the second century was the time when Rome conquered Greece.  When the Achaean Confederacy (a loose alliance of poleis) rebelled against Rome in 146 BCE, Rome defeated the rebellion and destroyed Corinth, the most important Greek city of the time.  After 146, Rome was the virtually unchallenged ruler of the Mediterranean.

 

9. Enter the Romans

But now that Rome is on the scene, we should go back and start with the beginning of Roman history.  [slide #27] The traditional date for the foundation of Rome was 753 BCE, and archaeology has shown that there was a town on the current site of Rome at that time.  For comparison, this was right around the reemergence of writing in Greece, the foundation of the Olympic games, and the wave of Greek colonization.  Elected kings ruled early Rome, and so the period following the foundation of the city is known as the Regal Period, from the Latin word for king, rex.  At the end of the sixth century BCE an aristocracy overthrew the monarchy and instituted the Republican government.  This was around the same time as the competition between tyranny and aristocracy was happening in Greece as well.

[slide #28] From an early date, the influence of Greek colonization in the south of Italy meant that the Romans connected their traditional Italian gods to the Greek gods.  This is why we can say that, for example, Jupiter is the Roman name for the Greek Zeus.  It also means that although Rome was a minor town, it was connected to the larger Mediterranean world from early in its history.

[slide #29] Moving ahead quickly to the third century BCE, the Hellenistic period in Greece, in 280 BCE Rome was in the final stages of conquering the Italian peninsula, and the Greek colony of Tarentum in southern Italy enlisted the aid of the Hellenistic king Pyrrhus of Epirus to resist Roman conquest.  Pyrrhus was initially successful, but he lost so many men in his victory that he was unable to win the war.  This is why we call it a Pyrrhic victory to win the battle but lose the war.  Rome defeated Pyrrhus in 275 and captured Tarentum in 272, completing the conquest of Italy.  A coin issued by Pyrrhus is our first example of the legend of the Trojan origin of Rome.  According to this legend, the descendants of Aeneas, who was a Trojan prince, founded Rome.  Aeneas appears in the Iliad, and the subsequent tradition says that he fled to Italy following the Trojan War.  Pyrrhus claimed that he was a descendant of Achilles who would repeat the Greek victory over Troy by defeating the Roman descendants of Aeneas.  The story is significant because it shows the Greeks and the Romans trying to make sense of the emergence of Rome as a Mediterranean power in the third century by connecting the previously unimportant city with Greek myth.  For the Greeks this allowed them to claim Rome as somehow belonging to Greek culture, and for Rome this allowed them to claim a place in the current dominant culture of the eastern Mediterranean.

As was the usual practice in antiquity, the victorious Romans brought war captives back to Rome from Tarentum as slaves.  [slide #30] One of these slaves was a man named Livius Andronicus.  In 240 BCE at a festival in honor of Jupiter, he staged a tragedy on a Greek subject—the first regular dramatic text in Latin.  He then translated the Odyssey into Latin.  This continued the integration of Rome into Greek culture and the appropriation of Greek culture by Rome.  It means that Roman culture as we know it is largely Greek, and a Greek slave founded Roman literature.

Besides Greece, the other major power in the Mediterranean was Carthage.  You may recall that Carthage was a Phoenician colony in North Africa, in what is now Tunisia. [slide #31]  By the third century BCE, Carthage was the major city in North Africa and also ruled much of Sicily and the coasts of western Europe (the green areas on the map).  Just as the conquest of Italy brought Rome into conflict with Greece, it also brought Rome into conflict with Carthage.  In the third century Rome fought the first and second Punic Wars against Carthage.  They are called “Punic” because Punic is a short version of Phoenician, and Carthage was originally a Phoenician colony.  In 218 BCE Hannibal the Carthaginian general crossed the Alps and ravaged Italy.  Rome decisively defeated Carthage finally in 201 BCE, thanks to Scipio Africanus.  It was not, however, until the Third Punic War from 149-146 BCE that Rome finally destroyed Carthage.  You may recall that it was also in 146 BCE that Rome destroyed Corinth, and the destruction of the major city of Carthage and the most important city in Greece of the time was an emphatic demonstration of Roman dominance over the two previous major powers of the Mediterranean.

[slide #32] The middle of the second century BCE was thus the time when Rome established Mediterranean dominance, but the foreign expansion also produced political instability in the capital city.  In 133 BCE, for example, a radical senator named Tiberius Gracchus was beaten to death by his colleagues.  The republican Roman government had been formed when Rome was an individual city-state, just like the Greek cities had been.  But the conquest of the Mediterranean and Rome’s efforts to continue to rule the conquered lands through governors put enormous strain on a system that was not designed for so large a scale.  So beginning in the second century and throughout the so-called Late Republic, our historical focus is largely on internal Roman politics and culture.

[slide #33] At the end of the second century BCE, then, and the beginning of the first century BCE, Rome faced great difficulties, including a difficult war in Numidia (in North Africa), a slave revolt in Sicily, a series of losses in the north against the Cimbri and the Teutones, and finally a rebellion of the Italian cities.  The two generals who succeeded in overcoming these challenges, Marius and Sulla, then themselves fought a civil war that only ended when Sulla became dictator in 81 BCE.  The conflict between Marius and Sulla set a pattern that Roman generals who succeeded in winning victories abroad would march against the city and seek sole power when they returned to Rome.

[slide #34] The precedent of civil war continued, and the next generation was the last generation of the Roman Republic.  The first century BCE was the time of Cicero, Pompey, Caesar, Crassus, and Cato as well as Antony, Cleopatra, and Octavian, who would later become Augustus.  This is an enormously chaotic and complex time in Roman history, but it’s also the period for which we have the most evidence.  When we think of the great achievements of Rome, they are often from the chaotic and violent first century BCE.  The HBO series Rome was about this time, and classes and scholarship are largely focused on this time as well.  Just as the great achievements of Greece were made in the midst of the upheaval and uncertainty of the Peloponnesian War, so the upheaval of the Roman civil wars somehow produced great cultural achievements in Rome.  When we get to Rome in this course, we’ll focus our attention on the late first century BCE.

Like Sulla and Marius, the great men of the next generation were generals who conquered foreign enemies and then returned to the city to take over it with their armies.  Pompey fought in Spain and the Middle East, and then he came back to Rome and imposed martial law.  While Pompey was taking over the city, Caesar was fighting in Gaul, and when he returned from the north Pompey fled the city with his army.  Meanwhile, in Rome steadfast republicans like Cicero and Cato were trying to keep the city ruled by the senate and prevent powerful generals like Pompey and Caesar from becoming monarchs and using their armies to rule Rome.  We should be careful to realize, however, that just as the tyrants in archaic Greece were often very popular among the poor people, so were the great generals of the late Roman Republic.  The senatorial faction, like Cato and Cicero, were extremely socially and economically conservative and they were doing their best to keep power in the hands of the wealthy elite as a class.  Men like Caesar and Pompey sought individual power rather than power for the elite class, and so they frequently appealed to the masses, spending enormous amounts of money on games, parades, free food giveaways, and redistribution of land.  Their imposition of sole power kept the courts and the laws largely intact and even in many cases allowed poor people access to the laws and the courts that they hadn’t had before.  It was the wealthy senators, who are some of our most eloquent authors of the period, who were upset by the generals and wrote against them.

 

10. The Roman Empire

[slide #35] The last major battle of the Roman civil wars was the battle of Actium in 31 BCE.  At Actium, Octavian, who was Julius Caesar’s nephew and heir, defeated Antony and Cleopatra, the Egyptian monarch, who had been Caesar’s lieutenant and lover, and when Octavian settled affairs in Rome in 27 BCE he took the name Augustus and became the first Roman Emperor.  Although officially he restored the Republic and handed power back to the senate and the people, in reality he maintained unofficial control for the rest of his life.  Cleopatra was the last of the Ptolemaic monarchs in Egypt, and the last of the Hellenistic successors of Alexander to rule an independent kingdom.  When Augustus annexed Egypt for Rome after the suicide of Cleopatra, it was the end of the last Hellenistic kingdom and so the end of the Hellenistic Period.  In Greece, the Augustan period is the beginning of the Roman Imperial Period.

[slide #36] The age of Augustus was a golden age of Latin literature, and some of our best sources for Roman mythology are from this period.  Virgil’s Aeneid, which tells the story of Aeneas’ flight from Troy to Italy, is from this time, as is Ovid’s Metamorphoses.  The Augustan period is the beginning of the so-called pax Romana and not only was it a time of peace after civil war, but it was also a time of great reform.  Just as the generals Pompey and especially Caesar had made legal reforms that helped the people to take advantage of the courts and laws, so the Augustan period was also a time of expanding the availability of the courts and the laws to lower classes.  Increasing the control of one man at the top of the hierarchy also meant increasing public order at the lower levels of the social and economic pyramid.

[slide #37] The first two centuries CE were a relatively stable time in Mediterranean history.  There were some tyrannical emperors, like Caligula and Nero, and there were some civil wars over the imperial succession, but in general it was a peaceful time.  The historian Edward Gibbon, who wrote The Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire, argued that it was in this time and place that the condition of humanity was the best it had ever been and the best it would be until Gibbon’s own time of the nineteenth century.  It was during this time that the emperor Trajan expanded the territory of the Roman empire to its greatest extent.  In 212 CE the emperor Caracalla extended Roman citizenship to all free inhabitants of the empire, which at the time included Spain, Gaul, Britain, Germany up to the Rhine and Danube, North Africa, Italy, Greece, Dacia, Thrace, Egypt, and the Middle East up to the middle course of the Euphrates.

[slide #38] After Caracalla, however, the third century CE was a time of crisis and repeated civil wars over the imperial succession.  The political instability was ended only by the powerful emperors Diocletian, who ruled from 285-305, and Constantine, who ruled from 312-337 CE.  Following Constantine’s conversion to Christianity, the gradual Christianization of the empire changed but did not end Rome.  This Later Roman Empire of the fourth and early fifth centuries was much more like the Early Roman Empire than it was like the Medieval Europe that would follow it.

[slide #39] Although the Eastern Roman Empire, also called the Byzantine Empire, with its capital in Constantinople, would continue until the Arab expansion of the seventh century CE, the Western Roman Empire fragmented in the fifth century and was effectively nonexistent after a Germanic general deposed the last Western Emperor, Romulus Augustulus, in 476 CE.  There was still much continuity, but Roman history effectively ends in the fifth century in the West, and late Antiquity becomes the early Medieval Period.

[slide #40 (end)] That was a very quick run through of Greek and Roman history from the Neolithic period through Late Antiquity.  I’m sorry we don’t have time for more, but the Classical Studies Department offers a large variety of courses, from the overview of all this in a full year course in CS1000 to advanced courses on subjects like Late Antiquity.  If you’re interested in this stuff, I encourage you to think about a minor in Classical Studies, which is very easy to do and is a lot of fun.  You just need to take 4.0 courses in Classical Studies at the 2000 level or above, and make sure 1.0 of them is an essay course.  A major is not too difficult either, with just 6.0 courses.  Keep in mind, though, that if you want to do a major you need to focus on 3000-level courses, since 5.0 of your 6.0 courses need to be at the 3000 level or above.   In Classical Studies, though, 3000-level doesn’t mean third-year.  You can take 3000-level courses in your second year if you want to.  They are smaller classes and offer a more interactive experience.  Let me know if you have any questions about taking more courses in Classical Studies.  Have a great week!
