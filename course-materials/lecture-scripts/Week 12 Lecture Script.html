2. The Five Things

If you recall from the Lysias week, there was some doubt about whether ‘Lysias 1’ was an actual court case. There’s no doubt about this Cicero speech. This lecture is about a historical Roman court case, and the assigned reading is a copy of one of the defense speeches from the case. [slide #3:] The author of the speech was Cicero. His full name was Marcus Tullius Cicero, and he was a Roman politician who lived from 106-43 BCE. He was an optimate and a novus homo [hopefully you will remember these terms from that last two lectures, just sayin’ – Dr. Nousek]. [slide #4] Cicero was a hugely prolific author; he was particularly well known as what we could call a defense lawyer, and that’s what we have here. We’re studying one of Cicero’s courtroom speeches, most commonly known in Latin as the Pro Caelio, translated in the assigned reading as In Defense of Marcus Caelius. [slide #5] The date is straightforward, since Cicero delivered the speech at the trial in 56 BCE. Incidentally, this was less than a decade after the Catilinarian conspiracy, which we looked at last week. [slide #6] For the location, Cicero lived in Rome, and this speech was for a court in the city, so the location is Rome. [slide #7] For the language, Cicero wrote in Latin.

3. History and Background

[slide #9] Here’s some background to the case at hand, in order to help you make sense of the participants mentioned in the text. The year is 56 BCE, not even a decade since the large-scale threat to political stability at Rome that was the Catilinarian conspiracy (what you read as Catiline’s War last week). There is a charge of vis (political violence) against Marcus Caelius Rufus (whom we mainly call “Caelius” in English) at a quaestio brought by a team of prosecutors. These men were Lucius Sempronius Atratinus, Publius Clodius (probably the Publius Clodius Pulcher who was Cicero’s archenemy), and Lucius Herennius Balbus. Caelius’ defense team included himself, along with the support of Marcus Licinius Crassus (the rich guy from Catiline’s War) and our author Marcus Tullius Cicero. [slide #10] Caelius was born in the early 80s BCE and as a young man was somehow associated with Catiline, but we don’t really know in what way. Cicero in his speech claims that Caelius did not join the conspiracy, and that’s probably correct. [slide #11] He went on to start a successful political career, and in 59—that is three years before the case we’re looking at—he successfully prosecuted Gaius Antonius Hybrida (you’ll remember him as the consul who had gout when he was supposed to be fighting Catiline) on a charge of repetundae (=financial misconduct in a province).

[slide #12] As for our case, the background is this: in the middle of the first century, Rome was increasingly involved in Egyptian politics, and the king in Alexandria was Ptolemy XII (the father of Cleopatra VII, who was the famous Cleopatra). In 58 BCE the Alexandrians deposed and expelled Ptolemy largely because he was decidedly pro-Roman. [slide #13] The Romans were keen to see Ptolemy restored to his throne, but there was some infighting about the whole issue, since whoever successfully engineered his return to power would gain a lot of prestige (and also a sizeable monetary gift in gratitude) and he eventually was restored in 55 BCE.

But before that happened, there were some diplomatic negotiations. The Alexandrians had ousted Ptolemy because he was too cozy with the Romans, and there was a major faction in Egypt who didn’t want him restored to power. In 57 or possibly 56, the Alexandrians sent a deputation of over a hundred people to Rome under the leadership of a guy named Dio to oppose the restoration, but in the course of that embassy Dio was killed. [slide #14] It’s not clear exactly how Caelius was (allegedly) involved, but his ex-girlfriend Clodia was somehow caught up in it. The precise details of what actually happened aren’t important for our purposes. In any case, the prosecutorial team of Lucius Sempronius Atratinus, Publius Clodius, and Lucius Herennius Balbus accused Caelius of vis, that is, political violence, among other charges, and the defense team consisted of Caelius himself, Marcus Licinius Crassus, the richest man in Rome, and Cicero. It’s Cicero’s speech from the trial that we have, and in it, Cicero argued that it was all a plot cooked up by the ex-girlfriend Clodia, and Caelius was acquitted. Caelius went on to have a successful career, and supported Julius Caesar in the civil war.

[slide #15] And just to take Caelius’ story down to its historical end, some years later (and in the middle of the civil war between Caesar and Pompey), in 48 BCE, Caelius was the praetor peregrinus. He proposed a radical program of debt relief, was expelled from office, raised an insurrection, and was killed fighting. The reason I bring up Caelius’ subsequent career and downfall is that it’s surprisingly similar to Catiline’s career. One of the reasons Sallust picked the story of Catiline to write about is because he saw it as the beginning of a pattern. It was a big deal for Catiline to propose radical debt reform, raise an insurrection, and be killed fighting against a Roman army. But by the time of Caelius, not even 20 years later, it wasn’t such a big deal anymore. The old Republican system of government was falling apart, and big changes were happening in Rome. My goal for this lecture is that, hopefully, by the end of our study of this speech from the crime and punishment perspective you’ll see how it shows us some of the changes that are taking place in the Roman attitude toward violence and criminality in this period.

 

4. The Case, and Cicero’s Defense

As usual, I will lead you through some significant excerpts from the speech to illustrate how it connects with our larger study of crime and punishment. Just to catch you up on the trial’s progress by the time Cicero rose to speak, the prosecution team had already argued their case, and the other two speakers for the defense had already delivered their speeches too. Cicero was the last to speak for the defense, and his speech is nothing short of brilliant. It’s also pretty entertaining. The whole speech was delivered by Cicero himself (he is both author and speaker, unlike what we saw with Lysias). And the first passage I want to look at is the very beginning of the speech, section 1 (the numbers in square brackets on the slides are section numbers): [slide #16]

Members of the jury: [1] If somebody were here who knew nothing about our laws, our courts, and our customs, he would certainly wonder what crime could be so vicious that on a holiday when public games are taking place, when all civic business is at a halt, this one court should be in session. He would have no doubt that the defendant is charged with so massive a crime that to leave it unattended would result in the collapse of the state. (Page 193)

 

Well that’s quite an opening, and it surely captivated the audience right from the get-go. The day of the trial happened to be a holiday in Rome, called the Megalensia, or the Festival of the Great Mother. The festival involved dramatic performances, and most legal business was suspended during it [Imagine if somebody told you that you had mandatory jury duty on the May long weekend! – Dr. Nousek]. The charge of vis however, which is the charge in this case, was so serious that, according to the rules, the trial couldn’t be delayed even for an important holiday. In this passage Cicero is mocking the charge, saying that the charge of political violence is inappropriate. The law on vis was designed for massive riots and insurrections, when the safety of the state itself was in danger, so that the trial couldn’t be delayed without further risk to the safety of the whole urban population. It’s a serious charge indeed. But Cicero will present Caelius as a charming and harmless young man, who couldn’t possibly be responsible for vis. We know, of course, that not too long after this Caelius will in fact raise an insurrection against Rome, but nobody at the trial could know that would happen.

            But the fact that the trial is on the holiday also gives Cicero another opportunity. The jurors, if they were not on a jury, would be at the theatre watching dramatic performances. Cicero is going to present the narrative of this trial as though it were the plot of a play, and Caelius is going to be the young hero. In fact, there are certain stock characters and plots in Roman comedy, and one of them is that the hero of Roman comedies is a young man who gets into some trouble—usually involving a sexual indiscretion—but in the end he turns out to be a good guy after all. So (try to wrap your head around this) the jurors are sitting at the trial, watching Cicero present Caelius as the hero of a comedy, so, on the analogy of ‘everything working out in the end’ in this kind of Roman comedy, it would be only natural that they’d decide in his favour. That’s Cicero’s strategy. In other words, Cicero is making this case less about the acts in question and whether or not they’re criminal, and more about the character of the accused. According to this logic Caelius can’t possibly be guilty if he’s the dashing young hero of the play.

But Cicero can’t just sail along breezily; he has some difficulties he needs to explain away if he’s going to present the character of Caelius in this manner. The first is Caelius’ former association with the wicked and dastardly Catiline. It’s an uncomfortable fact that our ‘hero’ had been a follower of the greatest villain in the history of Rome, from whom Cicero saved the state in its moment of greatest danger (and which Cicero has kept on mentioning for his own glorification ever since). But Cicero has a plan for this. Take a look at what he says in section 13: [slides #17, 18]

[13] Who was ever, at one time, more amiable to men of distinction, or who was more tightly bound to men of low vices? What citizen was ever a more staunch conservative at times, who was a more unspeakable enemy to this state? Who enjoyed nastier pleasures, who endured toil better? Who was greedier for plunder, who was more generous with gifts? He had, members of the jury, remarkable characteristics: he embraced many men in friendship, he was respectful and polite, he shared what he had with everyone, in time of need he assisted his friends with cash, influence, physical labor, criminal behavior if necessary, and daring; [slide #18] he could alter his character and direct it for the occasion, twisting and bending it in all directions. He behaved austerely with serious people, pleasantly with relaxed ones; he was solemn with old men, affable with young; daring with criminals, loose with the lecherous. (Page 197)

 

Ask yourself, who do you think Cicero is talking about here? Would it surprise you to learn that he’s describing Catiline? Yep. In fact, Cicero has a little problem in defending Caelius. Caelius is not the usual type of man that Cicero defends. No, Caelius is a playboy who goes to lots of parties and does all kinds of irresponsible things with his family’s money. In fact, he’s exactly the kind of young man who was a typical follower of Catiline—exactly the type that Cicero excoriated in his speeches against Catiline in 63—and Caelius was, not long ago, known to have been in Catiline’s circle. Moreover, in the complicated world of Roman politics and the courts, when Caelius prosecuted Antonius Hybrida for repetundae in 59, it had been Cicero who defended him (unsuccessfully, since Caelius [as prosecutor] made a name for himself with that case). Lawyers! So even though Caelius wasn’t actually a member of the Catilinarian conspiracy, it’s going to be a challenge for Cicero to present him as an upstanding young man unjustly accused in this case. But that’s exactly what Cicero’s strategy is going to be, and the first step is to say that when Caelius was a follower of Catiline, it was a time when Catiline wasn’t so bad—in fact he had decent political views, and anyway lots of young men were followers of Catiline. This is one of those times when it’s clear that courtroom rhetoric isn’t always exactly what the speaker really thinks (Just imagine any time that Harvey Specter on Suits appears in the courtroom! – Dr. Nousek, fondly remembering that show). What a speaker says in court is determined by the circumstances, and by what he wants the jury to think. As we’ve seen so often before, it’s all about persuasion. Cicero may really think that Catiline was terrible, or that Caelius is a worthless, snooty brat, but he outwardly crafts his speech around a defense strategy that’s likely to persuade the jurors that Caelius isn’t guilty (compare Lysias’ speech for Euphiletos).

            He continues to build his case around Caelius’ good character in sections 27-28: [slide #19]

But to you, Balbus [the prosecutor], I’ll answer with your approval, if I may, if it’s right for me to defend someone who never turned down a party, who has been in pleasure gardens, who has used unguents, who has been to Baiae. [28] I’ve seen and heard many people in this nation myself who not only tasted a small sample of this life and touched it, as they say, with the tips of their fingers, but even surrendered their entire adolescence to pleasure; they came out of it sooner or later and returned, as they say, to a good harvest and became serious men of consequence. (Page 204)

 

Cicero here mentions Baiae as a place Caelius likes to go. It was a swanky beach resort town not too far from Rome. Cicero recognizes that the prosecutors and jurors think that Caelius is not Cicero’s usual type of client—those were normally older, stodgier politician types—but Cicero answers that Caelius is young and that it’s appropriate for young men to be a little wild. They’ll settle down when they’re older, he’s saying. In essence, according to Cicero, Caelius is like the young men in Roman comedy, who are a little wild and get themselves in trouble, but it all turns out well for them in the end.

            So Cicero is really making this case about the kind of person Caelius is rather than making it about what he’s done. Why would Cicero do this? Well, he has the opportunity because he’s the third of the three defense speakers at the trial. The other two speakers for the defense, Caelius and Crassus, have already presumably dealt specifically with the charges, and its Cicero’s job to make this case less about the charges themselves than about Caelius’ character. Nevertheless, he doesn’t stay away from talking about the charges completely. And remember: the charge Cicero is dealing with is that Caelius was involved in political violence stemming from the death of the ambassador from Alexandria. But he does come at the issue from a tangent. The next passage is from section 30: [slide #20]

 

There are two charges. One involves gold, the other poison; in both of them one and the same person is concerned. The gold was borrowed from Clodia, the poison was sought to give to Clodia—or so they say. All the rest are not charges but slanders; they belong to a violent quarrel rather than a public court. “Adulterer, degenerate, graft-giver.” That’s brawling, not prosecution. There’s no foundation for these charges, no basis. They’re fighting words thrown out hit or miss by an angry prosecutor with no evidence. [31] (page 206)

 

The way Cicero argues here is to suggest that these ‘gold and poison’ matters don’t really have anything to do with the death of the ambassador Dio. And Cicero, who is absolutely making this case about character, starts accusing the prosecution of ignoring the facts of the case and attacking the character of his client. In that line of thinking, he’s not the one responsible for directing the case away from facts and events and toward character: the prosecution used it first. This gives him the opportunity to attack the character of the prosecution as well as defending the character of Caelius. Every hero needs a villain, and Cicero here introduces the villain in this case: Clodia. Now Clodia was a very famous woman in ancient Rome. She was the sister of the politician Publius Clodius Pulcher, and she was a fabulously wealthy and scandalous socialite. She certainly spent a lot of time at parties and at Baiae. [slide #21] One of the ways we know about her was through the poetry of Catullus. He wrote a series of popular and scandalous poems about a torrid love affair with a woman whom he called ‘Lesbia,’ and the real identity of that woman was Clodia. So she was beautiful and (at least by reputation) promiscuous, and she was famous for, well, being famous. [You can imagine a modern equivalent in the Hollywood or other celebrity crowd – Dr. Nousek] She was also Caelius’ ex-girlfriend, and she was related to one of the prosecutors in the case. It’s a messy situation.

            Clodia’s involvement (real or imagined) gives Cicero an inside track for crafting his defense of Caelius.[1] That is, it allows Cicero to take advantage of the jurors’ prejudices about men, and women, and appropriate sexual conduct. Caelius and Clodia did the same things and went to the same parties, but Cicero says it’s just normal indiscretion for a young man, but in a woman like Clodia he argues that it’s a sign of her disreputable character. We’ve seen this kind of double standard before, in the Oresteia, when Agamemnon could sleep with whoever he wanted while he was away at war and he could bring home his Trojan-princess-sex-captive Cassandra, but Clytaemestra was supposed to be faithful to Agamemnon and not sleep with anybody, much less Aegisthus. We still have this kind of double standard today, where female celebrities especially are both popular and derided for being supposedly ‘slutty’. And young men in many circles are still allowed to ‘sow their wild oats’ without any damage to their reputations. I’m sure you can think of a number of modern examples. Getting back to our speech, the best defense is sometimes a good offense, and at this point Cicero , in addition to defending Caelius’ character, starts attacking the character of Clodia (she’s the main witness for the prosecution). In section 32 he says: [slide #22]

But if, once that woman is removed, there's no charge and no backing remaining for their attack on Caelius, what else am I supposed to do as an advocate, except to fight off the attackers? And I would do it more vigorously, if l didn't have previous enmities with that woman’s husband—I mean her brother; I keep making that mistake. (Page 206)

 

There was a rumor going around that Clodia and her brother Publius Clodius Pulcher were having sex with each other. We have no idea how well founded the rumour was, but it seems far-fetched. (They’re not on Game of Thrones, after all!) Cicero implies here that it’s true, and pretends that he’s not actually attacking Clodia.

            Next, he starts combining his defense of Caelius’ character with his attacks on Clodia’s character. This is section 48: [slide #23]

[48] If there’s anyone who thinks hookers should be forbidden to young men, he’s really severe—there’s no denying it—but he’s completely at odds not only with the lax standards of the present generation but even with the customary allowances made by our ancestors. When was this not common, when was it criticized, when was it forbidden? When, in short, was it true that what is now permitted wasn’t permitted? Here I’m only defining the issue, and I’m not naming any particular woman. That much, I will leave unsaid. (Page 214)

 

Apparently a normal part of the youthful adventures of Caelius and his generation at Rome is to have sex with prostitutes. We’ve seen before that prostitution isn’t exactly illegal in either ancient Athens or ancient Rome, but that prostitutes are not allowed to be citizens or to participate in the government. One way of thinking of this is that the punishment for prostitution is disenfranchisement (=loss of citizen status), but another way of looking at it is just that prostitutes can’t be citizens. In any case, it’s not a problem for citizens to pay for sex, the only problem is when citizens get paid for sex. Citizens are supposed to choose sexual partners, not to be available to be chosen by others. Cicero is again drawing parallels from the theatre in terms of characterizing the relationship between Clodia and Caelius. It was perfectly acceptable for citizens to watch plays, but actors were well below the status of citizens. This is because the audience actively does the looking, while actors are available to the gaze of others. Being the subject of looking is fine, but being the object is bad. So in this excerpt Cicero effectively calls Clodia a woman who takes money to have sex, even though she was totally wealthy (and so didn’t need money for anything!) and apparently she was the one loaning money to Caelius. But as a sexually available woman, she looks like the object to the jury, while the citizen man Caelius is the subject. Even though Caelius and Clodia were having sex with each other, and even though if money changed hands it was Clodia giving money to Caelius, Cicero knows that, because of their genders, he can make Caelius look like a nice young man and Clodia look like a prostitute. Here’s another example in section 49: [slides #24, 25]

[49] If an unmarried woman opens her house to everyone’s desire and openly sets herself up as a whore and decides to enjoy parties with men with whom she has absolutely no connection, if she does this in Rome, in her gardens, in the crowds at Baiae, if in short she behaves loosely not just in the way she walks but in what she wears and who her friends are, it’s not just her flashing eyes and loose language but her hugs, her kisses, her beach parties, her boat trips, her carousals that make her seem not only a whore but a whore who solicits men shamelessly. [slide #25] If some young man perhaps hooked up with her, does he seem to you, Herennius, an adulterer or a lover; does he seem to you to have wanted to assault her modesty or just to relieve an itch? (Page 214)

 

Such arguments are, I should think, distasteful to us in the modern era, and I’m sure it seemed distasteful to some in the sophisticated environment of the elite social circles in Rome, but Cicero can count on at least a large part of the jury buying into this kind of argument. And since we know that Caelius was acquitted in this case, it seems to have worked. Just as in the Oresteia, this case presented by the lawyers is less about the crime of political violence than it is about the sexual practices of the accuser and the accused. Cicero is entertaining the jury: he puts on a good show (remember, he’s channelling the tropes of Roman comedy!) by making the case less about the criminal acts than about juicy, gossipy, sexual ones.

            In fact, he argues in section 55 that there really is no case about the seditious violence, and the only substance of the accusations is the sex part: [slides #26, 27]

In the charge there resides no suspicion, in the case there is no logical argument, in the action that’s supposed to have taken place there isn’t a trace of the conversation, of the place, of the time; no witness is named, no accomplice; the whole charge is brought forth from a hostile, disgraced, cruel, criminal, sex-crazed home. [slide #27] The house that is said to have been assaulted in this wicked crime is filled with honesty, dignity, duty, and conscience; that’s the house from which the testimony under oath that was recited to you comes, leaving to be decided something about which there is no doubt, whether it seems more likely that a rash, pushy, angry woman invented the charge or a serious, wise, and balanced man gave his evidence scrupulously. [56] (Page 217)

 

It’s not just Caelius and Clodia themselves who are involved here, but their families as well. When we were looking at positivist criminology, one of the points I made was that positivist criminology is less about the particular criminal acts in question and more about the characteristics of the criminals. That includes not just the criminals themselves, but also their ancestry and environments. Biological positivism argued that ancestry and genetics determine whether or not a person is likely to be criminal. Sociological positivism focused on environmental factors like whether or not a person grows up with a support network or a family that emphasizes law abiding behavior (and remember that both of these ideas cropped up in the arguments for Catiline’s criminality in Sallust last week). In this section Cicero makes an argument that looks like a kind of sociological positivism. Whatever the facts of the case are, he claims that Clodia is bad and Caelius is good because Clodia’s family is a criminal one and Caelius’ family is not.

            Near the end of the speech, Cicero returns to the theatrical strategy he alluded to at the beginning of the speech when he talked about the festival that was going on at the time of the trial. In sections 63-64, he suggests that the prosecution, in the person of Clodia, has made up a fictional story as though the court were the theater: [slide #28]

Great indeed is the power of truth! It can defend itself easily and on its own against the wits and cleverness and wiles of men, against everyone’s invented ambushes! [64] This whole tale, for instance, belongs to an aging lady poet who has written a lot of comedies—but it has no plot and no ending. (Page 220)

 

I’ve argued that it’s Cicero who is assimilating this case to Roman comedy, and that he is presenting Caelius as a typical comic hero and Clodia as a villain. But just as he accused the prosecution of ignoring the facts of the case and making it about Caelius’ character, here Cicero projects his own strategy onto the prosecution again, arguing that it’s Clodia, ‘directing’ the prosecution from behind the scenes, using theatrical strategies.

            And Cicero is not quite done going back to the beginning. This is kind of like when you write an argumentative essay for an academic course, it’s a good idea to make your introduction and conclusion respond to each other. They shouldn’t be exactly the same, but they should address the same points. Cicero does something similar here. He’s already gotten back around to the theater part, and now he needs to go back to the inappropriateness of the charge. Here’s section 70: [slides #29, 30]

[70] Members of the jury, my case is said and done. And now you understand how important the judgment is for which you’re responsible, what a serious matter has been entrusted to you. You’re sitting as a court dealing with crimes of violence. That law applies to the empire, to its dignity, to the condition of our country, to the well-being of us all. The law was passed on the proposal of Quintus Catulus during an armed revolt of citizens, when the commonwealth seemed on its deathbed, [slide #30] it is the law that, after the fire during my consulship had been brought under control, put out the last smoking remains of the conspiracy. This is the law under which this young man Caelius is being prosecuted not to exact punishment on behalf of the commonwealth but on behalf of the sexual perversions of this woman. (Page 223)

 

In addition to reminding the jurors about Clodia’s sexuality, Cicero here points to the nature of the charge. In this case, the specific charge on which Caelius is being tried is vis, which means political or seditious violence. The Quintus Catulus that Cicero refers to here is Quintus Lutatius Catulus, who was a consul in 78 BCE. His consular colleague, that is, the other consul in 78, was Marcus Aemilius Lepidus, who in that year had assembled an army and marched on Rome. That’s the kind of violence that this law is meant to deal with. The law was designed to deal with riots and insurrection against the government, and as it was originally conceived the lex de vi  may in fact have been more fully about vis contra rem publicam, that is, not just violence, but violence against the state.

 

5. Character and Criminality

At the beginning of the speech Cicero pointed out that the trial had to take place even on a holiday because the law was about matters that were really urgent. He also points out now that it was under this law that the last survivors of the Catilinarian conspiracy were prosecuted. He argues that it’s crazy that this would be the charge against Caelius, who is such a nice young man. It may not be as crazy as Cicero tries to get them to think it is. Remember that Caelius was charged in the context of the death of that ambassador from Egypt, Dio. And he’d been on a diplomatic mission to argue against the restoration of the king of Egypt, Ptolemy XII. The issue of Egypt was a really important one in the city, at least partly because Egypt was an important source of imported grain for Rome, and so much of the poor population of the city depended for food on friendly relations between Rome and Egypt. So when Dio showed up in Rome to oppose the restoration of Ptolemy XII who had been pro-Roman, tensions were high; it could have been very easy to incite some riots and cause real trouble. This case may have been less about the assassination of Dio than about the real possibility of chaos in the city. But whatever the real reason behind the charge, and we just don’t know enough about the case to say because we don’t have the speeches of the prosecution. Cicero has presented Caelius as just a nice young man who got himself in a little trouble with his slutty girlfriend, so it can’t be right that he would be as dangerous to the city as Lepidus and Catiline were. Ultimately, Cicero, like the positivists, makes this case about character rather than fact.

            I don’t have any more passages from the Pro Caelio to go over, but before I finish, I want to look at a few passages from a modern book about Cicero and crime. The book is called Crime and Community in Ciceronian Rome, and it’s by Andrew Riggsby, who published it in 1999. Riggsby makes a couple of arguments that are especially interesting for us, and I think you’ll see that my reading of Cicero’s defense of Caelius comes from his. First, he talks about the theatrical substitution: [slide #31]

Finally, we should make explicit reference to a radical technique for establishing judging paradigms which is illustrated in pro Caelio. We have already recalled one of Geffcken’s most interesting observations on this speech. Cicero pointed out that the performance in the court had taken the place (for the jurors) of a theatrical performance. He went on to draw parallels between figures and actions in the case and certain characters and plots of drama (especially comedy). By establishing these homologies Cicero invites the jurors to view themselves as a comic, not a judicial, audience. As such they would naturally support the young hero (Caelius) in preference to agelasts such as the prosecutors. (Riggsby 103)

 

Riggsby continues: [slide #32]

He in essence asked the jurors to use a general (theatrical) metaphor to govern their behavior in the court. Once he has done this Cicero’s view of specific issues (e.g., the proper outcome of the case) seems to flow naturally. A modern parallel might be use of the “war on ...” metaphor by politicians speaking to a citizenry generally suspicious of increases in state power or expenditure. If, however, the public can be made to feel that “war” (on drugs, on poverty, on cancer) is being waged, then unspoken consequences follow naturally. Of course, then, costs should not be counted carefully; of course, the situation demands unusual obedience to central authority. The trick here lies in trying to replace an entire metaphorical system. (Riggsby 104)

 

So the idea is that metaphors get us to think in different ways, and that a courtroom lawyer can get the jury to think the way he or she wants by proposing a metaphor. Cicero says that the trial is taking the place of a comedy, and that Caelius is like the comic hero. That makes the jurors fall into a habitual way of thinking that glosses over the particularities of the case. When we think about criminals, we fall into habitual patterns of thought—stereotypes, really—many of which we get from television shows or news reports about crimes and criminals. One of the jobs of jurors and of criminologists is to try to see through the habitual ways of thinking and instead to see the specifics of a case or of a person.

            The other point that Riggsby makes that I think is important for us in this class is about the changing view of criminality in the late first century BCE in Rome: [slide #33]

The crime of vis has two defining characteristics. The first is the actual act of violence. The second is an adverse effect on society as a whole. Perhaps by statute, and certainly in fact, it is vis contra rem publicam that is criminal. Lintott’s (1968: 28-34, 52-66) comparison of the treatment of violence in criminal law with that in civil law and in literary and philosophical texts is revealing. Rome had a long tradition of reliance on popular justice, usually in symbolic forms but even to the point of violence (Usener 1901). This does not so much indicate a “permissive” attitude toward violence as diffuse the authority to use it. Texts from the middle of the first century indicate that these attitudes were still broadly held and that violence in the pursuit of legitimate goals was itself legitimate, if not as a first resort. (112)

 

Back when we were looking at Athens, and at Draco’s homicide law in particular, one of the things I said was that it’s not so much that Draco’s laws always mandated death as the penalty for crimes as that Draco’s laws allowed for lethal self-help. In a society without police, self-help was a very important response to crime. In Rome, many people had the authority to use violence, but Riggsby argues that the law on vis, along with other laws of the first century BCE in Rome, began to limit in a serious way the authority to use violence. He continues: [slide #34]

Max Weber famously defined the “state” as that political institution which claims for itself a monopoly on the legitimate use of violence. Later sociologists have pointed out problems with extending this definition to the premodern period, but we can still use Weber’s insight to organize our thinking about the social context of violence. In particular, it will be argued in this section that the rise of the Roman principate involves the creation of a Roman state in roughly the sense of Weber’s definition. Or perhaps we should say that the two phenomena (the rise of a particular government of and the underlying “state”) are different aspects of the same political process of centralization of authority. We can also see that those changes had already started to take place in our period. (113)

 

One of the key elements of modern thinking about crime and criminality is that it is the state that uses legitimate violence, and that any violence that is not specifically authorized by the state is criminal. We have police who use violence to respond to crime rather than allowing ordinary civilians respond to crime with violence. This was simply not the case in ancient Greece and Rome, and it’s one of the fundamental differences between the ancient and modern worlds. Nevertheless, if Riggsby is right, then we can see some of the origin of the modern attitude towards crime and violence in Rome in the first century BCE. The law on vis, and its application in somewhat questionable circumstances to Caelius, shows that the Roman state is centralizing authority and restricting the authorization to use violence. In some sense, any use of violence that is not specifically authorized by the state is vis contra rem publicam, or violence against the state.

            But what does all this have to do with Cicero, then? Well, Cicero doesn’t dwell on the changing attitudes toward violence in Rome. Instead, he focuses his strategy in this case on the sensational, tabloid-worthy story of Caelius and Clodia. This, in its own way, is a modern approach to crime, because we’ve seen before that modern criminology has its roots in nineteenth and twentieth century positivism. Like positivist approaches to crime, Cicero focuses on people rather than acts, on character rather than crime itself. Another aspect of the case that is familiar to modern eyes is that the sensational story makes it celebrity gossip and links it to comedy, or popular entertainment for the jurors. The same kind of sexual double standard that allows Cicero to make his case is still around today, and it still has an impact on who we see as good and bad, and who we see as criminal or innocent.

6. Concluding Thoughts

            So, we’ve come to the end of my lecture on “Character and Ciceronian Crime,” which is the last lecture for the course. Allow me a few moments to reflect on things I hope you have learned over the semester. Back at the beginning of the term, we started with some modern examples of crime such as the cases of O.J. Simpson and Omar Khadr [slide #35] compared with the ancient examples of P. Claudius Pulcher and the chickens, as well as the crime of parricide. At first it may have seemed like those examples were hardly alike at all. But over these last few months we’ve examined several concepts that demonstrate themes that are common to both eras. In both antiquity and in our modern era crime and criminality is thought of as something that exists outside of the community—that is, criminals are removed from the community, turning them into prisoners (modern) or exiles (ancient), and the society is centered around ideas of non-criminals belonging to it as ‘insiders’ and criminals being characterized as ‘outsiders’. In addition, although the theories of criminality were developed to explain the modern world, we can now see that already in antiquity there were similar attitudes and approaches to explaining why crimes happen, or why people commit them. And finally, [slide #36] by examining the ‘popular culture’ of ancient Athens and Rome—the literature, philosophy, law-court speeches, historiography, and defense speeches disguised as theatre, we can see, I hope, that the gap between antiquity and modernity isn’t as big as we thought.
