2. Introduction

I like to think of this week as ‘Murder Week’ in CS 2301, since we’re going to look at Draco’s Homicide Law and its provisions that allowed exceptions to a charge of homicide. Our investigation is based on the evidence and arguments presented in a speech written by a man called Lysias, an Athenian speechwriter, called a logographos (English = logographer). We’ll use this speech, known as ‘Lysias 1’ as a case study to see how criminal law worked in Athens and to see how lawyers[1] characterized criminals. The case is a trial for murder; the victim’s family has brought a charge against the person who is the speaker in Lysias’ speech. The oration we have is thus a defense speech. One of the key points I want to convey with this is that in this particular case, the defendant will present himself as the victim of a crime, and he’ll present the man he murdered as the real criminal. It’s an interesting strategy because it relies on—and plays on—the jurors’ idea of ‘what or who a criminal is’, and that that will override their understanding of the law and the legal issues in the case. It will be an example where the idea of criminality is outside the confines of the legal definition. In the last lecture I spoke about the idea that criminality can be a key component of a person’s identity. We’ll see that the defendant in this case takes a similar approach; he argues that he should not be identified as a criminal, but that the murder victim should be.

3. The Five Things  [slide #2] 

            As was the case with the Oresteia you’ll be expected to know ‘the five things’ about this week’s reading as well. [slide #3] The author of the speech in question is named Lysias, who was a real person, and we know quite a lot about his life. Here’s the background. He was born sometime in the mid-fifth century (mid 400’s) BCE. He was born in Athens, but his father was a Syracusan resident alien, or metic, named Cephalus. Cephalus, incidentally, appears in Plato’s Republic, and Lysias himself appears in the Phaedrus. (I always love it when historical people known to us from one text appear in others!) After Cephalus died, the young Lysias and his brother Polemarchus left Athens for the panhellenic colony Thurii in southern Italy [slide #4], where he studied rhetoric (= persuasive speaking). Here historical events on a larger scale affected Lysias’ life. While Lysias was in southern Italy, the Peloponnesian War between Athens and Sparta was going on, and it grew to involve all of Greece. The war had started in 431 BCE. But in 415, Athens invaded Sicily, and this caused trouble for Lysias: he and his brother Polemarchus were exiled from Thurii as Athenian sympathizers and they moved back to Athens. When Athens surrendered to Sparta in 404, ending the war, Sparta imposed a ruling council of 30 men as a government for Athens, and they became known as the Thirty Tyrants because of their cruel and autocratic behaviour. They brooked no dissent. The Thirty Tyrants executed Polemarchus, but Lysias escaped and joined the democratic resistance. In September of 403, the resistance successfully overthrew the Thirty and reestablished the democracy in Athens. Lysias was given citizenship as a reward for his role in the resistance, but the grant was almost immediately annulled as unconstitutional and his status remained that of a metic, or resident alien.

[slide #5] Title: Lysias made a living by writing speeches for the law courts. In classical Athens, instead of hiring a lawyer to speak for you, you would speak for yourself, but hire a speechwriter to prepare a speech for you to give. Lysias wrote a large number of speeches and he was a successful orator. We have surviving 34 speeches and about 130 other titles. The speeches are numbered in the standard modern collected edition, and we often name them simply by their numbers, so we call this one “Lysias 1.” We also, however, sometimes give them titles based on their topics, so this one is sometimes called “On the Murder of Eratosthenes.”

[slide #6] Date: We know that Lysias was active as a speechwriter after the restoration of the democracy in 403, and we have a fairly reliable (but not certain) date for his death from ancient biographies in 380 BCE. It’s a good guess that the speech belongs sometime in that range.

[slide #7] Location: Lysias lived in Athens. He was a resident alien, even after his role in the restoration of the democracy. [slide #8] Language: He wrote in Attic Greek, and his style is clear and a good example of not just the Attic dialect, but also the Attic style that would be a model for future writers all around the Mediterranean. It’s characterized by straightforward simplicity. [slide #9: The 5 things collected] 

            [slide #10] Lysias 1, “On the Murder of Eratosthenes,” is the speech Lysias wrote for a man named Euphiletos (pronounced You-phil-EE-tus), who was accused of murdering Eratosthenes (pronounced Er-ah-TOSS-then-ees). There is some possibility that this was really just a show-piece and not ever actually a real case, or it may have been real. Scholars are divided on the issue, but I believe it was real. In the speech Euphiletos admits that he did kill Eratosthenes, but says that it was justified and therefore doesn’t count as murder. We don’t have the speech of the prosecution, so we don’t know exactly what they argued, but from Lysias’s speech we can guess that the prosecution argued that Euphiletos had some kind of beef with Eratosthenes, he found him on the street, brought him into his house, and killed him while he begged for his life. For the defense, Lysias and Euphiletos make the argument that you read. They say that Eratosthenes seduced Euphiletos’ wife. Euphiletos found out about it and arranged to have his slave tell him when they were having sex. When Eratosthenes came over, Euphiletos gathered as many neighbors as he could find, burst in on his wife and Eratosthenes having sex, tied him up, let him admit his guilt and beg to pay a fine, and then killed him. He argues that this was legal under Draco’s homicide law.

            [slide #11] As I’ve said previously in the Greek Law lecture, Draco’s homicide law was the oldest law still in effect in classical Athens, and it was the only one of Draco’s laws that Solon did not repeal. A very fragmentary piece of an inscription of the law has survived, and although the inscription we have is not complete, we also have some other evidence about the law which gives us a pretty clear idea of its provisions. A contemporary Athenian called Demosthenes, who was the most famous lawyer in the whole history of Athens, wrote a speech in which he quotes the section of Draco’s homicide law that’s relevant here. What it says is:

If a man kill another unintentionally in an athletic contest, or overcoming him in a fight on the highway, or unwittingly in battle, or in intercourse with his wife, or mother, or sister, or daughter, or concubine kept for procreation of legitimate children, he shall not go into exile as a manslayer on that account. (Demosthenes, Against Aristocrates 23.53)

 

This seems sort of reasonable. It lists exceptions to the ban on homicide, a little like a modern charge of ‘justifiable homicide’ or ‘voluntary manslaughter’. So, for example, according to Draco’s law, if you accidentally kill someone when you’re boxing or something, it’s not murder. If you have some bad road rage, it’s not murder. If you accidentally kill someone you’re not supposed to in battle—a friendly fire incident—it’s not murder. And most importantly for us, if you catch someone “lying next to” your wife, or mother, or sister, or daughter, or concubine kept for procreation of legitimate children, it’s not murder. Presumably, this one is like the road rage exception. It’s thought to be such an emotional situation that a man can’t be held responsible for his actions.

            So that’s the law that matters in the case at hand. And Lysias could have taken that approach when constructing the case for the defense. We’ll see, however, that Lysias is going to try a different line of argument. He’ll make the case that it’s not so much that a man can’t be held responsible for his actions in an emotional situation, like finding your wife having sex with some other guy, but rather that the law demands that adulterers caught in the act be executed on the spot. [Note: you’ll want to keep this argument in mind as we proceed – Dr. Nousek] Summary execution was not illegal in Athens. You may remember from the ‘Introduction to Greek Law’ lecture that The Eleven, who were the annually elected board in charge of prisons and executions, had the authority to summarily execute thieves who did not deny their guilt. The means that someone catching a thief in the act could bring him before The Eleven, and if the accused thief did not have a reasonable excuse, like that he was recovering his own property or something else plausible, he could be killed right away without trial. Lysias spins the homicide law in this way, that Euphiletos caught an adulterer in the act, who then admitted his guilt, and so Euphiletos executed him in accordance with the law on behalf of The Eleven, that is to say, summarily and without trial. [slide #12] And Lysias isn’t the only one to present the law this way. We already saw similar ideas at work in the Oresteia. Remember back in The Libation Bearers when Orestes quickly dismissed the killing of Aegisthus as unimportant:

The Libation Bearers 989-990

       …for of Aegisthus’ death I take no count:

he has his seducer’s punishment, no more than law.

 

So here we have the same idea, all the way back in 458 BCE, more than half a century before Lysias 1. That is, Athenian cultural understanding deems it appropriate to summarily execute a seducer. Orestes makes the same kind of argument as Lysias does about Draco’s homicide law. But however they want to make that argument, it’s not really true. The punishment, according to Athenian law, was not execution. The actual punishment for adultery was that the adulterer should pay damages to the man whose property has been harmed. If Euphiletos had taken Eratosthenes to the Eleven and demanded that they execute him, they would have refused, saying that they don’t execute adulterers. The upshot is this: if Euphiletos had caught Eratosthenes having sex with his wife and killed him, he could have argued that this didn’t count as homicide under the exceptions listed in Draco’s homicide law. But instead he argues that he carried out the prescribed punishment for adultery, which he (wrongly!) claims was execution, and that he did so because Eratosthenes was a Very Bad Man.

 

5. The Arguments: On the Murder of Eratosthenes

            So, there’s a lot to work with here in the context of crime and punishment. The reason I asked you to read this speech, then, is not because it reads like tabloid news (although it really does [slide #13])—Athenian Man Kills Wife’s Lover; Victim’s Family Claims Son Was “Just Passing By”—No, I asked you to read it because, (1) it’s an important case that teaches us about the actual provisions of Draco’s homicide law, and (2) because it shows us how Lysias and other Athenians conceived of criminality outside of the strict legal definition. We have here a literary work from ancient Athens that both provides evidence for a historical fact or event (Draco’s homicide law, which otherwise survives only in fragments), and shows an Athenian lawyer, and the law courts, in action. We get the law, and how it was interpreted in an actual case. And just like the way that modern lawyers sometimes try to put a spin on the letter of the law, we see Lysias doing this too. In fact, as you are reading this speech, it’s hard to remember that Euphiletos is the defendant and Eratosthenes is the victim. Lysias paints Eratosthenes as a criminal, although he has been legally accused of no crime, and Euphiletos as an innocent victim, although he has twisted the law to justify his murder. And especially because this is a strategy that was already in The Libation Bearers, it surely must have resonated with an Athenian jury. We don’t know how the case came out in the end, but Lysias was a good lawyer, one of the best, and he must have won a lot of cases. Even if he didn’t win this one, he is unlikely to have used a strategy that was too absurd to have a chance of working. Lysias can reasonably expect the jury in this trial to consider the murder victim as a criminal, and the alleged murderer as a victim.

[slide #14] Let’s now look more closely at how these arguments are constructed in the speech. In these passages, I’ll show that Lysias[2] has a rational interpretation of the law that runs counter to what seems reasonable to us, but it must have made sense to an Athenian audience. Lysias starts by asking the Athenian jurors to identify with Euphiletos:

[1] I would be very grateful, gentlemen, if you, the jurymen in this case, judged me as you would judge yourselves, were you to go through the same sort of experience. For I am well aware that if you employed the same standards for others as you do for your own behaviour, there is not a single one of you who would not be furious at what has happened. In fact, all of you would consider the penalties light for those who practise such things.

 

Right from the beginning, the idea is to create group of supposedly right-thinking and reasonable insiders and a group of evil and twisted outsiders. We saw in the Oresteia that according to Athenian cultural values, violence within the community is bad and violence outside the community is good. Crime is bad and war is heroic. The same sort of logic is at work here. Lysias is defining the community as including people like the jurors and Euphiletos, and excluding criminal types like Eratosthenes. They will thus argue that the violence Euphiletos has done to Eratosthenes is not only not criminal, it’s downright heroic. And this is an interesting point about judicial violence. One of the reasons it seems okay and even praiseworthy to do violence to criminals is because they are specifically excluded from membership in the community. In the modern world prisons help to serve this function. They form separate communities of outsiders, and so violence can be accepted and even expected. The way Euphiletos can present himself as good is by placing Eratosthenes in such a category of harmful outsiders. 

 

[slide #15] He continues boldly with this strategy:

[3] I believe, then, that all of you have the same opinion about the severity of the punishment, and that no one considers the matter to be so frivolous that he supposes that those guilty of such acts should be pardoned or deserve light penalties.

 

You wouldn’t expect someone who is giving a defense speech to be encouraging the jurors to be thinking about harsh penalties. This could totally backfire. But the idea is that Euphiletos is himself punishing a crime rather than defending himself against an accusation. And so he counter-accuses Eratosthenes of being a seducer, attempting to create a bond between himself and the jurors: [slide #16]

[4] I believe, gentlemen, that what I have to demonstrate is this: that Eratosthenes seduced my wife and corrupted her, that he brought shame on my children and insulted me by entering my house, that there was no cause for enmity between him and me apart from this, and that I did not commit this deed for money, to make myself rich instead of poor, nor for any other advantage except revenge, as the law allows.

 

The category of ‘seducer’ is an interesting one. We learned when looking at Greek law that the eponymous archon—one of the most prestigious offices in the democracy—had jurisdiction in Athens in cases of inheritance. This suggests that inheritance was a particularly important issue for the Athenians, and other evidence bears this out. First of all, the development of Athens as a polis emphasized the increasing role of small farmers. In Dark Age and early Archaic Greece, much of the land was devoted to pasturage for grazing animals, which was shared by the community. As time went on, the agricultural economy of Greece shifted towards the cultivation of crops, and as a part of this there was an increasing emphasis on dividing land into private property. At the time of the development of political institutions in the Archaic Period, one of the pressing issues was the cooperation of landholding families, households, oikoi. This means that inheritance of private property was one of the most central issues in early Greek poleis.

            Moreover, we can relate the position of women in Greece to this issue as well. Greek literature shows a lot of anxiety about seduction and parentage. In the Odyssey, the chastity of Penelope and the inheritance of Telemachus are central problems, while Odysseus’ faithfulness, or rather lack of faithfulness, is not at all a problem. In the Oresteia, Agamemnon’s sex life is far less significant than Clytaemestra’s, and she complains about that. One of the usual explanations for this is that nobody is uncertain who the mother of a baby is, but it’s easy to be uncertain who the father is. In order to know who is a legitimate heir and should inherit the family property, it’s necessary to keep a close eye on women’s sex lives so they don’t pass off another man’s child as a husband’s heir. Men’s sexual habits are not as big of a problem for the ancient Athenians because it’s not easy for a man to pass off another woman’s child as his wife’s heir. Men’s sexuality becomes a problem when a man sleeps with a citizen woman who might otherwise produce heirs. And remember the wording of Draco’s homicide law: [slide #17]

 

If a man kill another unintentionally in an athletic contest, or overcoming him in a fight on the highway, or unwittingly in battle, or in intercourse with his wife, or mother, or sister, or daughter, or concubine kept for procreation of legitimate children, he shall not go into exile as a manslayer on that account. (Demosthenes, Against Aristocrates 23.53)

 

The defining feature of the kind of woman who concerns the law is that she may produce legitimate heirs. So the category of a seducer is particularly hated in Athens because seducers confuse the issue of inheritance. And this is so deeply ingrained in Athenian culture that Euphiletos can just lie about the law and expect Athenians to believe him because his defense has what Stephen Colbert would call ‘truthiness’. The jurors will feel in their guts rather than in their minds that Euphiletos is right.

            He continues to set up the truthiness of his explanation by saying that it wasn’t just his wife that Eratosthenes seduced. It’s not just the action of seduction, but the repeated nature of Eratosthenes’ actions makes the issue one of Eratosthenes being a seducer: [slides #18, 19]

[15] Later, gentlemen, after some time had passed during which I remained quite ignorant of the terrible way I was being treated, an old woman came up to me. She had been sent in secret by a woman with whom that individual was having an affair, as I later heard. The woman was angry, thinking herself badly treated because he no longer visited her as he had, and so she waited until she found out the cause.

 

[16] Well, the old woman, who had been watching for me near my house, came up to me and said, “Euphiletos, do not suppose that I have approached you from any desire to interfere in your business. The person who is disgracing you and your wife happens to be our mutual enemy. If you catch your slave, the one who goes to market for you and waits on you, and if you torture her, you will find out everything. It is, “she said, “Eratosthenes from the deme of Oea who is responsible for this; he has not only seduced your wife but many other women, too. It’s his specialty.”

 

The strategy here is to deemphasize the particular action for which Eratosthenes was supposedly being punished and emphasize his character as a person. Violence is acceptable against him because it’s not just that he has performed a criminal act, it’s that he is a criminal person.

            So with this setup—namely that the old woman has repeated to him the nature of Eratosthenes’ habitual crimes—Euphiletos can tell the story of how he killed Eratosthenes and expect the jury to sympathize with him rather than with Eratosthenes. And that’s exactly what he does. He goes through the whole story of how he found out and gathered up witnesses and set the trap, and then he killed him. It’s worth looking at these chapters of the speech in detail: [slides #20-22]

[24] We took torches from the nearest inn, and entered -- the door was open because the girl had seen to it. We pushed open the door of the bedroom, and those of us who were the first to enter saw him still lying next to my wife; the ones coming in later saw him standing naked on the bed.

 

[25] I struck him, gentlemen, and knocked him down. Then I twisted him round and tied his hands behind his back. I asked him why he was disgracing my house by entering it. He confessed that he was in the wrong, and he begged and entreated me not to kill him, but to agree to a financial settlement.

 

[26] I said to him, “Your executioner is not I, but the law of the city, whose violation you thought less important than your pleasures. It was your choice to commit an offence like this against my wife and my children, rather than to obey the laws and behave properly.”

 

And here Euphiletos—as boldly as can be!—twists the law to make it what he wants. He presents himself as the embodiment of the law and totally abdicates responsibility for the killing. But that’s not all. In doing so he represents his actions as carrying out justice imposed by the result of a graphe, a public case, when in fact murder was cause for a dike, a private case. In effect, he is both the accuser and the punisher, and according to him, he does so lawfully. But of course, it’s Euphiletos who is on trial for murder, a dike. It was a fairly common strategy in Athenian courts to represent a dike as though it involved the community like a graphe. This helped to take personal responsibility away from the litigants and create a sense of community with the jurors. And that’s just what Lysias does. Here Lysias represents Euphiletos as acting on behalf of the entire community. And here again we find a parallel with what Orestes said to Clytaemestra: that it was not Orestes but Clytaemestra herself who would kill her. This idea represents the violence of justice as not actually the responsibility of the perpetrator. Euphiletos does not want to see himself as a violent criminal, and he doesn’t want the jury to see him that way either. By representing this as though seduction were a cause for a public case involving the death penalty (a graphe) he can avoid personal responsibility and maintain the sense of community he has built with the jurors.

This even allows him to tell the story in such a way as would otherwise make him look pretty bad. He emphasizes that Eratosthenes was helpless and begged for his life: [slide #23]

 [29] He did not argue, gentlemen, but confessed that he was in the wrong; he begged and pleaded not to be killed, and was ready to pay money in recompense. I did not agree with his offer; I considered that the law of the city was the greater authority, and I exacted that penalty you considered the most just, and that you ordained against those who practise such crimes. Now bring forward the witnesses of these events.

 

Not only does this passage, like others, show how he wasn’t fighting but his victim was tied up and unarmed, but it also shows again that Lysias is asking the audience to interpret the law as though it said that the punishment for adultery was death. And remember, too, that the jury was composed of 500 ordinary Athenian citizen men, who would not be experts in the laws, and so probably relied on their gut instincts as much as anything when deciding the case.

And Lysias continues to push this interpretation: [slide #24]

[31] Moreover, the lawgiver so strongly believed this to be the right course of action in the case of married women that he imposed the same penalty even in the case of mistresses, who are worth less than wives. Yet it is clear that, had he any better form of redress than this for married women, he would have introduced it. As it was, he could not discover a more powerful deterrent than this in their case, and he decided that the same penalty should apply even in the case of mistresses. Please read this law, too.

 

I would argue that the law makes no distinction between mistresses and wives because in both instances a man who catches the illicit lovers in the act will be emotionally uncontrollable, but Lysias can’t argue that. After all, according to the speech, Euphiletos goes to some trouble to arrange the “catching in the act”. Instead, he argues that there would be a harsher penalty than death for seduction of another man’s wife, but there is no harsher penalty than death.

Actually, there may be some reason behind this claim. Remember that the exception in Draco’s law is not just for any mistress, but for a mistress kept for the purpose of producing legitimate children. Athenians were very concerned that only legitimate children of citizens could be citizens. Ancient Greek culture in general was very concerned with paternity, and since there were no genetic paternity tests, Greek men were always anxious to make sure that they knew their children were their own. It’s one of the reasons why women’s chastity and faithfulness is so much more important than men’s. So this is why, I think, the exception to the homicide law includes mistresses who might produce legitimate children. Athenian men, being very anxious about the paternity and inheritance rights of their legitimate children, could be expected to be especially upset if another man was sleeping with a woman who could produce legitimate children. It’s not all about love, but also about maintaining control of inheritance and making sure than another man’s children don’t inherit your property. All of which means that given the anxiety surrounding this issue in general, the Athenian lawgivers could be expected not to care very much if a seducer, a very hated category of man, got killed. And so even though the penalty for seduction isn’t death, by presenting Eratosthenes as a seducer, Lysias can expect that the jury will lose sympathy and think that maybe he did deserve to die.

            The considerable anxiety felt by Athenian men regarding illicit sexuality results in a series of complicated categories. The idea of ‘adultery’ refers to the action; the category of ‘seducer’ implies habitual behaviour. The seducer, in fact, could be more hated than a rapist, even a child rapist: [slide #25]

[32] You hear, gentlemen, that it lays down that if anyone rapes a free man or child, he owes double the damages. If he rapes a woman, in those cases that carry the penalty of death, he is liable at the same rate. Thus, gentlemen, rapists are thought to deserve a lighter penalty than seducers, because the law condemned the latter to death, but assigned double the amount of the damages to the former.

 

In actuality, the law prescribes a harsher penalty for rape than for seduction. A rapist owes double the damages, whatever those damages are determined to be (usually a financial payment). A seducer does not. Nevertheless, Lysias is counting on the anxiety over seduction to make it seem not absurd that a seducer should be killed while a rapist merely owes monetary damages. He takes us through some rhetorical leaps to explain: [slide #26]

[33] The assumption is that those who achieve their aims by force are hated by those they have violated, while seducers so corrupt the souls of their victims that they make other men’s wives more intimate with them than they are with their husbands. They make the whole house theirs, and it becomes unclear to which father the children belong, the husband or the seducer. Because of this the lawmaker assigned death as the penalty for seducers.

 

And here it is as boldly as can be. Lysias says, “The lawmaker assigned death as the penalty for seducers.” This is a total lie, but Lysias expects it to work, and there’s good reason to believe it did. He presents death as a judicial penalty prescribed by the law, but in actual fact it was simply a permissible extra-judicial killing that, according to Draco’s law, ought not to result in a homicide trial.

 

6. Conclusions

The reason I assigned us this case is not only that it deals with one of the most important laws in Athens, Draco’s homicide law, and that it shows us what is presumably a real court case and the ways lawyers in Athens interpreted the law, but also that it shows how much identity and representation matter in the idea of criminality. This takes us back to the kinds of things we looked at last week, in the Walklate readings about the modern field of criminology. By representing his victim as a hated seducer, and by twisting the law, Euphiletos (via Lysias) can get his jury to sympathize with him and be on his side, even though he tied up and killed an unarmed man while he begged for his life in front of a group of witnesses. It’s not the reality of the law that matters here, but the character of those involved—here it’s the question of who the jury believe to be the criminal, and whom they believe is the victim.

            I want to look at one last passage from Lysias 1, and that’s on a slightly different topic. Near the end of the speech, Lysias has Euphiletos explain to the jury that they must acquit him or else the laws will be meaningless. He suggests that social disorder will result if he is convicted, and the argument he uses is an interesting one: [slide #27]

[36] I expect you to come to the same conclusion. Otherwise, you will create such a safe haven for seducers that you will find thieves claiming to be seducers in complete confidence that, if they put forward this excuse for themselves, and claim that this is why they are entering other people’s homes, no one will lay a finger on them. Everyone will know that the laws on adultery must be renounced, and that what they have to fear is your vote, because it is the supreme authority in the state.

 

Here, Lysias suggests that unless seducers are deterred by the threat of capital punishment, they will infest Athens. What I find most interesting about this is that Lysias is suggesting that potential seducers will make a rational cost/benefit analysis in deciding whether to commit a crime. This fits with rational choice theory, and it doesn’t really cast seducers as irredeemably immoral. In other words, he’s no longer arguing here that seducers are so bad they deserve to die to protect the community, but that Athens should protect itself by means of deterrence. This is a different kind of approach, as it’s no longer a positivist approach on the characteristics of a class of criminals, but now a seeming application of Rational Choice Theory. This passage, and others like it in the speech, has a very modern resonance for me.

            So, this speech has given us some insight into more similarities and differences between ancient and modern crime. On the one hand, the speech shows how much criminality in the ancient world is a matter of identity rather than action, and I think we can see this in modern criminology as well, particularly in positivist approaches—that certain people are criminal because of quantifiable characteristics. Moreover, it shows the limits of the legal view of crime, since here the technicalities of the law are overridden by cultural norms and perceptions. But we’ve also seen that, at least to some extent, Athenians were concerned with inheritance and sexual behaviour in a way that led them to believe that a crime of seduction could be a capital offense, i.e., worthy of summary execution. You might take some time to think about whether we today also link sexual morality and criminality like the Athenians did. Let me leave you with some food for thought: to what extent does our society think that people, especially women, who have active sex lives with multiple partners, deserve to be victims of violence? To what extent do people think that so-called “family men” are good people and not violent criminals? The ancient Athenians thought that people with immoral sex lives, like seducers, were bad people who deserved the violence they suffered. We can see when looking at this speech from a distance that that’s twisting the law, but I think we see the same kinds of twisting of the law happen today.
