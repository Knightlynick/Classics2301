2. Introduction         

[slide #2] For this week I’ve assigned a few chapters of Sandra Walklate’s book, Criminology: The Basics. It’s not a perfect book, but it has what we need for this class. It provides an introduction to the modern field of study; I want you to have some idea of the way modern criminologists think about crime and punishment, and how their ideas have developed over time. One of contentions I posit in this class is that although there’s nothing called “criminology” in antiquity, the literary, rhetorical, and philosophical texts of ancient Greece and Rome often tackle the same kinds of questions about crime and punishment that modern criminology does, but the ancient texts get at these questions in a different way. Questions such as, “What is crime? What causes crime? How can we prevent crime? What is the best thing to do after a crime has been committed?” are problems for antiquity as well as for modernity. As we discuss the readings from Walklate, I’d like you to focus on two major aspects and—for practical purposes—you should know that I will expect you to be familiar with them for testing purposes [slide #3]: first, the different approaches to aspects of criminological inquiry and second, the development of criminology as a field of study, including the major developments and the names of the scholars/scientists responsible for them.

 

3. Chapter 1: What is Criminology?

            [slide #4] In the first chapter of the assigned reading, “What is Criminology?” Walklate argues that criminology is an extraordinarily flexible subject because it’s possible to approach it from a number of perspectives.

 

4. Five Approaches to Crime and Criminology

She notes especially the following: First, a legal approach to criminology defines crime as law breaking behavior. This is a good approach and even necessary, since the law is a necessary benchmark in determining what is and what is not a crime in criminology, but it’s not the be-all and end-all. For one thing, laws vary from place to place and over time, and so the legal approach to criminology is very culturally and historically specific. To take just one example, up until 2018 the sale of cannabis products in Canada was illegal, but after the federal government passed a law regulating its sale, it was legal. The same goes for smoking in restaurants: legal until 2006 when the Ontario government passed a law rendering it illegal. This historical and geographical specificity is as much an advantage for us in looking at ancient crime and punishment as it is a limitation though, because it means that one of the things we can discuss in this class is what precisely the laws, like, for example, Draco’s homicide law, prohibited in a specific ancient place and time. The limitations occur when we try to find a broader common ground between ancient and modern ideas of criminality (compare the distinctions we made earlier between ideas of civil and criminal law in both periods). The legal approach to criminology leaves out questions like the extent to which ancient and modern cultures conceive of the category of crime and the nature of criminals independently of specific laws.

            Another approach to criminology is the moral approach. This approach defines crime as what is morally wrong, and that may or may not be against specific laws. Take the idea of jaywalking for example. There are laws that state that pedestrians must cross roads at marked crosswalks or intersections, and only when the walk signal allows it. If I cross the street against the signal, it’s a violation of the law, but I don’t think it’s especially immoral. And even if I have occasionally crossed against the light, I don’t think of myself as a criminal, and I would hope you don’t think of me that way either. On the other hand, some people and acts that we may think of as criminal are legally innocent. Just think of corporations or billionaires who use the laws (and legal loopholes) to maximize profit often at the expense of other people (e.g. Big Pharma and the opioid crisis). These people are acting criminally from a moral perspective, but not breaking laws. And even if you don’t think of such people as morally criminal, the idea of criminality is tied up with the idea of morality at the very least to the extent that we think of people we would categorize as criminals as people who are also immoral.

            There is also a social approach to criminology. This one is much like the moral approach, but emphasizes the socially specific nature of the category of crime. In ancient Greek, you’ll remember, the word for law is nomos, which also just means custom. That means that linguistically, the ancient Greeks didn’t make much distinction between criminal behavior as law-breaking or simply violating social norms. Both kinds of acts simply went against nomos. Especially in early Greek law, judges made decisions based on custom and what they thought was just and right rather than referring to a specific body of laws. This is a social approach to crime, defining crime as behavior that violates social norms whether or not it breaks the law. One way of thinking about it might be something like the social norm that one stands on the right side of an escalator to allow people in a hurry to walk up the stairs as the escalator moves. This is a social norm, but there’s no law stating that it’s required.

            Closely related to the moral approach is what Walklate calls the humanistic approach to crime. The idea here is that there are certain basic human rights that transcend the specifics of culture, and that violations of human rights are criminal whether or not they violate specific laws or social customs. One of the advantages of the humanistic approach to crime is that it’s universal. It defines the criminal in the same way for all of humanity, irrespective of time and place. But this is also limiting, for relatively few basic human rights would fit into such a framework. The humanistic approach presents us with some very interesting questions. The ancient Athenians, for example, kept slaves, violating the human rights of a large number of people. Was this criminal behavior? It certainly wasn’t against the law, so it’s not criminal in a legal sense. Nor did it violate social customs. It may have been morally wrong, and it may have violated the human rights of the slaves. If there were ancient Athenians living somewhere today, we might even say that they were international war criminals for enslaving prisoners of war. But I don’t know whether this really makes them criminals. Can we apply our own standards of judgment to the Athenians? Some might say that we have to judge them on their own terms, but I don’t think we can excuse slavery on those grounds. It’s not like they didn’t know any better, they were people who knew they were hurting other people.

And finally, an approach closely related to the social one, in that it involves the community as a whole, is the social constructionist approach to crime. On the one hand, the moral and humanistic approaches to crime may suggest that there is a natural, universal, or essential aspect to crime that doesn’t change culturally or historically. On the other, the social and social constructionist approaches argue instead that there is nothing natural, universal, or essential about the idea of crime, and that it is rather a part of the social fabric of communities. It is something created by the people who run the society. Moreover, the people who are in charge, the powerful, get to define what is ‘criminal’, and they’re most successful at doing that when it seems natural or universal. If, for example, the people in charge determine that women should be stay-at-home-moms, and make rules to prevent them from working outside the home, eventually that will seem like a natural role for women, and it would be subversive (if not downright criminal) for women to do otherwise.

One of the classic examples that ancient historians use to illustrate the power of the social construction of basic ideas and categories is the idea of homosexuality. We think of sexual orientation as being a part of a person’s identity. In classical Athens, the categories that defined sexuality were not about the gender of your sexual partner, but rather by the specific sexual acts you wanted to perform. Those who enjoyed being the active partner in penetrative sex belonged in one category, and those who enjoyed being penetrated were in another. If an ancient Athenian enjoyed penetrating, it mattered some, but not much, whether he preferred women or men. The Athenians recognized that some people preferred women and some preferred men, but they just didn’t care very much about that (and note that women’s sexuality as a whole is mostly unrecorded). If we compare modern categories of sexuality, we have labels such as gay/lesbian/straight/bisexual/queer/trans (and others), based on the identity of our sexual partners. In a way, then, the idea of homosexuality as a concept simply didn’t exist in classical Athens. The Athenians thought of sexuality with different categories than the ones we use today. Thus even fundamental categories that we think of as natural and unchangeable can change over time. Identity is a powerful thing, and to identify as gay or straight (or queer, or trans, or in none of these categories) is a big deal today. It’s a huge component of what makes us who we are. Modern society (in Western culture) largely argues that sexuality forms a part of one’s identity. And yet, in ancient Greece what we think of as ‘gay’ or ‘straight’ or ‘queer’ or ‘bi’ didn’t really exist.

In terms of crime and punishment, the parallel of homosexuality is important because the concept of crime seems to operate in the same way. The French philosopher Foucault, who was one of the first proponents of the idea that the concept of homosexuality is a recent invention, makes precisely this kind of social constructionist argument about crime and punishment. For the purposes of our class, one way in which he argues this is true is in the very concept of punishment. We have “correctional facilities” and we treat criminals as people who can be corrected, or fixed. Foucault argues that this is a modern idea, and that the spectacle of punishment in the premodern world is about fixing a problem with the community by getting rid of a criminal rather than about fixing a problem with the individual criminal. In other words, in the modern world criminality is about an individual, the criminal, while in the ancient world criminality was about the community. The modern prison seeks to correct the criminal, while in the ancient world the practice of sending a criminal into exile healed the community without caring much what happened to the criminal. And just like sexuality, criminality is for our modern society a part of identity. We think of people as criminals, and we think of ourselves as not being criminals. Crime isn’t just about a criminal act; it’s also about a criminal person. Think about the examples of people who say, “I’m not a racist, but …” and then go on to say something racist. Nobody wants to identify openly as a racist (or at least very few people do) because it goes against social norms of what is acceptable behavior. They don’t want that to be part of their identity, but they still hold racist views. And the same thing happens with criminals. Criminals may see themselves as good people who made a mistake, but others see them as bad people. Just as there’s a difference between saying racist things and being a racist, there’s a difference between doing criminal things and being a criminal. All of the above discussion comes back to this basic idea: for social constructionists, cultures and communities define not only what acts are criminal, but also what people are criminals.

 

5. 19th and 20th Century Criminology

[slide #5] A key figure in the development of modern criminology for both Walklate and Foucault is the nineteenth century Italian prison psychiatrist Cesare Lombroso. Lombroso was a biological positivist*, which means that he wanted to study crime scientifically, and he believed that criminals were biologically different from other people. He measured things like the distance between people’s eyes to try to collect data on the physical characteristics of criminals. Even though this may seem absurd to us now, both Foucault and Walklate argue that some remains of Lombroso’s ideas are still working behind the scenes in criminology. We still associate certain physical characteristics with criminality, and as much as we don’t like to admit it, there is considerable racism in popular depictions of crime. And even when we don’t work with physical characteristics, there is also sociological positivism, which still takes the scientific approach, but refocuses it on the social characteristics of criminals—that is, aspects of relations between an individual and their community. One of the key ideas in the early sociological positivist criminology that developed in the Chicago School in the early twentieth century was the idea of the zone of transition, or what we would now call the “inner city”. The Chicago School identified this inner city as a locus of greater social problems, including crime. But we should ask ourselves: how different is the idea that there is a correlation between crime and inner city youth from claims that whether someone is a criminal can be determined via their physical characteristics? And yet, it doesn’t seem nearly as absurd to us now as measuring the distance between people’s eyes or feeling the bumps on their heads.

Lombroso’s legacy survives in other ways, too. One of these is in the concept of psychological positivism. This is a scientific approach to crime that says that it’s not the physical, but rather the psychological characteristics of a person that make that person criminal or not. This still takes a scientific approach and, like Lombroso, suggests that it is primarily internal factors rather than external ones that determine crime, or in other words, the focus of this kind of study is on the criminal rather than the crime itself. Certain people are criminal people for one reason or another, and they will commit crimes. They have criminal minds. And so it’s fair to say that modern criminology is shaped by positivist approaches developed within the last few centuries and still bears the legacy of such men as Lombroso, even if we no longer ascribe to his exact ideas. The past informs our present and it also works the other way around. In terms of ancient societies, it’s fair to ask how much is our thinking likewise unconsciously shaped by specifically modern categories that don’t apply to ancient Greece and Rome?

Here’s an interesting example from ancient Greece, an illustration of how we can compare modern criminology with an ancient idea. In the Iliad there’s a scene where Achilles and Agamemnon are having an argument in front of the council over the distribution of war captives. Achilles is about to draw his sword, but the goddess Athena appears behind him and urges him to stop and use his words rather than violence. Nobody can see her, but Achilles does what she wants and doesn’t take violent action. Because nobody can see Athena and she only has influence on Achilles in this scene, we might view her not as an external force, but as an aspect of Achilles’ psychology. Athena is a goddess of practical wisdom, and she manifests in the Iliad as an external representation of Achilles’ practical wisdom, preventing him from killing Agamemnon. In this way, we get an insight into the psychology of Achilles, here only a potential rather than actual violent criminal. Like modern criminology, and psychological positivism in particular, the Iliad looks for causes of violence and ways to prevent it in the psychology of people—it merely expresses these ideas through the imagery of the Olympian gods rather than psychological theory.

We’ve also seen some psychology in Greek tragedy. In the Oresteia, we saw the Furies pursuing Orestes, and from a psychological perspective, I would argue that they were an external representation of Orestes’ feelings of guilt. We could at various points see the characters feeling torn about what was right and what was wrong, and the chorus commented on the process of determining right and wrong. As we continue to look at criminology, I’m going to argue that the Oresteia is a kind of ancient criminology, a trilogy of plays that looks at the causes of and solutions for crime, and the representation of psychological forces in the Furies, like the representation of Achilles’ psychology in Athena, suggests that the Greeks also saw the psychology of the criminal as a contributing factor to crime. In other words, one of the driving forces of Greek myth is to ask the same kinds of questions that modern psychology does. The gods in Greek myth can be read as representations of internal, psychological forces, and in that way the mythical story of the Oresteia is also a psychological inquiry into crime and punishment.

We can only take this so far, though. Modern psychology and neuroscience are far more scientific and direct than the explorations of criminal psychology in the Iliad and the Oresteia. They’re also a lot more extensive. This makes criminology as a discipline a particularly modern phenomenon, and to the extent that we think of criminality as influenced or even defined by the psychology of an individual criminal that idea stems more from nineteenth century positivism than the myths of ancient Greece and Rome, even if the root of the ideas is similar.

Positivism operates all through criminology. One way Walklate points out that our thinking is shaped by positivist criminology is in what she calls the “criminological other.” Positivist criminology, by associating personal characteristics with criminality, privileges some kinds of crime and some kinds of criminals over others. That is, they take up a lot more space in the public imagination and pop culture representation in society. In general, crime is a concept that evokes thinking of violent crimes committed by young men often in ethnic or racial minorities and in urban environments. Some of the characteristics of the criminal are biological, some sociological, and some psychological, but the positivist focus on the nature of the criminal person conditions the way we think about crime.

Many of these characteristics or categories were very different in the ancient world. Since what we’ve read so far is the Oresteia, let’s think about the example of classical Athens and how it’s different from modern concepts. This was a city, but without an “inner city” or industrially urban environment like in modern cities. There were immigrants and ethnic minorities, but these people were not the focus of the courts or the legal system. Rather, all of the evidence we have points to the focus of Athenian criminal law on wealthy citizen men. It may be the case that this is because our historical record is incomplete. We don’t have a lot of evidence for the lives of enslaved or poor people, children, or women, and so these people are left out of our record of ancient thinking about crime. But I don’t think that accounts for all of it. If, in the modern world, we associate crime with individuals who are poor and members of minority cultural groups, in classical Athens it was rich white men who were the focus of criminological thought. You can see this in action in the Oresteia, as the ‘criminals’ were  in fact the ruling family of Argos. In one case, one of the criminals was a woman, but she was presented as very manly. Moreover, her victim, Agamemnon, was not only feminized, but made into an ethnic other as a part of his victimization. We’ll see next time when we look at a murder trial from Athens that the litigants are all wealthy citizen men as well. This is a big difference between ancient and modern thinking about crime, but in both cases there are more and less visible types of crime and criminals. In the ancient world the visible types of criminals are wealthy and powerful men, but in the modern world the visible types of criminals are poor men belonging to ethnic or racial minorities.

 

6. Chapter 4: The Search for Criminological Explanation

In Walklate’s first chapter, “What is Criminology?”, the focus is on the history of the discipline as it developed over the last couple of centuries, and in particular, on how criminologists have thought about criminals. [slide #6] The next chapter I asked you to read was Chapter 4, “The Search for Criminological Explanation.” There, Walklate again discusses criminological theory, but focuses not so much on the history of the various theories, but on how they stand today. Moreover, her focus in this chapter is less on the nature of criminals and more on the nature of crime itself and criminal acts. The first theory she discusses is Rational Choice Theory. Simply put, this is the idea that a person commits a crime as a rational decision, weighing the potential benefits against the risks. This is a powerful explanation for many kinds of crime, but it has a couple of gaps in what it can explain. First, many criminals are not exactly rational, and anyway the definition of rational can vary. Second, it doesn’t directly address the deeper question of why some people are in a situation where they might rationally decide to commit a crime. Nevertheless, it can work pretty well as an explanation. Taking the Oresteia as an example, Orestes might have chosen to kill his mother and Aegisthus by making a rational cost/benefit analysis. He’d get to be king of Argos and he’d avenge his father, but he’d have to kill his mother and maybe get attacked by Furies. And even though Aeschylus doesn’t put it in those terms, all of the teeth gnashing and wavering decision making in The Libation Bearers could be read this way. (Note from Dr. Nousek: Keep an eye out for passages in the primary source readings that suggest that crime is a rational choice.)

The next theory Walklate discusses is Social Control Theory. Like Rational Choice Theory, Social Control Theory focuses less on internal characteristics of the criminal and more on the act of crime itself; but whereas Rational Choice Theory still works on the individual’s decision making process, Social Control theory rather looks at the societal factors surrounding the individual that lead to crime. The two are not incompatible. They’re just two different approaches or ways of thinking. Social Control Theory is in large part about what sorts of factors in an individual’s life and relationships would prevent criminal behavior. Travis Hirschi (Walklate, pp. 89-90) argued that there were four main factors involved: attachment, commitment, involvement, and belief. Attachment is about the relationships a person has with a group. The more connected a person is to other people, the less likely they are to commit crime. Commitment is a measure of how much a person is invested in those relationships. Involvement is not about relationships with other people, but about being involved in non-criminal activities. The more one does besides crime, the less one does crime. Finally, belief is a measure of how much a person believes, often thanks to upbringing, that crime is something people shouldn’t do. None of these things fully describes the moment of crime in the way that Rational Choice Theory might, but they are non-rational factors that have some influence over that moment.

We can use Social Control Theory to analyze the Oresteia as well. Orestes has been cut off from his family and so has a lack of attachment and commitment. He’s been exiled from the city of Argos, which he might one day have ruled, and he’s not really doing much with his life, so he has a lack of involvement. Apollo has told him it’s not wrong to kill his mother, so he has a lack of belief, although his hesitation to kill his mother as well as his expectation that the Furies would appear to drive him mad even before they show up suggests he does at least a little bit believe that what he does is wrong. So given the lack of controlling factors, we shouldn’t be surprised that Orestes kills his mother. From another point of view, since Orestes is ultimately judged innocent of any crime, we might say that the absence of attachment to his birth family has made him less likely to follow their criminal beliefs and habits—remember, of course, that Agamemnon’s whole family tree had been involved in the cycle of crime and vengeance since the time of Tantalus. Either way, Orestes’ relationships with other people are a factor in his actions as ‘criminal’ or as a ‘just punisher of criminals’.

The third theory to explain crime that Walklate discusses is Relative Deprivation. Relative Deprivation takes the idea that people commit crime because other people are better off and criminals want to even things out. She generalizes it by saying that anybody, no matter how well off, can feel deprived of something in relation to some other group. Orestes is a rich white guy, and he’s doing okay, but he might feel deprived of his rightful place as ruler of Argos. Certainly Clytaemestra fits this category as well: she’s been deprived of her child (Iphigeneia) because Agamemnon sacrificed her to get favourable winds to Troy, and then when Agamemnon comes home, she’s deprived of her “rightful” place in his bed, displaced by the war-captive-sex-slave Cassandra. Relative deprivation is not just about money, but about feeling deprived of what you believe is yours by right. Aeschylus doesn’t write it that way explicitly, but it fits with the story. This idea doesn’t contradict Rational Choice Theory or Social Control Theory, but it adds another largely irrational factor to the criminal decision making process (i.e. why people commit crimes).

One final aspect of criminal explanation that Walklate suggests in this chapter is the concept of Hegemonic Masculinity. It operates both in modern society and in antiquity. This one answers the question of why it’s Orestes (as a man) and not his sister Electra (a woman) who kills Clytaemestra and Aegisthus. Orestes, as a man, has much more to gain and much more social pressure to be the one to kill Clytaemestra than Electra does. Electra is expected, as a woman, to accept her circumstances rather than fight them. Compare Clytaemestra: when she refuses to accept her circumstances and her relative deprivation and instead decides to fight back and kill Agamemnon, she is violating the usual gender roles and overcoming social pressure to be submissive. And it’s not just in Greek tragedy that that’s the case. Today, men are much more likely to be involved in crime either as criminals OR victims in large part because of the social expectations of their gender. Men are believed to be violent, and women are not, and this leads men to be more involved in violent crime.

These four ideas, namely Rational Choice Theory, Social Control Theory, Relative Deprivation, and Hegemonic Masculinity, do not provide a complete picture of what causes people to commit crimes, but they do form a good part of that picture. Keep them in mind as the semester goes on and we read more ancient sources. Look out for passages that suggest that ancient authors had similar explanations for why the criminals they discuss decided to commit their crimes.

 

7. Chapter 5: Thinking about the Victim of Crime

So far I’ve talked about Biological Positivism, Sociological Positivism, Psychological Positivism, Rational Choice Theory, Social Control Theory, Relative Deprivation, and Hegemonic Masculinity. [That’s a lot of theories, and yes, you should know them! – Dr. Nousek] All of these ideas focus on the perpetrator of crime. Walklate, however also has some things to say about the victims of crime. [slide #7] Many of the things we can say about criminals also apply to victims. We might, with the positivists, argue that victims of crime have certain physical, social, or psychological characteristics that make them more likely to be victims of crime. We might also talk about the rational decision making processes and social pressures that lead criminals to choose a particular victim. What Walklate spends the most time on is a discussion of Hegemonic Masculinity and its role in victimization. In particular, while young, poor, minority men who do not live with family are statistically the most likely to be victims of crime, as victims they are largely invisible. That is, they are statistically likely but under-perceived. On the other hand women, and especially elderly women or wealthy women who live with families are not very likely to be victims of crime, but are highly visible victims when they are. This is in large part because just as we associate criminality with certain types of people, we also associate victimhood with certain types of people. And we can see this working in the Oresteia, when the perpetrator of a crime, Clytaemestra, becomes masculine through her crime, while she feminizes her victim, Agamemnon. And Clytaemestra is at her most feminine when she is pleading with Orestes that she is his mother and he shouldn’t kill her. So in the Oresteia, people are most masculine when they are doing violence, and most feminine when they are victims of violence. This doesn’t mean that in real life all men are criminals and all women are victims. In actuality, it’s men who are much more likely to be victims of violent crime. What it does mean is that we associate the characteristics of criminals with men, and the characteristics of victims with women. In writing about Hegemonic Masculinity Walklate is discussing the perceptions and expectations of who is likely to be a perpetrator or a victim. The label of ‘Other’—whether it is applied to the ‘criminological other’ or the ‘victimological other’ represents the opposite of what society generally expects. A criminological ‘other’ might be, for instance, a little old lady (that is, someone not expected to be a perpetrator of crime). A victimological ‘other’ might be a man who was a victim of domestic violence (since society normally associates that role with women).
