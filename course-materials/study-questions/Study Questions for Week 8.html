Study Questions

These questions are meant to direct your studying for tests and the final exam. When you consider an answer, don't think about only one answer for each question, but rather consider the many different things you've learned that could help answer the question. These questions should get you thinking broadly about the material and how the smaller details fit together and help us build an analysis of how the ancient Greeks and Romans thought about crime and punishment.

  1. What are 'The Five Things' for this week's text?
  2. Who are the main characters/speakers in the dialogue?
  3. What is meant by 'rhetoric'? How does it apply to crime and punishment?
  4. Who is Gorgias and how does he relate to Socrates, in terms of their teachings?
  5. Who are Polus and Callicles? What arguments do they advance? How does Socrates counter them?
  6. What criminological theories are evident in the arguments and ideas presented in this dialogue? What does this tell us about the ways ancient Greeks thought about crime, punishment, justice, and criminality?
  7. Can the speakers be associated with criminological concepts that you learned from reading Walklate and the lecture on modern criminology?
