## Core Themes You Should See Everywhere

### 1. Crime is not the same thing in antiquity and modernity
- Modern systems sharply separate criminal law and civil law, rely on police and public prosecutors, and use imprisonment as a central punishment.
- Ancient Athens and Rome distinguished public and private matters, but the line was much narrower and much stranger. Many things modern people call crimes were handled as private wrongs.
- In classical Athens, homicide was not a public crime in the modern sense. In Rome, many violent acts remained outside a strong state criminal apparatus for a very long time.

### 2. Self-help matters
- Ancient societies expected households, kin groups, patrons, and private citizens to respond to wrongs.
- Law often regulated self-help rather than replacing it.
- Greek homicide law and Roman patron-client practice both show that the state did not monopolize force in the modern way.

### 3. Rhetoric shapes criminality
- Ancient law-court speeches do not just prove facts. They construct identities.
- Lysias turns Euphiletos into a modest victim and Eratosthenes into a corrupt seducer.
- Cicero turns Caelius into a charming young hero and Clodia into a scandalous villain.
- Sallust frames Catiline in contradictory ways: criminal, aristocrat, revolutionary, enemy, and heroic commander.

### 4. Crime is often treated as a matter of character, not just action
- Modern positivist criminology looks for causes of criminality in biology, environment, and psychology.
- Ancient texts often do something similar: they explain crime through ancestry, upbringing, moral weakness, gender, class position, and desire.
- This matters especially in Weeks 11 and 12.

### 5. Insider versus outsider is a recurring structure
- Criminals are cast as threats to the community.
- Punishment often removes them from the community: exile in antiquity, imprisonment in modernity.
- But the dangerous figure in this course is often an insider: a citizen, aristocrat, householder, wife, husband, politician, or general.

### 6. State violence develops over time
- In both Greece and Rome, early law coexists with private retaliation.
- Over time, institutions become more formal.
- In Rome especially, the state gradually centralizes legitimate violence, and the law on `vis` becomes a key turning point in that story.

## How to use ancient/modern comparisons on the exam
Do not treat the course as a hunt for "the first version of our system." A stronger answer does three things at once:
- it names the ancient institution or argument on its own terms;
- it explains what work that institution performs in its own society;
- it then compares that function, not just the label, to a modern criminal-justice equivalent.

That means a good comparison does not simply say that the `quaestio perpetua` is "like a criminal court" or that Draco's law is "like self-defense." A better comparison says that both ancient and modern systems try to channel violence into recognizable procedures, but they do so with different assumptions about who may initiate action, what counts as public harm, and how much the state controls punishment.

## Week 1: Introduction to Crime and Punishment in Ancient Greece and Rome

### What Week 1 is really doing
Week 1 is not just an introduction. It sets the method of the course. The main claim is that modern categories like "crime," "criminal justice," "punishment," "democracy," and even "prison" do not map neatly onto ancient Greece and Rome.

### Key examples and why they matter
- Ned Stark executing a deserter: judgment and punishment are not separated.
- Salem witch trials: formal procedure can still be unjust.
- Omar Khadr: one event can generate both criminal and civil consequences.
- Mike Duffy: public scandal, criminal process, and civil litigation can all diverge.

### Ancient case study: Publius Claudius Pulcher and the sacred chickens
- Date: 249 BCE, First Punic War.
- Claudius ignored bad bird omens before attacking at Drepana.
- He mocked the sacred chickens, attacked anyway, lost badly, and was prosecuted.
- The case mixes religion, war, politics, procedure, and punishment.
- It shows that what counts as a punishable public wrong in Rome includes religious failure, not just modern-style criminal conduct.

### Major concepts
- Public case versus private case
- Criminal law versus civil law
- Self-help
- Exile versus imprisonment
- Parricide and the `culleus`
- Foucault and historical discontinuity

### High-yield takeaways
- Ancient Athens and Rome did distinguish public from private matters, but the categories were narrower than in modern law.
- Homicide in Athens was not prosecuted the way a modern state prosecutes murder.
- Ancient prisons were generally temporary holding places, not long-term correctional institutions.
- Exile is one of the most important ancient punishments.

### Study questions answered
1. What comes to mind when you think about crime and criminals?
   The course wants you to realize that modern answers are shaped by media, police, courts, and prisons, not by universal truths.
2. Where do we get information about crimes and criminals?
   Mostly through popular culture, news, legal drama, social media, and highly mediated public stories.
3. How are modern and ancient justice systems similar?
   Both distinguish serious public wrongs from private disputes, use formal procedures, and try to protect the community.
4. How are they different?
   Ancient systems rely much more on kinship, self-help, exile, status, and weak state enforcement.

### Deep study layer
#### What this lecture is arguing
Week 1 is not just course introduction. It is a warning against category mistakes. The lecture argues that if you bring modern assumptions about crime, prison, police, prosecution, appeal, and rights directly into Greece and Rome, you will misunderstand almost every later reading. The point of the opening modern examples is not that antiquity and modernity are identical. It is that even modern systems already blur criminal and civil law, emergency power, media spectacle, and political maneuvering, so we should expect even bigger category problems in antiquity.

#### Concept bubbles
- `Self-help`: what it does
  Self-help is the right or expectation that a wronged person, family, or patron will respond directly rather than waiting for a professional state apparatus. In antiquity, law often regulates self-help instead of replacing it.
- `Criminal versus civil`: what it does
  In a modern system this split channels some harms toward public prosecution and others toward private compensation. The lecture shows that ancient systems often sort harms differently, so the split exists but does not map neatly onto modern categories.
- `Exile versus imprisonment`: what it does
  Modern states often remove offenders from society by long-term incarceration. Ancient societies more often protected the community by expelling, fining, disabling, or executing rather than warehousing people in prisons.

#### Why this matters for crime and punishment
The course is asking you to study not just ancient laws but ancient ways of imagining wrongdoing. Once you see that the category "crime" is historically unstable, you can understand why adultery, homicide, sacrilege, debt conspiracy, and political violence look so different from week to week.

#### Ancient vs modern comparison
- The Omar Khadr case shows how modern states also blur citizen, enemy, soldier, and criminal.
- The Mike Duffy example shows that one factual controversy can produce both criminal and civil proceedings.
- The Claudius Pulcher story shows that Roman public wrongdoing could combine religion, military disaster, assembly procedure, and something like retrial rules.

The modern parallel is not that Rome had "military courts like ours." The better point is that both societies can use extraordinary procedures when the alleged wrong touches public safety, religion, or the state, but they justify those procedures differently.

#### Exam trap
Do not write that ancient Greece and Rome "had the same justice system as us, just older." The lecture is structured to make exactly that answer impossible.

## Week 2: Historical Background and Context

### Why this week matters
Week 2 gives you the chronological map required for the whole course. If you cannot place authors, legal reforms, and political systems in the right order, later weeks become confusing.

### Greek periods you should know
- Neolithic
- Bronze Age
- Dark Age
- Archaic
- Classical
- Hellenistic

### Roman periods you should know
- Regal Period
- Republican Period
- Imperial Period
- Later Roman Empire

### High-yield anchors
- Draco and Solon are Archaic, before tragedy and before Plato.
- Aeschylus belongs to the early Classical period.
- Socrates and Plato belong to the later Classical period.
- The Roman law material in Weeks 10-12 belongs mainly to the Republic and early Empire, but codification reaches late antiquity.

### Study questions answered
1. What are the major systems of dating chronology for antiquity?
   BCE/CE dating plus period labels like Archaic, Classical, Hellenistic, Regal, Republic, and Empire.
2. What are the major periods of Greek history?
   Neolithic, Bronze Age, Dark Age, Archaic, Classical, Hellenistic.
3. What are the major periods of Roman history?
   Regal, Republic, Empire, later empire.
4. When did Greek culture emerge in the Mediterranean?
   In the Bronze Age and especially again after the Dark Age with polis formation and colonization.
5. How did Greek culture develop politically?
   From small communities and aristocratic structures toward poleis, tyrannies, reforms, and in some places democracy.
6. Which wars shaped Greek and Roman history?
   Persian Wars, Peloponnesian War, Punic Wars, Roman civil wars.
7. What is meant by "Hellenistic"?
   The post-Alexander Greek world, after 323 BCE.
8. When did Romans control the Mediterranean?
   Gradually through the middle and late Republic, especially after the Punic Wars.
9. What connections can you draw between Greek and Roman culture?
   Roman culture borrows heavily from Greek culture, but Roman legal and political development remains distinct.

### Deep study layer
#### What this lecture is arguing
Week 2 argues that chronology is part of interpretation. You cannot explain a law, a play, a speech, or a conspiracy if you cannot place it inside the right political world. A law from an early city-state, a tragedy from democratic Athens, and a Roman speech from the late Republic do not belong to the same institutional environment.

#### Concept bubbles
- `Periodization`: what it does
  Periodization breaks history into usable segments. It is an interpretive tool, not a fact of nature. It helps you connect ideas of crime and punishment to larger political and social changes.
- `Historical context`: what it does
  Context explains why a text assumes certain institutions, anxieties, and audiences. In this course, context is what stops you from confusing Aeschylean tragedy with Ciceronian advocacy or Roman imperial law.

#### Why this matters for crime and punishment
If you know the period, you know what legal machinery is even available. Classical Athens can give you citizen juries and litigant speeches; late Republican Rome can give you praetors, `quaestiones`, juristic development, and emergency decrees. This is why the timeline is not filler. It is the backbone that makes the later comparisons coherent.

#### Ancient vs modern comparison
Modern students often imagine legal systems as relatively stable national structures. Antiquity is less stable. Regime change, democratization, aristocratic conflict, conquest, and empire frequently alter how wrongdoing is classified and punished. The closest modern parallel is not a single legal code but a system repeatedly changed by constitutional crisis, war, and state-building.

#### Exam trap
Avoid floating names without periods. "Draco," "Aeschylus," "Socrates," "Cicero," and "Justinian" are not just names to memorize; they belong to different institutional worlds.

## Week 3: Introduction to Greek Law

### What this lecture teaches
Week 3 gives the vocabulary and institutional structure for Greek law, especially Athenian law. It is one of the densest lectures in the course.

### Core Greek legal ideas
- Earliest Greek dispute resolution often involves arbitration, compensation, and vengeance.
- Written law becomes important in the Archaic period.
- Law emerges alongside political reform.

### Key terms
- `nomos`: law, custom, convention
- `thesmos`: law laid down
- `dike`: often private suit or justice
- `graphe`: public prosecution
- `Heliaia`: people's court system
- archons
- Areopagus
- boule
- assembly
- juries
- lethal self-help

### Draco and Solon
- Draco is associated especially with homicide law.
- Solon is associated with broader reform and with appeal rights.
- The important point is not "Draco always imposed death," but that Athenian law formalized when force and homicide were legally permitted or forbidden.

### Private versus public cases
- Private cases are brought by the injured party or family.
- Public cases are brought in the name of the community.
- This distinction is crucial for understanding why homicide can be legally important without being a public crime in the modern sense.

### Typical Athenian jury trial
- No professional judge in the modern sense.
- Large citizen juries.
- Timed speeches.
- No lawyers speaking for you in the modern sense; litigants speak, though they may use speechwriters like Lysias.
- Heavy dependence on rhetoric.

<!-- START WEEK 3 MIDTERM FRAME -->
### Lecture frame in plain English
Week 3 argues that Greek law grows out of custom, arbitration, and feud management rather than a modern state monopoly on prosecution. The procedural topic shows that Athenian institutions distribute legal authority across assemblies, councils, magistrates, and large citizen juries.

This vocabulary is the foundation for the Greek legal and literary weeks that follow. Knowing who does what lets you explain how a case actually moves through the Athenian system. The difference explains why ancient litigants spend so much effort constructing character. Athenian procedure makes persuasive speech part of adjudication itself.

### Key concepts in paragraph form
**nomos.** Law, custom, or accepted social norm. It ties law to shared social practice rather than only to statute. It helps explain why Greek law often shades into morality and custom.

**Lethal self-help.** Legally bounded private violence used in response to certain serious wrongs. It channels retaliation without eliminating it. It prepares you for Draco's homicide law and the logic behind Lysias 1.

**graphe versus dike.** A public action versus a more private action tied to the injured party. The distinction tells you whose interest the law recognizes and who gets to bring the case. It is central for explaining why some grave harms are not public crimes in the modern sense.

### MCQ Trap
> **MCQ Trap:** Do not assume written law automatically means equality before the law. Do not call the Athenian jury system a simple equivalent of a modern jury trial.
<!-- END WEEK 3 MIDTERM FRAME -->

### Study questions answered
1. What are the major terms for Greek laws?
   `nomos`, `thesmos`, `dike`, and `graphe` are among the most important distinctions.
2. What is meant by lethal self-help?
   Legally permitted private violence used to defend household order or respond to certain wrongs.
3. What are the main sources for Greek law in the pre-Classical period?
   Epic, inscriptions, later historians, and surviving statutes cited in speeches.
4. What are the main features of Greek law in the Archaic period?
   Increasing written law, lawgivers, kin-based retaliation still present, and formal limits on violence.
5. Who are the major reformers at Athens?
   Draco, Solon, and later Cleisthenes in political organization.
6. What are the main organizations of the Athenian system?
   Archons, Areopagus, boule, assembly, juries.
7. What is the procedural difference between private and public cases?
   Who brings the case, whose interest is represented, and sometimes how the case is framed.
8. How does a typical Greek jury trial proceed?
   Complaint, pretrial stages, speeches, witnesses, jury vote, and sometimes a separate penalty phase.

### Deep study layer
#### What this lecture is arguing
Week 3 argues that Greek law develops out of custom, arbitration, and feud management rather than out of a modern state monopoly on prosecution. The lecture repeatedly shows that the main legal problem is often not simply "a killing happened," but "how do we stop retaliation from spiraling?"

#### Concept bubbles
- `nomos`: what it does
  `Nomos` means law, but also custom. It reminds you that ancient Greek law is deeply tied to social norms and accepted practice, not just to statute books.
- `thesmos`: what it does
  Early written enactment. The term marks a move toward public, fixed, readable rules that constrain elite arbitrariness.
- `dike` and `graphe`: what they do
  `Dike` channels a wrong into a more private action connected to the injured party. `Graphe` treats the wrong as something broader, affecting the community. The distinction tells you who gets to act and what sort of interest the law recognizes.
- `Lethal self-help`: what it does
  It preserves family honor and practical enforcement in a world without modern policing, but it also threatens endless reciprocal violence. Greek homicide law therefore disciplines revenge rather than eliminating it.

#### Why this matters for crime and punishment
This lecture gives you the vocabulary for almost every Greek week that follows. Without it, you cannot explain why `Lysias 1` is about lawful killing rather than "murder with a weird defense," and you cannot explain why the `Oresteia` ends with the creation of a court.

#### Ancient vs modern comparison
A modern criminal trial usually assumes police investigation, public prosecution, legal professionals, and a judge who controls procedure. Athenian law assumes self-representation, citizen juries, strong rhetorical performance, and a much thinner professional legal class. The functional comparison is that both systems seek legitimacy through procedure, but the burden of producing the case falls on different people.

#### Exam trap
Do not collapse `dike` and `graphe`, and do not say that written law eliminates social inequality. The lecture explicitly notes that early written law can publicize rules while still encoding inequality.

## Week 4: Greek Tragedy of Crime - Aeschylus' Oresteia

### The Five Things
- Author: Aeschylus
- Title: `Oresteia`
- Date: 458 BCE
- Location: Athens
- Language: Greek

### What the trilogy is about
The `Oresteia` tracks a chain of violence inside the house of Atreus:
- Agamemnon sacrifices Iphigenia.
- Clytemnestra kills Agamemnon.
- Orestes kills Clytemnestra to avenge Agamemnon.
- The Furies pursue Orestes.
- Athena creates a court and transforms vengeance into civic adjudication.

### Why the trilogy matters for the course
- It dramatizes a world before stable civic justice.
- It presents violent revenge as both necessary and destructive.
- It shows the transition from blood vengeance to courtroom justice.
- It treats crime as moral, familial, civic, and divine at the same time.

### Major themes
- Hospitality
- Kinship violence
- Gender inversion and anxiety
- Divine justice versus human justice
- Pollution
- Founding of the court

### High-yield interpretation
The trilogy does not simply say "law replaces revenge." It stages a compromise:
- older forces of vengeance are not destroyed;
- they are incorporated into civic order as the Eumenides;
- justice becomes institutional, but only by accommodating older claims.

<!-- START WEEK 4 MIDTERM FRAME -->
### Lecture frame in plain English
The Oresteia week argues that tragedy is a public arena for thinking about justice, guilt, revenge, and institutional change. The second Oresteia topic argues that Athena's court is a political settlement that transforms vengeance into civic order without abolishing its emotional force.

The trilogy provides the course's clearest mythic model for the move from blood revenge to civic adjudication. It gives you a model for explaining how institutions manage violence instead of magically ending it. The trilogy is useful because it shows what the triumph-of-law story usually hides. Institutional justice often survives by channeling emotion rather than eliminating it.

### Key concepts in paragraph form
**Pollution.** A stain or contagion attached to grave wrongdoing, especially bloodshed. It makes homicide a problem for households, cities, and gods, not just for one victim. It explains why the trilogy treats violence as socially expansive.

**Furies.** Divine agents of kin-based vengeance. They keep old obligations to blood and family alive. The final settlement matters because Athens must incorporate them, not erase them.

**Eumenides.** The renamed Furies, now integrated into Athens. They preserve fear, sanction, and continuity between old and new justice. The civic court succeeds because it absorbs old powers into the city.

### MCQ Trap
> **MCQ Trap:** Do not say the play simply proves revenge is bad and courts are good; it is about compromise and incorporation. Do not reduce Athena's solution to a simple acquittal.
<!-- END WEEK 4 MIDTERM FRAME -->

### Study questions answered
1. How does the backstory set up revenge?
   The house of Atreus is already trapped in inherited violence before the trilogy begins.
2. How does Aeschylus describe life before a justice system?
   As a world where blood must answer blood and no stable neutral authority resolves conflict.
3. Does Clytemnestra fit expected gender norms?
   No. She is repeatedly coded as masculine, authoritative, strategic, and politically dangerous.
4. What role does hospitality play?
   Violations of household trust and guest relationships deepen the moral horror of the crimes.
5. Human versus divine justice?
   Human judgments are limited and political; divine claims are older, broader, and harder to escape.
6. How does Orestes resolve the conflict?
   He does not resolve it privately. The case is transferred into a court.
7. How does the trilogy reflect court development?
   Athena's institution of trial mirrors the move toward civic adjudication.
8. What role do divinities play?
   They embody competing justice systems.
9. Do punishments fit crimes?
   Not neatly; each punishment generates further guilt.
10. What do the Eumenides preserve?
   Order, fear, and the older logic of vengeance, now folded into civic life.

### Deep study layer
#### What this lecture is arguing
Week 4 argues that tragedy is not a break from legal history but another place where Athenians think publicly about violence, guilt, legitimacy, and institutional change. The `Oresteia` dramatizes the movement from household revenge to civic adjudication, but it refuses to pretend that the old logic disappears.

#### Concept bubbles
- `Pollution`: what it does
  Pollution marks serious wrongdoing as spiritually and socially contagious. It explains why homicide threatens more than the immediate victim.
- `Furies/Erinyes`: what they do
  They personify the older order of kin-based vengeance. Their function is to insist that blood crimes demand response and cannot simply be rationalized away.
- `Athena's court`: what it does
  The court creates a civic forum for deciding blood guilt, transferring enforcement from kin retaliation to the city. But it succeeds only by incorporating, not erasing, the old vengeance powers.

#### Why this matters for crime and punishment
The trilogy gives the course a master myth for state formation. Civic justice is shown as a political settlement with older ideas of honor, blood, family obligation, and divine anger. That is exactly the pattern you later see in Roman material, where new public courts never fully erase status politics and private influence.

#### Ancient vs modern comparison
Modern states often tell a self-congratulatory story in which courts replaced vengeance and made justice rational. The `Oresteia` is more honest. It shows that legal institutions do not abolish anger, fear, or memory. They redirect them. The closest modern parallel is not simple "rule of law" rhetoric, but modern debates over vigilantism, victim rights, restorative justice, and the emotional afterlife of violent crime.

#### Exam trap
Do not say Athena simply proves Orestes innocent in a factual sense. The play is about the creation of authority and legitimacy, not only about evidentiary truth.

## Week 6: Modern Criminology and Ancient Crime

### Why this week matters
Week 6 gives the theoretical toolkit the professor uses to read the ancient materials. You should know the theory names, but more importantly you should know how they are applied in later weeks.

### Five approaches to defining crime
- Legal: crime is law-breaking.
- Moral: crime is what is wrong.
- Social: crime is norm violation.
- Humanistic: crime is violation of fundamental human rights.
- Social constructionist: societies construct both crime and criminality.

### Positivism
- Biological positivism: Lombroso and the search for physical criminal types.
- Sociological positivism: environment, neighborhood, and measurable social forces.
- Psychological positivism: mental and internal causal models.

### Walklate's four explanation models
- Rational Choice Theory
- Social Control Theory
- Relative Deprivation Theory
- Hegemonic Masculinity

### Otherness
- Criminological other: a person who does not fit the expected image of a criminal.
- Victimological other: a person who does not fit the expected image of a victim.

### High-yield takeaways
- Ancient texts can look strikingly modern when they explain criminality through personality, desire, class frustration, or social environment.
- The course repeatedly asks whether ancient writers focus on the act or on the person.

<!-- START WEEK 6 MIDTERM FRAME -->
### Lecture frame in plain English
Week 6 uses modern criminology as an interpretive toolkit rather than as a template to be imposed mechanically on antiquity. The positivism topic explains why ancient writers can look modern when they explain wrongdoing through character, environment, desire, gender, or social frustration.

These definitions let you explain how ancient texts construct criminality beyond strict legal categories. It gives you a language for analyzing cause, stereotype, and social labeling in later weeks. The continuity lies in construction; the difference lies in style and authority. Both are explanatory projects, but they authorize themselves differently.

### Key concepts in paragraph form
**Social construction of crime.** The view that crime and criminality are made by institutions, norms, and power relations. It shifts attention from timeless essence to labeling and social meaning. It is one of the most useful models for reading Catiline, Clodia, and Euphiletos.

**Positivism.** A family of approaches looking for observable causes of criminal behavior. It organizes explanation around empirical or quasi-empirical causal models. It helps you compare ancient character narratives with modern causal theories.

**Criminological other.** A person who does not fit the expected image of offender or victim. It reveals how public stereotypes shape who seems believable or dangerous. This matters for Clodia, Caelius, Catiline, and many ancient defendants.

### MCQ Trap
> **MCQ Trap:** Do not treat the legal definition as the only definition that matters in the course. Do not call ancient authors criminologists in the disciplinary sense; the lecture is using analogy, not identity.
<!-- END WEEK 6 MIDTERM FRAME -->

### Study questions answered
1. How is criminology defined?
   As a flexible field studying crime, criminality, punishment, victims, and social responses.
2. What are the approaches to defining crime?
   Legal, moral, social, humanistic, social constructionist.
3. How did criminology develop?
   Through changing emphasis from law and punishment to scientific, social, and cultural explanation.
4. What is positivism?
   The search for empirical causes of criminal behavior.
5. What are the three positivist strands?
   Biological, sociological, psychological.
6. What are Walklate's four models?
   Rational choice, social control, relative deprivation, hegemonic masculinity.
7. What are the criminological and victimological others?
   People who do not fit expected public stereotypes of offender or victim.

### Deep study layer
#### What this lecture is arguing
Week 6 argues that modern criminology is not the subject of the course so much as the toolkit for reading the ancient material. The lecture does not claim that Greeks and Romans were criminologists in the modern disciplinary sense. It claims that they often ask the same big questions: what counts as crime, what causes it, who gets labeled criminal, and what punishment is for.

#### Concept bubbles
- `Social construction of crime`: what it does
  This idea insists that crime is produced through institutions, norms, and power rather than discovered as a timeless natural fact. It is one of the most useful tools in the whole course.
- `Positivism`: what it does
  Positivism looks for causes of criminality in observable traits, environments, and measurable patterns. It helps you see why ancient texts that explain wrongdoing through upbringing, desire, gender, class, and moral weakness can feel strangely modern.
- `Criminological or victimological other`: what it does
  This concept explains why some offenders or victims are believed immediately while others are read as unlikely, suspicious, or socially unintelligible. It is crucial for understanding Euphiletos, Catiline, Clodia, and Caelius.

#### Why this matters for crime and punishment
This lecture is what lets you move from description to analysis. Instead of saying only that Catiline is called corrupt, or that Clodia is represented as dangerous, you can ask what explanatory model is being used. Is the speaker blaming character, gender, status anxiety, social deprivation, rational calculation, or political theater?

#### Ancient vs modern comparison
Modern criminology often claims scientific neutrality, but it still depends on social fears and public narratives about who looks criminal. Ancient texts do the same thing more openly. They make criminality visible through stories, metaphors, genealogy, and moral language rather than through expert reports or statistics.

#### Exam trap
Do not write as if the legal definition of crime is the only one that matters in the course. The professor explicitly uses Week 6 to show why legal definitions are too narrow for the ancient material.

## Week 7: Athens and Draco's Homicide Law / Lysias 1

### The Five Things
- Author: Lysias
- Title: `On the Murder of Eratosthenes` (`Lysias 1`)
- Date: probably after 403 BCE, before Lysias' death
- Location: Athens
- Language: Greek

### Who is who
- Author: Lysias
- Speaker in court: Euphiletos
- Defendant: Euphiletos
- Dead man: Eratosthenes

### Why this case matters
This week shows how Athenian homicide law works in practice and how rhetoric bends law. Euphiletos must show that the killing was legal, not premeditated murder.

### The key legal issue
The defense relies on the exception in Draco's homicide law for killing a seducer caught in the act. The most important exam point is this:
- the law creates an exception to homicide liability;
- it does not simply say "adultery is punishable by death."

### Why "seducer" matters
The lecture stresses that seduction is treated as worse than rape because seduction threatens:
- household authority,
- legitimacy of children,
- inheritance,
- the integrity of the household.

### Euphiletos' strategy
- portray himself as ordinary, modest, gullible, and law-abiding;
- portray Eratosthenes as a serial corrupter of households;
- claim the killing was lawful revenge, not murder;
- imply he acted as the law commanded, even though the law is more limited than that.

### Direct passages from the reading

From `Lysias 1.pdf`, section 4:

> "I believe, gentlemen, that what I have to demonstrate is this: that Eratosthenes seduced my wife and corrupted her ... and that I did not commit this deed for money ... except revenge, as the law allows."

Why it matters:
- This is the entire defense theory in miniature.
- Euphiletos reframes the case from homicide to justified household revenge.
- The phrase "as the law allows" is doing major rhetorical work.

From `Lysias 1.pdf`, section 21:

> "Make sure, then, no one else finds out about this; otherwise, our agreement will be worth nothing. I expect you to show me them in the act. For I do not need words, but clear evidence..."

Why it matters:
- Euphiletos tries to prove that he waited for lawful, direct confirmation.
- It supports his claim that he was not acting out of long-term personal hatred.
- It also reveals calculated preparation, which is exactly why the speech needs to manage the line between plan and lawful response.

From `Lysias 1.pdf`, opening:

> "If you employed the same standards for others as you do for your own behaviour, there is not a single one of you who would not be furious..."

Why it matters:
- Euphiletos invites the jury to identify with him personally.
- The case is not presented as technical law alone, but as shared masculine household experience.

Lysias 1 turns a homicide prosecution into a contest over whether the dead man, not the defendant, was the real criminal threat. The rhetoric topic shows how Lysias builds Euphiletos as an ordinary, credible man while recasting Eratosthenes as the recognizable type of a dangerous seducer.

## Week 8: Greek Philosophy of Crime - Plato's Gorgias

### The Five Things
- Author: Plato
- Title: `Gorgias`
- Date: fourth century BCE, set in the late fifth century
- Location: Athens
- Language: Greek

### Main figures
- Socrates
- Gorgias
- Polus
- Callicles

### Why rhetoric matters
Rhetoric is the art of persuasion. In Athens, persuasive speech could shape assemblies and trials. The dialogue asks whether persuasion without truth is power, corruption, or both.

### Socrates versus Polus
Polus argues that a successful criminal can be happy if he avoids punishment.
Socrates argues:
- wrongdoing makes the soul worse;
- escaping punishment is worse than being punished;
- punishment is a kind of cure.

This is one of the clearest ancient arguments for rehabilitation over simple deterrence.

### Socrates versus Callicles
Callicles argues:
- `physis` (nature) and `nomos` (convention/law) are opposed;
- the strong should dominate the weak;
- justice is a tool invented by the weak majority;
- self-indulgence is natural.

Socrates argues:
- justice and self-discipline are real goods;
- unrestrained desire produces disorder;
- the predatory outlaw cannot live in stable community.

### High-yield takeaways
- Gorgias is not a law lecture; it is a lecture on competing definitions of crime and justice.
- Socrates takes a moral and almost religious view of criminality.
- Callicles pushes a radical social-constructionist and anti-moral line.

<!-- START WEEK 8 MIDTERM FRAME -->
### Lecture frame in plain English
The rhetoric topic argues that persuasive speech is one of the central mechanisms through which law, politics, and public truth are shaped. The punishment topic stages three rival ways of thinking about wrongdoing: success without punishment, punishment as cure, and power beyond conventional justice.

It gives you the philosophical backdrop for understanding both Lysias and Cicero. It lets you map ancient debate onto modern questions about deterrence, rehabilitation, morality, and social construction. The lecture treats rhetoric as a live modern problem, not an antique curiosity. The ancient argument stays useful because it is about purpose, not just policy.

### Key concepts in paragraph form
**Rhetoric.** The art or technique of persuasive speech. It moves juries, assemblies, and publics toward belief and action. In Athens, rhetorical skill is a route to power and a danger to justice.

**Sophist.** A teacher of persuasive technique and argument. The sophist professionalizes persuasion as a teachable civic skill. He becomes a symbol of both democratic opportunity and manipulative danger.

**Punishment as cure.** Socrates' claim that punishment improves the diseased soul of the wrongdoer. It redefines punishment as moral repair rather than only pain or deterrence. This is one of the clearest ancient arguments for a rehabilitative model.

> **MCQ Trap:** Do not treat rhetoric as decoration; for Plato and the course it is a technology of power. Do not reduce Socrates, Polus, and Callicles to one-word modern theories and stop there.

   See above.

Week 8 argues that rhetoric is not secondary to crime and punishment. It is one of the places where crime and punishment are made intelligible. Plato's `Gorgias` is therefore part legal theory, part political theory, and part critique of persuasive power.

#### Concept bubbles
- `Rhetoric`: what it does
  Rhetoric produces belief, moves audiences, and can detach persuasion from truth. In a city where citizens judge cases and vote in assemblies, rhetoric is a practical technology of power.
- `Sophist`: what it does
  The sophist represents professionalized persuasion. He offers the skill of winning arguments, which makes him useful, profitable, and politically dangerous.
- `Punishment as cure`: what it does
  Socrates' claim that punishment is good for the wrongdoer reimagines criminal justice as moral medicine rather than mere retaliation or deterrence.

The whole second half of the course keeps returning to this problem: is persuasion helping a community find justice, or is it simply helping the clever dominate the less powerful? Once you see the `Gorgias` clearly, you can see Lysias and Cicero as case studies in exactly the danger Plato is worried about.

#### Ancient vs modern comparison
The lecture explicitly compares rhetoric to advertising, social-media persuasion, targeted messaging, and "fake news." The modern point is not that sophists were influencers in togas. It is that democratic and mass-media societies remain vulnerable to polished persuasion that can outstrip truth, evidence, or justice.

#### Exam trap
Do not reduce Socrates, Polus, and Callicles to three neat modern theories and stop there. The point is the clash between rival visions of power, happiness, and punishment, not a one-to-one labeling exercise.

## Week 10: Introduction to Roman Law

### Why this is a very high-yield lecture
Week 10 is one of the most term-heavy lectures in the course. It covers Roman legal history, magistrates, assemblies, jurists, codification, criminal procedure, and the growth of state authority.

### Origin story of Roman law
- Rome begins, in legend, in 753 BCE.
- Early Rome is governed by kings.
- Justice in the regal period is largely custom-based.
- Priests initially control written law.
- Family law and religious regulation are early formal areas.
- Patron-client relations act as a parallel social structure for dispute resolution.

### Core early developments
- `imperium`: power to command
- `intercessio`: veto or blocking power
- Struggle of the Orders
- tribunes of the plebs
- Twelve Tables, 450 BCE
- `provocatio ad populum`: citizen appeal from magistrate to people

### Magistrates and offices you must know
- consuls
- praetors
- `praetor urbanus`
- `praetor peregrinus`
- tribunes of the plebs
- quaestors
- aediles
- censors
- dictator
- prorogation, proconsuls, propraetors

### Praetors and legal development
- Praetors are central to the justice system.
- They issue edicts when entering office.
- Law developed from the edicts is called `ius honorarium`.
- This is different from more formal law made by assemblies.

### Roman lawyers and jurists
- Early legal specialists are the `pontifices`.
- Roman legal work includes:
  - `ad respondendum` - giving legal advice
  - `ad agendum` - preparing cases
  - `ad cavendum` - drafting documents
- Under Augustus and later emperors, some jurists receive `ius respondendi ex auctoritate principis`.

### Jurists and schools you should know
- Marcus Antistius Labeo
- Proculian school
- Masurius Sabinus
- Sabinian school
- Gaius
- Papinian
- Ulpian
- Paulus
- Modestinus

### Codification stages
- Law of Citations, 426 CE
- `Codex Theodosianus`, 439 CE
- Justinian's `Corpus Iuris Civilis`, from 528 CE
  - `Codex Iustiniani`
  - `Novellae`
  - `Institutiones`
  - `Digest`

### Republican constitution and assemblies
- SPQR = Senate and People of Rome.
- Senate: advisory, but hugely authoritative through `senatus consulta`.
- Centuriate assembly:
  - wealth-based;
  - favors the rich;
  - elects consuls, praetors, censors;
  - votes on war;
  - hears appeals in capital cases.
- Tribal assembly:
  - tribe/residence based;
  - more egalitarian;
  - elects quaestors and curule aediles;
  - votes on `leges`.
- Plebeian assembly:
  - plebeians only;
  - elects tribunes and plebeian aediles;
  - votes on `plebiscita`;
  - after `lex Hortensia`, plebiscites bind all Rome.

### Criminal procedure development
- Early criminal action comes from magistrates with `imperium`.
- Most cases are still private for a long time.
- `quaestio`: ad hoc criminal inquiry/court.
- `quaestio perpetua`: permanent court, beginning with `lex Calpurnia` in 149 BCE.
- Key permanent courts include:
  - `repetundae`
  - `maiestas`
  - `veneficia et sicarii`
  - `ambitus`
  - `vis`

### Why `lex Cornelia de sicariis et veneficis` matters
- 81 BCE
- Establishes a permanent court for murder and poisoning.
- Also criminalizes carrying weapons with intent to kill or steal.
- This is one place where you can argue that murder becomes criminal in Rome in a stronger sense than earlier.

### Later empire and class
- `cognitio extraordinaria` eventually takes over much judicial work.
- Urban prefects and other prefects extend enforcement.
- `honestiores` receive better treatment than `humiliores`.
- Augustus adds moral legislation, including adultery courts.

### Delicts
- Not quite crimes, not quite torts.
- Core delicts:
  - `furtum`
  - `rapina`
  - `damnum iniuria datum`
  - `iniuria`

### Lecture frame in plain English
The origins topic argues that Roman law emerges through legend, custom, priestly control, patronage, and political struggle rather than appearing as a ready-made code. The magistrates topic argues that Roman law works through offices, assemblies, and checks on power rather than through a simple separation of branches. The jurists topic argues that Roman law becomes increasingly shaped by expert interpretation, teaching, and codification. The criminal-procedure topic argues that Rome slowly develops more regular public courts for recurring political and social dangers.

It shows that Roman legal history is inseparable from Roman constitutional history. You need these institutions to understand how legislation, appeal, and legal administration operate in the Republic. It explains why Roman law has such a long later legacy even after the political world that produced it has changed. It is the bridge between general Roman legal history and the specific Roman crime material in Weeks 11 and 12. Roman law is foundational, but not because it already looks modern in every respect. Roman law must be explained as a political process, not just a doctrinal one. Roman law's legacy lies heavily in reasoning, classification, and textual authority. The Roman development matters because it is partial, contested, and status-laden.

### Key concepts in paragraph form
**Patron-client system.** A hierarchy in which patrons protect and assist dependents or clients. It channels disputes, protection, and influence through social hierarchy. It modifies self-help and weak state enforcement instead of eliminating them.

**Twelve Tables.** The early public posting of Roman law in the fifth century BCE. They reduce priestly secrecy and make law more publicly knowable. They are a landmark in access and publicity, not the final form of Roman law.

**imperium.** The power to command held by certain Roman magistrates. It authorizes coercion, command, and political action. It explains why legal administration is bound to office-holding.

**intercessio.** The power to block or forbid another magistrate's action. It checks concentration of power inside a collegial magistracy. It is one of Rome's key anti-monarchical mechanisms.

**provocatio.** Citizen appeal from coercive magistrate action to the people. It limits state coercion against citizens. It becomes crucial background when emergency execution without trial is later debated.

**ius honorarium.** Law and remedies developed through magistrates, especially praetors, in office. It adapts rigid older law to new disputes and social realities. It shows Roman law's flexibility and practical problem-solving.

**Codification.** The collection and ordering of legal materials into more authoritative bodies of text. It stabilizes and transmits legal knowledge across time. This is why the later Roman legal tradition matters far beyond the Republic.

**quaestio perpetua.** A permanent criminal court for a recurring category of wrong. It regularizes prosecution and classification for repeated public harms. It marks Rome's movement toward a more recognizable criminal-law structure.

**vis.** Unauthorized force or violence treated as a public danger. It shifts violence from a private quarrel frame toward a state-protection frame. It becomes crucial for the Catiline and Caelius material.

Do not call the praetor a straightforward modern judge or the Senate a parliament. Do not reduce codification to memorizing dates; the point is the growth of legal authority through texts. Do not assume all serious Roman violence was always handled as a modern public crime.

### Study questions answered
1. **What are the main features of the origin story of Roman law?** Roman law begins as a layered story rather than as a finished code. The lecture starts with kings, custom, priestly control, household authority, and patronage, then shows how plebeian struggle, publicity, magistracies, and writing slowly make law more visible and more civic.

2. **Why are the Twelve Tables important?** The Twelve Tables matter because they reduce priestly secrecy and make law publicly knowable. The lecture treats them as a landmark in access and publicity, not as a complete rational code that solved Roman legal development once and for all.

3. **Which officials are mainly responsible for legal affairs?** In Republican Rome the praetors matter most for day-to-day legal administration, especially the praetor urbanus and praetor peregrinus. That answer should still be placed inside a wider constitutional setting that includes magistrates, assemblies, jurists, and later imperial authority.

4. **What are the main sources of law?** The lecture wants you to think historically here. Early Rome emphasizes custom, priestly expertise, and the Twelve Tables; the Republic adds leges, plebiscita, praetorian edicts, and senatorial influence; the Empire gives growing weight to jurists and imperial constitutions.

5. **How did legal education develop?** Roman legal expertise begins in the hands of the pontifices, then becomes a prestigious juristic culture in which elite experts teach, interpret, and classify law. By late antiquity, legal knowledge is increasingly textual, school-based, and tied to state-backed authority.

6. **What are the main stages in codification?** The course emphasizes three big codifying moments: the Law of Citations in 426 CE, the Codex Theodosianus in 439 CE, and Justinian's Corpus Iuris Civilis from 528 onward. The key point is not just memorizing dates but seeing the long shift from scattered expertise toward curated bodies of authoritative text.

7. **How did legislation work?** Roman legislation is political before it is purely doctrinal. Magistrates propose, assemblies vote, the Senate advises with enormous practical authority, and later emperors dominate the process more directly.

8. **How did criminal law and trials develop?** The lecture presents Roman criminal law as gradual and uneven: magistrate coercion comes first, ad hoc quaestiones follow, and permanent courts eventually appear for recurring public wrongs such as extortion, treason, bribery, and violence. That development matters because it marks Rome's slow movement toward stronger public control over harms that were earlier treated more privately or more politically.

9. **What role did the emperor play?** The emperor becomes increasingly central as law develops: he appoints officials, shapes appeals, strengthens juristic authority, and ultimately stands behind late codification. A strong answer explains that this is part of the wider centralization of interpretive and coercive power.

### Deep study layer
#### What this lecture is arguing
Week 10 argues that Roman law is really a history of institutions, political struggle, and state-building. The lecture does not present Roman law as a single finished code. It presents it as a long process in which custom, priestly knowledge, aristocratic office, plebeian agitation, praetorian innovation, juristic expertise, and imperial authority gradually produce something that later Europe will treat as a foundational legal tradition.

#### Concept bubbles
- `Patrons and clients`: what it does
  Patronage gives Romans a semi-private mechanism for protection, representation, and pressure. It modifies self-help by embedding it inside hierarchy rather than replacing it with a neutral state.
- `imperium`: what it does
  `Imperium` is command power. It lets magistrates act, enforce, and govern, and it explains why Roman law is inseparable from Roman office-holding.
- `intercessio`: what it does
  This is the power to block a colleague's or magistrate's action. It acts as an anti-monarchical check inside Republican government.
- `provocatio`: what it does
  Citizen appeal to the people limits magistrates' coercive power and becomes a key background principle whenever emergency punishment of citizens is debated.
- `quaestio perpetua`: what it does
  A permanent criminal court turns repeated political or public harms into recognizable categories for adjudication. It is one of the main ways Rome moves toward a more regular criminal-law system.
- `ius honorarium`: what it does
  Praetorian law adapts rigid old rules to new situations. It shows Roman law's practicality and institutional flexibility.
- `vis`: what it does
  The category of `vis` treats certain unauthorized force not just as a private injury but as a threat to public order and the commonwealth.

#### Why this matters for crime and punishment
Week 10 is the hinge between Greek and Roman material. It explains why later Roman cases are obsessed with state authority, procedure, status, and public order. Without this week, Catiline and Caelius become isolated stories instead of examples of a legal system deciding what counts as public danger.

#### Key chronology to remember as a story
- Regal Rome: custom, kings, priestly control, household and religious regulation.
- Early Republic: collegial magistrates, `imperium`, `intercessio`, plebeian resistance.
- Fifth century BCE: Twelve Tables publicize law and reduce priestly secrecy.
- Middle Republic: praetors and assemblies structure legal life more visibly.
- Late Republic: permanent courts make repeated public wrongs more classifiable and more political.
- Empire and late antiquity: jurists, emperors, `cognitio extraordinaria`, and codification centralize interpretive authority.

#### Ancient vs modern comparison
Roman law is often called the ancestor of modern law, but that statement hides a lot. It is truest for private law and legal reasoning, not for a modern police-centered criminal state. Rome shares with modern systems an increasing desire to classify harms, formalize procedure, and centralize authority; it differs in its dependence on status, patronage, office-holding, and weak separation between law and politics.

- Do not call the Senate a legislature in a modern parliamentary sense.
- Do not treat the Twelve Tables as a complete rational code; the lecture presents them as a landmark in publicity and access, not as the end of development.
- A likely comparison question is how Rome moves from regulating violence to monopolizing violence. Use `quaestiones`, `vis`, and later imperial procedure in your answer.

## Week 11: Criminals and Enemy Combatants - Sallust's Catiline

### The Five Things
- Author: Sallust
- Title: `Catiline's War` (`Bellum Catilinae`)
- Date: c. 44-40 BCE
- Location: Rome
- Language: Latin

### Why this week matters
This is the course's big Roman case study in how rhetoric, political crisis, and criminality overlap. The central question is whether Catiline is a criminal conspirator or an enemy combatant in a civil war.

### Background to the conspiracy
- Catiline is a noble but indebted aristocrat.
- Roman politics is split between `optimates` and `populares`.
- Debt crisis matters.
- Sulla's civil wars and veteran settlements leave instability in Italy, especially Etruria.
- Gaius Manlius becomes crucial in Etruria.

### Core chronology of 63-62 BCE
- 64 BCE: Catiline loses bid for the consulship of 63.
- 63 BCE: Catiline loses again for the consulship of 62.
- July 63 BCE: failed election deepens his crisis.
- October 20: anonymous letters reveal conspiracy.
- October 21: Senate passes the `senatus consultum ultimum`.
- October 27: Manlius raises revolt in Etruria.
- early November: Catiline charged with `vis`.
- November 7: attempted assassination of Cicero fails.
- November 8: Cicero delivers the First Catilinarian.
- mid-November: Catiline declared `hostis`.
- December 3: Allobroges produce evidence; conspirators arrested.
- December 5: Senate advises execution of captured conspirators without trial.
- January 62 BCE: Catiline dies in battle.

### Why the legal issue is so important
The captured conspirators were Roman citizens. They should still have had `provocatio`. Cicero executes them without trial under cover of the `SCU`, and that choice later contributes to his exile.

### Criminological reading of Sallust
Sallust does not present Catiline simply as a villain. He mixes:
- psychological explanation,
- moral corruption,
- class resentment,
- elite decadence,
- relative deprivation,
- rational calculation,
- charisma and heroism.

- Catiline is not a lone deviant.

- This supports a relative-deprivation reading of the conspiracy.

Why it matters:
- Cato shifts the frame from prosecution to war.
- He justifies extreme punishment as preemption and deterrence.
- This is the decisive move from "criminal conspiracy" to "enemy threat."

### Catiline's contradictory image
Sallust makes Catiline:
- depraved but charismatic,

### Lecture frame in plain English
The chronology topic shows that the Catilinarian crisis unfolds as a rapidly escalating sequence of elections, plots, emergency decrees, arrests, executions, and battle. The debate topic argues that Caesar and Cato are really debating whether the captured conspirators remain punishable citizens or have become war enemies.

### Key concepts in paragraph form

### Study questions answered
1. **What are the Five Things?** For the exam, the basic frame is Sallust, Catiline's War, written around 44-40 BCE, set in Rome, and written in Latin. The point of memorizing the Five Things is to anchor the reading in late Republican political crisis rather than treating it as free-floating moral literature.

2. **What social issues lead Catiline toward revolution?** The lecture highlights debt, blocked aristocratic ambition, veteran unrest, and the aftereffects of civil conflict. Sallust therefore presents conspiracy not only as personal wickedness but also as something that feeds on structural grievance and social instability.

3. **What is happening in Etruria?** Etruria is the crucial armed base of the crisis, especially under Manlius. On the exam, treat it as the place where political conspiracy starts to become something closer to open revolt or civil war.

4. **Who is Catiline?** Catiline is an indebted noble, a failed candidate, a charismatic political actor, and an unstable criminal category all at once. Sallust keeps him morally corrupt, socially intelligible, and even heroic in battle, which is why the lecture insists that he cannot be reduced to a one-word label.

5. **Who is Cicero?** Cicero is consul, orator, and the self-fashioned savior of the Roman state. He is also the figure whose later vulnerability shows how dangerous emergency punishment without trial could be, even when it was done in the name of public safety.

6. **How does Sallust explain Catiline's criminality?** Sallust mixes moral corruption, ambition, desire, elite decadence, social crisis, and political grievance. That mixture is the point: Catiline is explained at once through character and through the diseased condition of the Republic.

7. **How does Catiline explain his own turn to crime?** In the lecture's terms, Catiline sounds like a figure of grievance, exclusion, and frustrated entitlement. That makes him useful for comparison with relative deprivation and rational-choice style explanations, even though Sallust never stops framing him as dangerous.

8. **How do Caesar and Cato relate to crime and punishment?** Caesar resists execution without trial and warns about precedent, but he still imagines harsh detention and control. Cato shifts the frame toward deterrence, war, and political signaling, which is why the lecture treats their disagreement as a debate over whether the captives are still citizens or already enemies.

9. **What is the outcome and lasting issue?** The conspiracy is crushed and Catiline dies in battle, but the legal problem remains unresolved. The lasting issue is when a state may stop treating insiders as citizens subject to procedure and begin treating them as enemies who can be neutralized exceptionally.

#### What this lecture is arguing
Week 11 argues that the Catilinarian crisis is not only a story about one conspiracy. It is a case study in what happens when a state cannot decide whether to treat dangerous insiders as citizens, criminals, rebels, or enemies. The lecture frames the whole week through modern terrorism discourse in order to show how emergency politics pressures law.

#### Concept bubbles
- `SCU` (`senatus consultum ultimum`): what it does
  The `SCU` is not a formal suspension of law, but it authorizes extreme emergency action in the name of saving the state. It creates precisely the ambiguity that later makes Cicero vulnerable.
- `hostis`: what it does
  Calling someone an enemy pushes him out of the normal civic frame. It does rhetorical work as much as legal work, because it makes extraordinary violence feel necessary.
- `Relative deprivation`: what it does

- `Enemy combatant`: what it does

This is one of the course's clearest tests of the public/private distinction. If captured conspirators are treated as criminals, procedure and citizen rights matter. ## Week 12: Character and Ciceronian Crime - Pro Caelio

### The Five Things
- Author: Marcus Tullius Cicero
- Title: `In Defense of Marcus Caelius` (`Pro Caelio`)
- Date: 56 BCE
- Location: Rome
- Language: Latin

### Background to the case
- Caelius is charged with `vis`, political violence.
- Prosecutors: Atratinus, Publius Clodius, and Herennius Balbus.
- Defense: Caelius, Crassus, and Cicero.
- The wider background involves Egyptian politics, Ptolemy XII, the Alexandrian embassy, and the death of Dio.
- Clodia, Caelius' former lover, becomes central to Cicero's version of events.

### Why the charge matters
`Vis` is supposed to cover dangerous violence against the state, not private scandal. Cicero's whole strategy is to make the jury think the charge is absurdly overblown.

### Cicero's main strategy
He does not focus primarily on reconstructing facts. He:
- turns the trial into comedy;
- turns Caelius into a charming young hero;
- turns Clodia into the scandalous manipulator behind the prosecution;
- makes the case about character instead of action.

### Direct passages from the reading

From `Cicero In Defense of Marcus Caelius.pdf`, opening section:

> "If somebody were here who knew nothing about our laws, our courts, and our customs, he would certainly wonder what crime could be so vicious ... this one court should be in session."

Why it matters:
- Cicero opens by exaggerating the seriousness of the legal frame.
- He then undercuts it by suggesting the charge of `vis` is wildly disproportionate to the real case.
- He prepares the jury to see the prosecution as theatrical overreach.

From `Cicero In Defense of Marcus Caelius.pdf`, on the charges:

> "There are two charges. One involves gold, the other poison; in both of them one and the same person is concerned."

Why it matters:
- Cicero narrows the case to Clodia-centered allegations.
- He tries to strip away the public-crime frame and recast the prosecution as a private vendetta.

From `Cicero In Defense of Marcus Caelius.pdf`, on Clodia's narrative:

> "This whole tale, for instance, belongs to an aging lady poet who has written a lot of comedies - but it has no plot and no ending."

Why it matters:
- Cicero accuses the prosecution of theatrical fabrication while doing theatrical fabrication himself.
- This is a perfect example of rhetorical projection.

From `Cicero In Defense of Marcus Caelius.pdf`, section 70:

> "Members of the jury, my case is said and done ... This is the law under which this young man Caelius is being prosecuted not to exact punishment on behalf of the commonwealth but on behalf of the sexual perversions of this woman."

Why it matters:
- Cicero returns to the seriousness of the `vis` law.
- He argues that the state is being misused for private erotic revenge.
- He fuses legal argument, misogyny, and comic performance.

From the Week 12 lecture script, quoting Cicero's description of Catiline:

> "he could alter his character and direct it for the occasion, twisting and bending it in all directions"

Why it matters:
- Cicero uses Catiline's adaptability to excuse Caelius' youthful association with him.
- This shows how flexible courtroom rhetoric can be: last week's monster becomes this week's useful comparison.

### What the lecture wants you to notice
- Cicero makes a public-crime case look like sex comedy.
- He uses family, class, sexuality, and gender performance to assign criminality.
- He relies on what looks very much like positivist thinking: good families produce good citizens, corrupt households produce corrupt actors.
- The case also reveals a larger Roman shift toward treating unauthorized violence as violence against the state.

### Riggsby's contribution
The lecture uses Andrew Riggsby to argue:
- Cicero replaces a judicial frame with a theatrical one.
- Metaphor changes what outcomes feel "natural."
- The law on `vis` helps show the Roman state's growing monopoly on legitimate force.

### Lecture frame in plain English
The background topic shows that the Caelius trial is entangled with Egyptian diplomacy, elite rivalry, and the afterlife of the Catilinarian moment. The strategy topic argues that Cicero wins by theatrical substitution: he moves the case from state-danger narrative to comic-performance narrative. The criminality topic argues that Pro Caelio is about character, family, sexuality, and state control of violence at least as much as it is about factual guilt.

The charge does not make sense if you strip it out of late Republican politics and the Dio affair. It is the clearest Roman demonstration that genre and persona can do legal work. It brings together rhetoric, public/private wrong, gendered blame, and the Roman move toward stronger public claims over force. The public-private tension is part of the case, not background noise. The techniques change, but the struggle over interpretive frame remains familiar. The difference is partly one of overtness, not the disappearance of character-based reasoning.

### Key concepts in paragraph form
**vis.** A public-law category of force or violence threatening civic order. It lets Rome frame certain unauthorized actions as attacks on the commonwealth. Cicero's defense depends on making this category look absurdly misapplied.

**Megalensia.** The festival setting during which public games and dramatic performances were happening. It gives Cicero a stage-like frame for turning the courtroom into a theater. The holiday setting is part of the speech's persuasive strategy, not a random detail.

**Theatrical substitution.** Replacing one interpretive frame with another more emotionally persuasive one. It makes jurors feel that one narrative genre fits the case more naturally than another. Cicero uses it to turn a public-law case into a comedy of youthful indiscretion and malicious plotting.

**Character evidence.** Reasoning from the kind of person someone is said to be rather than only from the specific act alleged. It makes social identity do evidentiary work. Cicero's portrait of Caelius and Clodia drives the entire defense.

**State monopoly on violence.** The gradual claim that legitimate force belongs increasingly to public authority. It turns private violence into a problem for the commonwealth. The law on vis is one of the clearest Roman markers of that shift.

### Study questions answered
1. **What are the Five Things?** The exam frame is Cicero, Pro Caelio, delivered in 56 BCE at Rome in Latin. Those facts matter because the speech belongs to the late Republic, to a law court on vis, and to a political culture in which rhetoric, festival atmosphere, and elite scandal all overlap.

2. **What are the background issues of the trial?** The lecture places the case inside Egyptian diplomacy, the Alexandrian embassy, the death of Dio, and the aftershocks of late Republican instability. A good answer makes clear that the trial is not merely a private quarrel but a public prosecution entangled with politics and foreign affairs.

3. **Who is on the prosecution team?** Atratinus, Publius Clodius, and Herennius Balbus form the prosecution side. The important point is not only the names but the fact that Cicero turns their case into something that looks dependent on Clodia's scandalized narrative.

4. **Who is on the defense team?** Caelius, Crassus, and Cicero appear for the defense, with Cicero serving as the climactic speaker. On the exam, connect that role to performance: Cicero is not simply arguing doctrine but controlling the whole emotional frame of the trial.

5. **Who is Cicero in this trial?** He is the final defense speaker and the architect of the speech's theatrical strategy. The lecture treats him as someone who wins not mainly by technical refutation but by making one story feel comic, natural, and socially legible while making the rival story feel ridiculous.

6. **How does Cicero characterize Caelius?** Cicero presents Caelius as talented, socially acceptable, and guilty only of ordinary youthful excess. 8. **What is the relationship between Caelius and Catiline?** Caelius had connections to Catiline's circle, and that association is dangerous after the events of Week 11. 9. **What is Cicero's legal strategy and what theory does it resemble?** His strategy is to replace a strict public-law frame with character, genre, ridicule, and theatrical substitution. That resembles positivist or stereotype-based thinking because it asks the jury to decide what kind of people Caelius and Clodia are before deciding what happened.

10. **How are elements of this trial still visible today?** The lecture points to media spectacle, celebrity scandal, sexual double standards, character assassination, and metaphor-driven politics. The strongest comparison is that modern trials still compete in public culture through branding, persona, and story, even when formal doctrine pretends to be more controlled.
